"Get an LLC" is the most repeated NIL advice online. Here's what an LLC actually is — so you can tell if it applies to you.

An LLC is a legal container. It separates your business activity from your personal life: its own name, its own bank account, contracts signed by the business, and a layer of separation around personal assets.

What an LLC doesn't automatically do: lower your taxes. A single-member LLC is usually taxed exactly like no LLC at all.

It also isn't free — there are filing costs, annual requirements, and paperwork.

Worth it? Often, for athletes with real deal flow. Unnecessary, for a one-off payment. An attorney and tax professional can read your situation.

Few pieces of NIL advice travel faster than "form an LLC." The structure is genuinely useful — and genuinely misunderstood.

What it is: a limited liability company is a legal entity that holds your business activity. Deals are signed by the company, income flows to its bank account, and your business dealings gain a layer of separation from your personal assets. For an athlete running steady NIL activity, that separation — plus the cleaner contracts and records — is the real value.

What it is not: a tax strategy by default. A single-member LLC is typically "disregarded" for tax purposes, meaning the income lands on your return just as it would have without the entity. Whether any tax-oriented election ever makes sense is a fact-specific question for a professional.

It also carries real costs: formation fees, annual filings, and the discipline of actually keeping business and personal separate — an LLC you ignore protects no one.

The honest answer to "do I need one?" is: it depends on your deal volume, your risk, and your state. That's a one-hour conversation with an attorney and a tax professional, not a comment section.
