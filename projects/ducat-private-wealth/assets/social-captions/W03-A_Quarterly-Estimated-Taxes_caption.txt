The U.S. tax system has a rule that surprises almost every new NIL earner: it's pay-as-you-go.

When you earn money with no withholding — like most NIL income — the IRS doesn't want to wait until April. It generally expects payments through the year, on a quarterly schedule, four times a year.

Skip them, and you can owe not just the tax but an underpayment penalty on top.

The working system: a separate tax account that receives a share of every payment, plus the four due dates on your calendar, plus a tax professional who sizes the payments for your situation. Boring, effective, and much cheaper than the alternative.

Most people meet the tax system once a year. Self-employed earners — including most NIL athletes — meet it quarterly.

The mechanism: U.S. tax is pay-as-you-go. Employees satisfy this automatically through paycheck withholding. When income arrives with nothing withheld, the IRS generally expects estimated payments across the year, on a four-date schedule, rather than one settling-up in April. Underpaying along the way can add a penalty on top of the tax itself.

For an athlete family, this converts into three practical pieces. A separate account that receives a fixed share of every NIL payment on arrival. The quarterly due dates, on a calendar someone owns. And a tax professional who calculates what those payments should be — because the right amount depends on total income, state, and circumstances.

It's unglamorous, and it's also the single system that prevents the most common first-year NIL story: a good year of deals followed by a very bad spring.
