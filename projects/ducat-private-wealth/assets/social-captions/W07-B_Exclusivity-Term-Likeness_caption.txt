Three contract clauses decide what an NIL deal really costs you — beyond the work itself:

Exclusivity: which brands you can't work with while the deal runs. The wider the category ("all beverages" vs. one product), the more future money it blocks.

Term: how long it lasts. Watch for auto-renewals and options that extend it without a new signature.

Likeness rights: how long — and where — they can keep using your image. "In perpetuity" means forever. That word deserves a lawyer.

The pattern: narrow categories, defined periods, and rights that end when the deal ends. Anything broader should cost extra — because it's worth extra.

The price of an NIL deal isn't only the work you deliver. It's also what the fine print takes off the table.

Exclusivity defines who you can't work with during the term. Its cost depends entirely on how the category is written: "this energy drink" blocks one competitor; "all beverages" can block a dozen better offers. Category width is negotiable — treat it that way.

Term defines duration, and the quiet danger is automatic renewal: language that extends the deal unless someone remembers to object in a specific window. Calendar those windows, or strike the clause.

Likeness rights define how long, and where, the brand may keep using your name and image — sometimes well after the relationship ends. "In perpetuity, in all media" is a permanent grant of something you can never take back. Sometimes that's acceptable; it should never be accidental.

The professional pattern is consistent: narrow exclusivity, defined terms, and usage rights that expire with the deal. Broader grants aren't necessarily wrong — they're simply worth more, and should be priced and reviewed accordingly. Not legal advice: put real agreements in front of a qualified attorney before signing.
