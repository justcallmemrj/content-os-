"Name, image, and likeness" is a legal idea before it's a paycheck: you have the right to be paid when your identity promotes something.

That's what NIL compensation is — money for the use of who you are. A sponsored post, an autograph signing, a local ad, a camp you host: each one is a use of your name, image, or likeness, with a price attached.

What NIL is not: a salary from your school, part of your scholarship, or free money. It's business income, with contracts and taxes attached.

Which is why the smartest first move isn't chasing deals — it's understanding exactly what you're selling. Rules differ by state, school, and level, so your school's compliance office is always step one.

Before an athlete can manage NIL money well, it helps to be precise about what NIL is.

Name, image, and likeness compensation is payment for the commercial use of your identity — your name on a product, your face in a promotion, your presence at an event, your content on a feed. Legally, it works less like a paycheck and more like running a small business: you're licensing something you own.

That framing carries practical consequences. NIL income is generally taxable business income. Deals come with contracts that define what you're granting, to whom, and for how long. And the eligibility rules — what's permitted, what must be disclosed, who may be involved — vary by state, school, and level, and continue to change.

For families, the most useful early step isn't evaluating offers. It's building vocabulary: what's being sold, what's being signed, and who to ask. Your school's compliance office is the first stop before any deal.
