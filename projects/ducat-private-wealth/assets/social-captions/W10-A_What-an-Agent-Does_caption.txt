"I have an agent" is not the same as "I have a financial team." Knowing the difference protects you.

What an agent typically does: finds and negotiates deals, markets you to brands, manages the commercial side of your career — and earns a percentage of what they bring in.

What an agent typically is not: your tax professional, your investment advisor, your estate planner, or your attorney. Those are different licenses, different duties, different people.

The team, at full strength: agent for deals · tax pro for the IRS · fiduciary advisor for the plan · attorney for the contracts.

One person claiming all four seats isn't a team. It's a concentration of risk with a nice title.

The most misunderstood role in athlete finance is the agent — mostly through over-assignment. An agent is one seat on the team, not the team.

What the role covers: sourcing and negotiating deals, marketing the athlete to brands, and managing the commercial relationships around a career. Compensation is typically a percentage of the deals delivered — which means the agent's expertise and incentive both live on the revenue side of your life.

What the role doesn't cover: the stewardship side. Tax planning and filings belong to a tax professional. Investment strategy belongs to an advisor — ideally a fiduciary, legally bound to your interests. Contracts and legal exposure belong to an attorney. These are separate licenses and separate duties, and the incentives that make a great dealmaker are not the incentives you want managing what the deals earn.

Two diligence notes: agent and NIL-representation rules vary by state and level — your school's compliance office knows the current ones. And representation agreements are themselves contracts: percentage, scope, term, and exit clauses deserve an independent attorney's read before signing.

Build the full bench. Then let each professional play their position.
