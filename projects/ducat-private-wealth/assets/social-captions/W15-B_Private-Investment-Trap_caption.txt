Subhead:   Why exclusive-sounding deals burn athletes more often than markets do.

Ask the professionals who clean up athlete finances what did the damage. It's rarely the stock market. It's the private deals.

The pattern has a shape:

It arrives through a friend, so diligence feels rude.
It's framed as exclusive — access as flattery.
There's no public price, so nothing contradicts the story.
It can't be sold — money in, no way out.
And it's concentrated — one restaurant, one app, one "sure thing."

The defenses: assume every private dollar is locked and could go to zero. Size it so zero doesn't change your life. Verify the seller at adviserinfo.sec.gov or brokercheck.finra.org. And let an independent professional read it — before, not after.

Public markets, whatever their moods, come with disclosure, pricing, and an exit. The deals that hollow out athlete wealth usually have none of the three.

The trap's anatomy is consistent. Sourcing through relationships — a teammate's cousin, a gym friend — so skepticism feels like betrayal. Exclusivity as the pitch, because access flatters people whose lives run on being selected. No market price, so the story is the valuation. Illiquidity, so by the time doubt arrives, the money can't follow it out. And concentration — a single restaurant, product, or venture carrying a meaningful slice of a short career's earnings.

None of this makes private investing illegitimate; it makes it a category with the guardrails removed. The professional defenses are correspondingly blunt. Assume any private dollar is locked up indefinitely and can go to zero — then size the position so that outcome is an anecdote, not an era. Verify whoever's selling at brokercheck.finra.org or adviserinfo.sec.gov; unregistered sellers of securities are the end of the conversation. Demand the documents, and pay an independent professional — one with no stake in the raise — to read them. And impose time: real opportunities survive two weeks of questions; manufactured ones expire loudly.

Private and equity investments are high-risk, illiquid, and can lose everything. That's not the fine print here — it's the lesson.
