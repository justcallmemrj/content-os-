One banking habit quietly upgrades everything else: keep NIL money and personal money in separate accounts.

Why it works:

Taxes get cleaner. Business income and deductible expenses sit in one place — no archaeology in April.

Records get safer. If anyone ever asks you to document your NIL activity, the account is the record.

Decisions get clearer. You can see what the business earns versus what you spend — two numbers that should never be strangers.

The habit: every deal pays into the business account; you transfer to personal on your schedule, not the money's.

Simple to set up this week. Valuable for years.

If there's one administrative habit worth building in an athlete's first earning year, it's separation: business money in one account, personal money in another.

The payoff shows up three ways. First, taxes — NIL income and its deductible expenses live in one clean ledger, which makes accurate filing dramatically easier and helps nothing get missed. Second, documentation — schools, tax authorities, or future advisors may need a clear record of your NIL activity, and a dedicated account is that record by default. Third, judgment — when earnings and spending share one account, neither number is visible; when they're separated, you can actually see what your name is earning and what your life is costing.

The operating rhythm is simple: every deal pays into the business account; transfers to personal happen on a schedule you set. Add a basic expense log and a folder for contracts and invoices, and you have a bookkeeping system a professional can work with — built in an afternoon.
