Subhead:   How rights-based income works — and why it needs managing.

Some income you work for. Some income works for you. Royalties are the second kind — and athletes are earning them more than ever.

The mechanism: when your name, image, voice, or content gets used on an ongoing basis — a video game, merchandise lines, streaming content, licensed media — the contract can pay you per use or per sale, for as long as the use continues. Residuals are the entertainment version: repeat payment when work re-airs or re-sells.

The catch: rights-based income is only as good as its paperwork. The rate, the duration, the reporting, the audit rights — all live in the contract.

Which is why the rule is absolute: agreements that pay forever deserve an attorney before they're signed once.

Most athlete income is traded hour-for-dollar: play, appear, post, get paid. Rights-based income breaks that trade. Royalties and residuals pay because something you own — your likeness, your content, your brand — keeps getting used after the work is done.

The modern athlete versions are concrete: group licensing that puts your name and likeness in video games and on merchandise; content libraries that platforms continue monetizing; licensed product lines; media work that generates residuals when it re-airs or re-sells. Each is a stream defined not by effort but by an agreement — the rate, the base it's calculated on, the term, and the territory.

That makes the paperwork the asset. Four contract elements decide what a royalty stream is actually worth: the rate and its base (a percentage of what, exactly — gross, net, and "net of what" being the classic trap); the duration and any reversion of rights; the reporting obligation (how you learn what's owed); and audit rights (how you verify it). Streams without reporting and audit language have a way of underperforming quietly for years.

The management burden is real but bounded: track the agreements, read the statements, audit periodically, and treat the income as the variable stream it is. And the standing rule: any contract that can pay — or bind — for years gets a qualified attorney's review before signature. Income that outlasts the season should, at minimum, be understood as well as a season is.
