Your credit history is a record of how you handle borrowed money — and it starts the day you start it.

The clean way in: one starter or secured credit card. Put one small recurring charge on it. Pay the full balance, on time, every month. That's the entire program.

What quietly builds the record: on-time payments, low balances relative to your limit, and accounts that age.

What quietly damages it: missed payments, maxed cards, cosigning favors, and cash-advance habits.

You can check your reports free at annualcreditreport.com — worth doing once a year, even young.

Boring, consistent, early. Credit rewards exactly the habits training does.

Credit is one of the few financial systems where starting small and early is the entire strategy.

Your credit history is a documented track record of borrowing behavior. Lenders, landlords, insurers, and sometimes employers read it. For a young athlete, the goal isn't to use credit for lifestyle — it's to quietly build a clean record before you ever need it for something that matters.

The starter program is deliberately boring: one card (a secured card works if approval is hard), one small recurring charge — a subscription, a phone bill — and a full, on-time payment every month. The record builds itself from there. Time does most of the work: payment history, low utilization relative to limits, and account age are the durable ingredients.

The traps are equally predictable: missed payments, balances that ride near the limit, casually cosigning for friends or family, and cash advances. Each one costs far more to undo than it took to avoid.

Once a year, pull your reports free at annualcreditreport.com and confirm everything on them is actually yours. Quiet maintenance now buys real options later.
