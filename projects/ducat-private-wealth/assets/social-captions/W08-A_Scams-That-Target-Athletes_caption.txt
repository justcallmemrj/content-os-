Athletes get targeted by scams for a simple reason: visible money, busy schedules, and networks full of friendly introductions.

The patterns repeat:

"Guaranteed" returns — real investing never guarantees.
Pressure to move fast — urgency is a tactic, not a coincidence.
Exclusive access "just for you" — flattery is the sales pitch.
Secrecy — "don't tell your advisor" is a confession.
Friend-of-a-friend deals — trust transferred is not trust earned.

The defense is unglamorous: verify anyone touching your money at brokercheck.finra.org or adviserinfo.sec.gov, slow every decision down, and let no payment leave under pressure.

If it can't survive a week of questions, it was never real.

Fraud against athletes rarely looks like fraud. It looks like opportunity, introduced by someone you know.

The targeting logic is straightforward: young earners with visible income, demanding schedules, limited financial experience, and tight-knit networks where one warm introduction opens every door. Scammers don't defeat your judgment — they route around it, through people you trust.

The recurring signatures are worth memorizing. Guarantees of return (legitimate investing offers none). Manufactured urgency ("the round closes Friday"). Exclusivity as flattery ("we only bring this to a few athletes"). Requests for secrecy, especially from your existing advisors. And deals that arrive through social proximity rather than professional channels.

The countermeasures are free and fast: look up anyone offering investments at brokercheck.finra.org or adviserinfo.sec.gov — unregistered "advisors" end the conversation. Impose a waiting period on every opportunity; real ones survive a week of diligence. And treat "don't tell anyone" as the loudest possible alarm.

The FTC's consumer.ftc.gov is worth a bookmark for current fraud patterns. Skepticism isn't cynicism — it's how earned money stays earned.
