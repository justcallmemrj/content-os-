Not all of your dollars are taxed alike — and the difference is worth understanding early.

Money you earn — NIL deals, salaries, appearance fees — is ordinary income, taxed at the regular rate schedule as you earn it.

Money your investments earn when you sell them is a capital gain, and here the calendar matters: hold an investment beyond the required period, and the gain generally qualifies for long-term treatment — historically taxed more favorably than ordinary income. Sell quickly, and it's taxed like your paycheck.

Current rates and holding rules live at irs.gov, and your own picture belongs with a tax professional. The principle to keep: earning builds capital, but patient ownership is often taxed kindest.

Do you know what your after-tax number really is? Save this for tax season.

One distinction quietly shapes long-term wealth more than most investment picks: the tax system treats earned money and grown money differently.

Earned income — deals, wages, bonuses, appearance fees — is taxed as ordinary income, at the familiar bracket rates, in the year it arrives. There's little to optimize about that beyond good planning.

Investment gains run on different rules. Sell an asset for more than you paid and the profit is a capital gain — but its tax character depends on the holding period. Gains on assets held beyond the required threshold generally qualify as long-term and have historically been taxed at preferential rates; gains taken quickly are short-term and taxed like ordinary income. Same asset, same profit, different patience — meaningfully different after-tax outcomes. The specific rates, thresholds, and holding rules are set by current law and belong to irs.gov and your tax professional, not to a social post.

Why this matters especially for athletes: a short earning window means the real wealth plan is converting income into investments and letting them work for decades. Understanding that the tax system generally rewards that patience — and quietly penalizes rapid trading — aligns the incentives with the strategy already worth following.

After-tax compounding is the compounding that counts.
