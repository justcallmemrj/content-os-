Here's the part of an NIL deal nobody posts about: a share of it already belongs to the tax system.

Most NIL income arrives with no tax withheld. There's no employer quietly setting money aside from each payment the way a job's paycheck does. The full amount lands in your account — and the tax on it is still owed, generally as both income tax and self-employment tax.

That's why experienced earners think in after-tax numbers. The deal amount is the headline. The amount you can actually spend is smaller.

A simple habit: every time NIL money arrives, move a portion into a separate tax account before you touch the rest. A tax professional can help you size that percentage for your situation.

Do you know what your after-tax number really is? Save this for tax season.

The most common NIL surprise isn't a bad deal — it's April.

Most NIL income is paid gross, with nothing withheld. A traditional paycheck quietly routes money to taxes before you ever see it; a brand payment does not. The athlete receives the full amount, and the obligation — generally both income tax and self-employment tax — remains outstanding until it's paid.

The practical discipline is to think in after-tax numbers from the moment a deal is signed. The contract figure is a headline; the spendable figure is smaller, and the difference is not optional.

One habit solves most of this: a separate account that receives a fixed share of every payment on arrival, reserved for taxes. The right percentage depends on your income, your state, and your circumstances — which is exactly the conversation to have with a qualified tax professional before the first payment, not after the first notice.

Do you know what your after-tax number really is? Save this for tax season.
