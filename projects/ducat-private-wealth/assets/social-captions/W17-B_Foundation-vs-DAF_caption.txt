When giving gets serious, two structures come up: the private foundation and the donor-advised fund. Different tools, different weights.

A DAF is the light option: quick to open, low cost, the sponsor handles administration, grants stay private if you want. Trade-off: you recommend grants; the sponsor technically approves.

A private foundation is the heavy option: its own legal entity, your family's control, the ability to hire staff and run programs — with real costs, governance duties, public filings, and annual payout requirements.

The honest rule of thumb: most families start with a DAF; foundations earn their overhead at serious scale and involvement. A tax advisor and attorney can map which fits your numbers.

Structured giving eventually asks a fork-in-the-road question: donor-advised fund, or private foundation? They're often discussed as rivals; they're really different weights of machinery.

The DAF is minimal machinery. Opened in days through a sponsoring organization, inexpensive to run, administration handled, grants recommendable on your schedule and — if preferred — private. Its limit is formal control: donors advise, sponsors approve (in practice, recommendations to qualified charities are routinely honored), and you won't employ staff or run your own programs through it.

The foundation is full machinery: a legal entity your family controls — board seats, hiring, direct charitable programs, multi-generational governance. The machinery bills accordingly: formation and ongoing costs, fiduciary duties, public annual filings (a foundation's giving is visible; note that for a public athlete), excise-tax rules, and a required minimum annual payout.

The pattern professionals see: DAFs fit most families most of the time, including as the permanent solution; foundations earn their overhead when scale, family involvement, and the desire to operate programs — not just fund them — all arrive together. Some families ultimately run both, using each for what it does best.

Where the line sits for your numbers is a planning conversation with a qualified tax advisor and attorney. The good news embedded in the question: a family asking it is already giving at a level worth structuring.
