The uncomfortable statistic behind athlete fraud: the thief usually isn't a stranger. It's someone with access — a manager, an advisor, sometimes family.

Not because athletes trust the wrong people. Because access without oversight tempts even "safe" people, and busy athletes delegate access constantly.

The controls that actually work:

No one person moves money alone — every transfer needs a second eye.
Statements go directly to you — from the bank or custodian, not through the person they'd expose.
Someone independent reviews the accounts on a schedule.
No blank signatures, no full powers of attorney handed out for convenience.

Trust is a relationship. Controls are a system. You need both — because betrayal never announces itself early.

Financial betrayal in sport has a consistent profile, and it isn't the anonymous scammer. It's the insider: the business manager, the longtime advisor, the relative with the login — someone whose access was total and whose oversight was none.

The mechanism is rarely sophisticated. It's unsupervised access, compounding: authority over accounts, statements routed through the very person they would incriminate, an athlete busy being an athlete, and years of small diversions that no one was positioned to notice. By the time it surfaces, the money is spent and the relationship is the second casualty.

The defense is not better instincts about people — everyone's instincts fail exactly once. It's controls, boring and structural. Segregated authority: no single person, however trusted, can move money alone; meaningful transfers require a second, independent approval. Direct visibility: statements delivered to you from the custodian itself, reviewed monthly — access no intermediary can filter. Independent review: a professional with no transaction authority reconciling the accounts on a schedule, precisely because they have nothing to hide and no one to protect. And signature discipline: no blank authorizations, no sweeping powers of attorney issued for convenience.

Well-designed controls don't insult honest professionals; they protect them, by making the question unnecessary. The people who resist oversight are answering it.

Trust generously. Verify structurally. In that order, always.
