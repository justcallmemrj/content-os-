Some brands will offer you equity instead of cash — "a piece of the company" instead of a check. Neither is automatically better. They're different bets:

Cash is certain, spendable, and taxed as income now. Its value doesn't depend on anyone's business plan.

Equity is a stake in what the company becomes. It can be worth multiples of the cash offer — or nothing. Usually you can't sell it for years, and its tax story is its own conversation.

Before trading cash for equity, ask: what percentage, of what company, with what rights, sellable when — and what happens if it fails?

A useful discipline: never trade all the cash. Certainty pays bills; equity pays maybe.

As NIL matures, more athletes see a new line in term sheets: equity — ownership in the brand — offered in place of some or all of the cash.

The trade is real, and it deserves clear eyes. Cash is certainty: spendable, bankable, taxed as ordinary income now, indifferent to whether the company thrives. Equity is a claim on a future: potentially worth far more than the displaced cash, potentially worth zero, and usually illiquid — unsellable until some distant event like an acquisition, if that event ever comes.

The questions that separate an informed holder from a hopeful one: What percentage is this, and of what entity? What rights attach — voting, information, protection against dilution when new investors arrive? When, realistically, could this ever be sold? What does the company's survival actually depend on? And how will this be taxed — a question with genuinely different answers depending on structure, worth a professional's attention before signing.

A discipline many professionals apply: treat equity as upside, never as rent money. Take enough cash to make the work worth doing if the equity dies; let the ownership be the bonus.

Private stakes are high-risk and illiquid by nature — nothing here is a recommendation. It's the vocabulary for reading the offer.
