Athlete income doesn't arrive like a paycheck. It arrives like weather — a big month, then quiet.

"Pay yourself first" is the system built for that. The move: the day money lands, split it by fixed percentages before you spend anything. A share to taxes. A share to savings. A share to spending. Every payment, same split, no negotiation with yourself.

Then live off a steady monthly "salary" you transfer from the spending pool — so a big month doesn't become a big lifestyle, and a quiet month doesn't become a crisis.

Discipline in training is percentages and reps. Money works the same way.

Variable income breaks the budgeting most of us were taught, because most budgeting assumes a predictable paycheck. Athletes rarely have one.

The system that survives irregular income is paying yourself first: the day any payment arrives, it's divided by fixed percentages — before any spending happens. One share is reserved for taxes. One share goes to savings and long-term goals. What remains funds day-to-day life.

The refinement that makes it durable: pay yourself a level monthly "salary" out of the spending share. Income arrives in waves; your lifestyle draws a straight line through them. Strong months build the buffer. Quiet months draw on it. Nothing about your standard of living has to whipsaw with your deal flow.

None of this requires sophistication — it requires the same thing training does: a routine you don't renegotiate every day. Set the percentages once, automate the transfers where you can, and let the system do the deciding.
