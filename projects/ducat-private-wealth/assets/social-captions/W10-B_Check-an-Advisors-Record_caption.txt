Anyone can print "wealth advisor" on a business card. Regulators keep the actual receipts — and they're free to read.

Two public databases cover nearly everyone legitimate:

BrokerCheck (brokercheck.finra.org) — brokers and their firms: licenses, employment history, customer complaints, regulatory actions.

IAPD (adviserinfo.sec.gov) — investment advisers: registrations, firm details, disclosures.

The five-minute habit: search the name before any serious conversation. Licensed and clean? Proceed to better questions. Not findable at all? That's not a maybe — someone selling investments without registration is your answer.

Every professional expects to be looked up. The ones who discourage it are telling you something.

Due diligence on a financial professional sounds like work reserved for lawyers. In practice, the core of it is two free websites and a few minutes.

FINRA's BrokerCheck (brokercheck.finra.org) covers brokers and brokerage firms: whether they're licensed, where they've worked, and — most usefully — their disclosure history: customer complaints, regulatory actions, terminations, and certain financial events.

The SEC's Investment Adviser Public Disclosure database (adviserinfo.sec.gov) covers investment advisers and their firms: registration status, the firm's Form ADV, and disciplinary history.

Between them, nearly every legitimate professional who would touch your money appears somewhere — searchable by name in under a minute. The reading takes four more: verify the license is current, scan the history, and note whether the story they told you matches the record.

Two outcomes end the process early. A record of repeated disclosures invites harder questions. And no registration anywhere, from someone offering investments, isn't ambiguity — it's the conclusion.

For athlete families building a team: run every name, every time, before the first dollar moves. Professionals expect it. Investor.gov has more on what the records mean.
