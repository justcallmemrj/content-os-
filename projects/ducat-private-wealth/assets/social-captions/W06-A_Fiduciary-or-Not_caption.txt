One word tells you more about a financial advisor than any brochure: fiduciary.

A fiduciary is legally required to put your interests ahead of their own — not just to sell you something "suitable." Registered investment advisers owe this duty. Many other people with advisor-sounding titles don't, or only owe it part-time.

The test is simple. Ask: "Are you a fiduciary to me, at all times, on all accounts — and will you put that in writing?"

A real fiduciary answers fast and in writing. Hesitation is also an answer.

For athlete families building a team, this is question one. Everything else comes after.

Titles in financial services are marketing. Duties are law. The word that separates them is "fiduciary."

A fiduciary advisor is legally obligated to act in your best interest — to put your outcomes ahead of their compensation, and to disclose conflicts. Registered investment advisers carry this duty. Many other professionals who look identical at a distance operate under lighter standards, where a recommendation only needs to be broadly appropriate, not best-for-you.

For athlete families, the distinction is not academic. Young, visible earners attract advice from every direction, and the difference between "suitable" and "best interest" is exactly where expensive products live.

The screening question costs nothing: "Are you a fiduciary to me at all times, across everything you'll handle for me — and will you confirm that in writing?" Clean answer, in writing: proceed to the next questions. Hedging, pivoting, or "let me explain how I'm paid" without an answer: you've learned what you needed.

Understanding comes before action — and before signatures.
