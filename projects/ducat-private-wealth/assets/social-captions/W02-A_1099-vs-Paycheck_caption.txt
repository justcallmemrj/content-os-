A paycheck and a brand payment can be the same dollar amount — and completely different money.

With a paycheck (W-2 work), your employer withholds income tax, pays part of your Social Security and Medicare taxes, and may add benefits.

With 1099 income — how most NIL money arrives — you're an independent contractor. Nothing is withheld. You owe the taxes yourself, including self-employment tax, which covers both halves of Social Security and Medicare. No benefits come attached.

The upside: legitimate business expenses may reduce what's taxable — which makes recordkeeping worth real money.

Same number on the check. Different job to do after it arrives.

Do you know what your after-tax number really is? Save this for tax season.

One distinction explains most of athlete tax life: W-2 versus 1099.

W-2 employment handles the machinery for you. Taxes are withheld from each paycheck, the employer pays a share of Social Security and Medicare, and benefits often ride along.

1099 income — the form behind most NIL payments — makes you the machinery. You're treated as an independent business. Nothing is withheld; you owe income tax plus self-employment tax, which covers both the employee and employer halves of Social Security and Medicare. No benefits are attached.

There is a compensating advantage: as a business, your legitimate, documented expenses may reduce taxable income — equipment, travel for deals, production costs, professional fees. That turns recordkeeping from a chore into something with a real return.

For families new to this, the takeaway is simple: 1099 money isn't worse money, it's money with homework. Know what's owed, set it aside on arrival, and keep the receipts. A tax professional can tailor the details to your state and situation.
