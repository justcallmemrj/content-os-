There's a retirement account almost perfectly designed for a young athlete with NIL income — and most never open it.

A Roth IRA works like this: you contribute money you've already paid tax on. It grows for decades. Qualified withdrawals in retirement are generally tax-free.

The catch that's actually a feature: you need earned income to contribute — and NIL income generally counts.

Why now beats later: young earners are usually in their lowest tax bracket. Paying tax on the seed while it's small, then never on the tree, is the whole point.

Contribution limits apply and change — check the current figure at irs.gov, and size it with your tax professional.

One of the quietest advantages a young NIL earner has is time — and one account is built to exploit it.

A Roth IRA reverses the usual tax deal. Contributions are made with money that's already been taxed; in exchange, growth compounds untouched and qualified withdrawals in retirement are generally tax-free. You pay tax on the seed, not the harvest.

Two mechanics matter for athletes. First, contributions require earned income — and NIL compensation generally qualifies, which puts this account within reach years before most peers. Second, the math favors low brackets: a college athlete is often paying less tax on that seed money than they ever will again, which makes the trade unusually attractive right now.

Annual contribution limits apply and are updated over time — verify the current figure at irs.gov rather than relying on a post. And because eligibility interacts with total income and filing details, sizing the contribution is a good first project with a tax professional.

Decades of tax-free compounding, started from a college dorm. Few financial moves age better.
