An athlete's most valuable asset isn't a deal or a car. It's the future earning power of a healthy body — and that asset can be insured.

Two coverages matter here:

Disability insurance replaces income if injury or illness ends your ability to earn as an athlete. It answers: what happens to the plan if the body stops cooperating?

Loss-of-value coverage is more specialized — built for elite prospects, it can pay when an injury doesn't end a career but measurably lowers a future contract's value.

Who needs what depends on realistic projected earnings — a conversation, not a slogan. These are specialized policies: work with a licensed specialist, and read every exclusion before relying on it.

In most careers, earning power builds for decades. An athlete's is concentrated, early, and physically fragile — which changes how insurance should be thought about.

Disability coverage addresses the fundamental risk: an injury or illness that ends the ability to compete. A policy replaces a portion of the income the career would have produced. For an athlete whose family finances lean on that future, this is the foundational protection — in place before it's needed, because it can't be bought after.

Loss-of-value coverage is the specialized layer. Designed for athletes with significant projected professional value, it can pay when an injury doesn't end the career but demonstrably reduces what the next contract is worth — the gap between the expected and the actual.

Both are underwritten, priced on sport, position, health, and projections, and dense with exclusions that matter. Which is the honest takeaway: this isn't a product to buy from a post. It's a category to understand, then evaluate with a licensed specialist who works with athletes — ideally alongside your fiduciary advisor, before the season where it matters.
