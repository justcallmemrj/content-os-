Money habits get taught two ways: by conversation, or by accident. Giving is the habit most worth teaching on purpose.

A family giving tradition can be simple:

A planned amount — giving comes off the top, decided yearly, not squeezed from leftovers.

Everyone chooses — each family member, kids included, directs a share to a cause they picked and can explain.

One annual review — what did we give, what did it do, what deserves more next year.

What it builds: a family that treats generosity as a discipline, kids who learn money has purposes beyond spending, and a tradition that outlasts any season.

Have you and your family talked this one through yet? Save it for that conversation.

Ask families who handle wealth well what they got right early, and giving comes up more often than investing. Not because of the tax lines — because of what the practice teaches.

A giving tradition needs only three components. A planned amount: generosity budgeted annually, off the top, so it's a commitment rather than a remainder. Distributed choice: every family member — including the youngest — directs a share toward a cause they select and must be able to explain. The explaining is the education. And an annual review: an hour, once a year, on what was given, what it accomplished, and what earns more next year.

For athlete families, the timing has extra weight. Early income compresses financial adulthood into a few seasons; a giving practice is how a family says, out loud and on a schedule, that the money serves values and not only appetites. It also builds the muscle that later, larger philanthropy will need: evaluating organizations, measuring impact, saying no kindly.

Structure can come later — donor-advised funds and formal vehicles exist when scale justifies them, and advisors can help there. The tradition doesn't wait for structure. It starts at whatever size the season allows, this year, with everyone at the table.

Have you and your family talked this one through yet? Save it for that conversation.
