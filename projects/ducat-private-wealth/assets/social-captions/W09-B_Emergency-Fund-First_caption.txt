Before investing, before deals, before anything interesting: the emergency fund. It's the least exciting money you'll ever hold — and the most load-bearing.

What it is: cash, in a separate savings account, reserved for genuine emergencies. Commonly sized at several months of essential expenses — more when your income arrives in waves, like athlete income does.

What it's for: making bad months boring. A car repair, an injury, a deal that falls through — the fund absorbs it, so you never have to borrow at the worst moment or sell investments at the wrong one.

It's not an investment. It's the reason your investments never get raided. Build it first, then build on it.

Every durable financial plan rests on the same unglamorous base: cash that exists so surprises don't become decisions.

An emergency fund is liquid savings — separate from spending, separate from investing — reserved for genuine disruption: injury, a canceled deal, family emergencies, the expensive week no one schedules. Guidance commonly lands at a few months of essential expenses, and athletes sit at the higher end, because variable income means the gaps between paydays are part of the design, not a malfunction.

Its job is structural. With a funded buffer, a bad month is absorbed quietly: no high-interest borrowing, no liquidating investments in a down market, no cascade where one setback prices the next. Without it, even a good portfolio is fragile, because life's timing doesn't consult your accounts.

Order of operations matters: the buffer comes before serious investing, not after. Growth can wait a season; the foundation can't. Build it in the same boring way everything durable gets built — a fixed share of every payment, automatically, until it's full.
