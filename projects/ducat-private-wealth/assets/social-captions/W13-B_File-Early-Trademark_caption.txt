The trademark system has a bias most athletes learn too late: it rewards whoever moves first — not whoever's most famous.

Wait until your name is big, and you may find someone already registered it — your nickname, your logo, your catchphrase — and now you're negotiating for your own identity.

File early, while it's cheap and uncontested, and the record shows what's yours before it's worth fighting over.

What's registrable: names, logos, and phrases used as a brand — tied to specific categories of goods and services you actually use them on.

An attorney who handles trademarks can run a search and file properly. As brand moves go, this one is famously cheaper early.

In trademark law, timing is a form of leverage — and the leverage runs against whoever waits.

Registration at the USPTO establishes enforceable, documented rights in a mark: a name, logo, or phrase used in commerce, registered against specific classes of goods and services. The practical problem for rising athletes is that registration is available to whoever properly files and uses the mark — which means a growing name that nobody registered is an invitation. Athletes have discovered their own nicknames and catchphrases registered by strangers, then faced the absurd task of negotiating back the use of their own identity.

Filing early inverts that story. While a brand is small, searches are cleaner, oppositions are rarer, and the cost is modest. The registration then grows in value silently alongside the career — infrastructure nobody sees until the day it's the difference between a cease-and-desist you send and one you receive.

The sensible sequence: inventory what you actually use as a brand; have a trademark attorney run a clearance search; register in the classes that match your real merchandise and services, with room for the obvious next ones.

Not legal advice — filing strategy is technical and fact-specific. But the principle is simple enough to act on: protect the name before it's big, because "big" is exactly when it gets expensive.
