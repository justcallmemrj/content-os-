Your name earns money. That makes it an asset — and assets get protected on purpose.

Two protections cover an athlete's identity:

Right of publicity — your legal right to control the commercial use of your name, image, and identity. It's the reason companies can't put you in an ad without permission. Rules vary by state.

Trademark — registered protection for your name, logo, or catchphrase used as a brand on merchandise and services. Registration turns "that's mine" into something you can enforce.

The habit that matters: treat unauthorized use of your name as seriously as a bad contract — document it, and know an attorney who handles brand work.

Athletes talk about "building a brand." Fewer talk about the legal architecture that makes a brand ownable.

Two doctrines do most of the work. The right of publicity is your inherent right to control how your name, image, and identity are used commercially — the reason your face can't sell someone's product without your consent. It exists automatically, but its strength and details vary meaningfully by state.

A trademark is different: registered protection for the specific marks you use in commerce — a name, a logo, a phrase on merchandise or services. Where the right of publicity protects who you are, a trademark protects the brand you're building, and registration converts informal ownership into enforceable rights with real remedies.

For an earning athlete, the practical program is modest: know what your identity is appearing on and where; keep records of your marks and when you started using them; treat unauthorized use as a business problem, not a compliment; and have a relationship with an attorney who handles trademark and publicity work before you urgently need one.

Not legal advice — brand law is genuinely state-specific and technical. But the mindset is universal: names that earn deserve the same protection as anything else that earns.
