The best time for an athlete family's first real money conversation is before the first real money. The questions matter more than the answers:

Who sees the accounts — and who decides?
What happens to each check — spend, save, tax, give: in what split?
What do we say when people ask for money — and who says it?
Who are our professionals — and who vets them?
What does this income need to become in ten years?

One hour, everyone present, notes taken. Then a short check-in each month.

Families that talk about money on purpose make decisions once. Families that don't, renegotiate every deal in the kitchen.

Have you and your family talked this one through yet? Save it for that conversation.

Athlete money is family money — not legally, but practically. Decisions land at kitchen tables, and the families who navigate NIL well tend to be the ones who scheduled the conversation before the income made it urgent.

The agenda that works is short and direct. Visibility and authority: who sees the accounts, and which decisions need which voices. The split: what happens to every payment on arrival — the percentages for taxes, saving, spending, and giving — so each check follows a rule instead of a mood. The outside world: what the family says when requests come, and who delivers it, so no single person carries every no. The bench: which professionals — tax, advisory, legal — the family uses, and who checks their records. And the horizon: what this compressed earning window should have built by the time it closes.

Then the maintenance rhythm: a brief monthly review — what arrived, what moved, what's coming — so the plan stays a shared fact rather than one person's burden.

None of this requires expertise. It requires a calendar invite and the willingness to say numbers out loud to people you love. Understanding comes before action — for families too.

Have you and your family talked this one through yet? Save it for that conversation.
