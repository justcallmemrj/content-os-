### Introduction: Before the money moves

Here is the situation nobody fully prepared families for.

For over a century, college athletes could not be paid. Then, in the span of about four years, almost everything changed. Since 2021, athletes can earn money from their name, image, and likeness. Since the summer of 2025, many schools can pay athletes directly through revenue sharing. High school athletes in most states, including Florida, can now sign deals under certain rules. Money that used to be impossible is now sitting in inboxes, DMs, and contract PDFs — sometimes before an athlete has taken a single college class.

The playbooks that existed for this were written for professionals: 22-year-olds with agents, union protections, and team accountants. Almost nothing was written for a 17-year-old with a strong junior season, a growing follower count, and a family that has never talked openly about money.

That is the reader this book was written for.

Two beliefs shape every page. First: **the amount doesn't matter yet; the system does.** An athlete earning $1,800 from a local car dealership and an athlete earning $180,000 in revenue sharing need the same foundation — separate accounts, a tax routine, a deal log, honest family rules. The dollars change. The system doesn't. Second: **this is a family subject.** Money that arrives during high school or college lands in the middle of a household, with all of its love, pressure, history, and hope. Pretending otherwise is how good families end up in bad arguments.

By the last page, you will have that foundation in place: accounts opened and organized, a percentage-based budget built for uneven income, a tax routine that removes the scariest surprise in athlete finance, ground rules your family actually said out loud, and a short list of professionals who do what this book cannot.

None of it requires being rich. All of it works better started early.

A word about who this book is for — and who it isn't. If you're a high school athlete whose phone just started buzzing, this is for you. If you're a college athlete already earning and quietly aware that your system is a shoebox, this is for you. If you're the parent reading over a shoulder, unsure whether to steer or stay out of the way, large parts of this book were written directly to you, in sections marked for families. And if you're a coach, counselor, or compliance staffer who hands books to families — welcome; you'll know exactly who needs which chapter.

Just as important is what this book will not do. It will not tell you what to invest in; investing gets its own book at the end of this series, after the foundation exists to build on. It will not predict your income, promise any outcome, or pretend that rules written in pencil are written in stone. Only people who know your facts can hand you answers.

Let's build it before the money moves.

## CHAPTER 1 — The New Money Era of College Sports

*Rules described in this chapter are current as of July 2026 and continue to change. Confirm details with your school's compliance office.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Jalen found out from a group chat. His teammate sent a screenshot — their university had opted into the new revenue-sharing model, and football players would be getting direct payments starting in the fall. Fifteen minutes later, Jalen's uncle texted: *You about to be rich, nephew.* Ten minutes after that, a guy he'd met once at a camp DM'd him about a "can't-miss investment opportunity for athletes like you."
>
> Jalen was seventeen. He hadn't signed anywhere yet. He didn't know what revenue sharing was, whether it applied to him, or why a near-stranger suddenly wanted his money. He just knew that everyone around him had opinions, and he had questions.
>
> That night he tried to research it himself and found three articles that contradicted each other, a forum thread from two years ago describing rules that no longer existed, and a video from a guy whose credentials were a rented Lamborghini. His mom asked what he'd learned. "That nobody agrees on anything," he said. He was closer to the truth than he knew — and still nowhere near prepared.
>
> This chapter is for the questions.

### What actually changed

For most of college sports history, the rule was simple and strict: play for the school, and the school can give you a scholarship — nothing more. Payment of almost any kind could end your eligibility.

That world ended in two big waves.

**Wave one: NIL, 2021.** After a Supreme Court decision and a rush of new state laws, the NCAA changed course in July 2021. Athletes could now earn money from their **name, image, and likeness** — NIL for short. Not payment for playing; payment for being *you*. A local restaurant pays you to post about their wings. A trading-card company licenses your autograph. A camp pays you to teach kids your position. Chapter 2 unpacks how it all works.

**Wave two: revenue sharing, 2025.** A massive legal settlement — usually called the **House settlement**, after one of the athletes who sued — was approved by a federal judge in June 2025. It did two enormous things. It set aside roughly $2.6 to $2.8 billion in back pay for athletes who competed before the NIL era. And going forward, it allowed schools to pay current athletes **directly**, out of the school's own athletic revenue, starting July 1, 2025. Most big athletic programs — roughly four out of five Division I schools — opted in. Chapter 3 explains how that money works, because it follows completely different rules than NIL.

Parts of the settlement were still being challenged in appeals courts as this book went to print. The structure described here is the one operating today; details can shift, which is exactly why the habit of *verifying before acting* shows up in every chapter of this book.

### The three lanes of athlete money

From here forward, sort every dollar you hear about into one of three lanes. They look similar from the outside. They are not.

**Lane 1 — Scholarships and financial aid.** The oldest lane. Tuition, housing, meals, stipends. Mostly familiar, though even this lane has tax wrinkles (Chapter 7) and new roster rules following the settlement.

**Lane 2 — Revenue sharing.** Direct payments from your school, under a school-wide cap, governed by a real contract with real terms. Not guaranteed to any individual athlete. Not the same as NIL, no matter how often people blur them together.

**Lane 3 — Third-party NIL.** Money from businesses, brands, collectives, and people who are *not* your school, in exchange for the use of your name, image, or likeness plus real work — posts, appearances, content, autographs. This lane has its own reporting rules and its own referees.

Why does sorting matter? Because each lane has different paperwork, different tax treatment, different rules about what you must report and to whom, and different ways to lose the money — or your eligibility — if you handle it carelessly. When someone says "athletes get paid now," they are compressing three complicated systems into four words. You no longer have that luxury. The athletes who do well in this era are the ones who know which lane a dollar came from before they decide what to do with it.

### What didn't change

Amid everything new, four old truths survived intact, and they anchor this whole book. **Eligibility still has rules** — the money is legal now, but mishandling the process around it (unreported deals, prohibited categories, unregistered agents) can still cost you the sport. **Taxes never took a day off** — every era's income has always belonged partly to the IRS, and this era's arrives with nothing withheld. **Contracts still bind** — a signature meant everything in 1990 and means everything now. And **character still compounds** — the athlete who was honest, punctual, and hard to rush before the money is the one businesses re-sign after it. The revolution changed what's possible. It changed nothing about what's wise.

### Why "early" is the real advantage

Here is the quiet truth underneath all the headlines: most athletes will not earn life-changing money in college. Some will earn a few hundred dollars. Some a few thousand. A smaller group will earn serious five- and six-figure income. A very small group will earn more.

The habits, however, don't care which group you're in. Opening the right accounts takes one afternoon whether you earn $500 or $500,000. Setting aside a percentage of every check for taxes is the same motion at every size. Learning to read a contract slowly is free. And the athlete who builds these habits on small money is the one who is calm and organized if big money ever arrives — while everyone else is learning in public, under pressure, with real dollars at stake.

You cannot control which lane pays you or how much. You can completely control whether there is a system waiting when it does.

### A short history in five dates

Tape this timeline somewhere mental. It's the whole revolution in five lines, and it's also your reminder of how fast this world moves — because every date on it landed inside a single athlete's eligibility window.

- **July 2021** — the NCAA permits NIL. Athletes can earn from name, image, and likeness for the first time.
- **June 6, 2025** — a federal judge approves the House settlement: billions in back pay to former athletes, and a green light for direct school payments.
- **July 1, 2025** — revenue sharing begins. Schools that opted in start paying athletes out of athletic revenue, under a cap.
- **2026** — the cap rises to roughly $21.5 million per school; a new enforcement body and reporting system settle into daily operation.
- **Now** — Congress and the courts are still arguing over the top layer of the rules. The story is not finished, which means your habit of checking can't be either.

Five dates, one lesson: an athlete who built their money knowledge in 2023 and never updated it is already working from an obsolete map. Date-stamp what you know. Refresh it every year, ideally each summer before the season resets.

#### What this means in real life

*Hypothetical, to make the three lanes concrete.* Picture Jalen one year later, a freshman now. His financial life looks like this: a scholarship covering tuition, housing, and meals (**Lane 1**); a revenue-sharing agreement paying $18,000 across the school year on a monthly schedule, per a contract he and an attorney actually read (**Lane 2**); and a $2,500 deal with a regional restaurant chain — four posts and two appearances (**Lane 3**).

Watch how differently each lane behaves. The scholarship mostly doesn't hit his bank account at all, but a slice of it may show up at tax time (Chapter 7). The rev-share money arrives from the school with no taxes withheld, governed by contract terms that include what happens if he transfers. The restaurant deal had to be reported through the clearinghouse within days of signing, obligates him to specific deliverables with deadlines, and will generate a tax form next January. Three lanes, three sets of paperwork, three different ways to get it right or wrong — inside one nineteen-year-old's ordinary Tuesday. That's why the sorting habit comes first in this book: every chapter that follows assumes you know which lane you're standing in.

#### What families should understand

Parents and guardians: the ground rules you grew up with are gone, and the new ones are genuinely confusing — schools, states, the NCAA, and Congress are still arguing about them. Two things help immediately. First, learn the three lanes alongside your athlete, because family decisions (taxes, whose name goes on accounts, financial aid) touch all three. Second, resist the urge to either take over completely or check out completely. Your athlete needs a partner in this, not a replacement and not an audience. The chapters ahead will give you specific places to plug in.

#### Mistakes to avoid

- Treating all athlete money as one thing. The lanes have different rules, taxes, and paperwork.
- Assuming a school's opt-in means *you* are owed anything. Revenue sharing is discretionary and contractual.
- Taking financial cues from group chats and DMs. Enthusiasm travels faster than accuracy.
- Waiting for "real money" before building the system. The system is the advantage.
- Believing the rules you heard last year still apply. In this era, last year is a long time ago.

1. Which lane is this money actually coming from — school, third party, or aid?
2. Who at my school's compliance office can confirm the current rules for my situation?
3. What has changed in the last twelve months that my family and I haven't caught up on?
4. Who in my circle actually understands this era, and who just has opinions?

## CHAPTER 2 — NIL, Explained in Plain English

*This chapter is education, not legal advice. Deal rules vary by school and state — confirm your situation with your compliance office and a qualified attorney.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Maya, a sophomore volleyball player, got two offers in the same week. A smoothie shop near campus offered her $400 a month to post twice a week and appear at one event per semester. A supplement brand offered her "free product for life" — no cash — in exchange for three posts a month with their hashtag.
>
> Her roommate said take both, it's free money and free smoothies. Her mom said take neither, it's probably against the rules. Her coach said ask compliance. All three were reacting to the same two emails, and none of them — including Maya — could yet answer the basic questions: what exactly was being bought, what exactly was being owed, and what did "free" actually cost?
>
> Maya did one smart thing before anything else: nothing. She thanked both companies, said she'd respond within the week, and printed the offers. Neither business flinched at the wait — a detail she'd later learn was itself information. Then she sat down to figure out what an NIL deal actually *is*, which is where this chapter begins.

### What NIL income actually is

NIL stands for **name, image, and likeness** — the three legal ingredients of your public identity. Your name on a poster. Your image in an ad. Your likeness in a video game or on a T-shirt. When a business pays you for permission to use any of these, that's an NIL deal.

Here is the part people skip: in almost every real deal, you're not just renting out your name. You're also doing **work** — creating posts, showing up to events, signing autographs, teaching at camps, filming content. An NIL contract is usually a bundle: *permission* (use of your identity) plus *deliverables* (things you must actually do). Both halves matter. The permission half determines what the business can do with your face after the deal ends. The deliverables half determines whether you've met your obligations — and whether you get paid.

Say it plainly and the mystery drains out: **an NIL deal is a small business transaction in which you are the business.**

### Who pays, and why

The money comes from a few recognizable directions:

- **National brands** — apparel, beverages, gaming, trading cards — paying for reach and story.
- **Local businesses** — restaurants, car dealerships, gyms, credit unions — paying for hometown attention. This is where most athletes' first deal comes from.
- **Collectives and booster-connected entities** — organizations formed around a school's fan base. These are real, common, and heavily watched by the new enforcement system (Chapter 4), because payments connected to boosters get extra scrutiny to confirm they're genuine business deals and not disguised recruiting payments.
- **Camps, lessons, and your own ventures** — money you generate yourself by teaching your sport, selling merchandise, or building content.

Notice what every payer has in common: they expect something back. Attention, content, appearances, sales. The moment you internalize that NIL money is *earned*, not gifted, most of the confusion in this space disappears — and so does most of the danger, because you start asking what's expected of you before you ask what's offered to you.

### Deal types at a glance

Most offers fall into a short list of shapes. You'll meet each in depth in Book Two; for now, learn to recognize them.

| Deal shape | What it usually involves |
|---|---|
| Social media promotion | Posts, stories, or videos featuring a product, on a schedule |
| Appearances | Store openings, meet-and-greets, watch parties, speaking |
| Autographs & memorabilia | Signings, signed merchandise, trading-card licensing |
| Camps & lessons | Teaching your sport, often under your own name |
| Merchandising & licensing | Your name/number on products; group licensing deals |
| Content & media | Podcasts, YouTube, streaming, brand-produced videos |
| Ambassadorships | Longer-term relationships bundling several of the above |

### "Free" product is income

The supplement offer in Maya's inbox — free product, no cash — is still an NIL deal, and here is the sentence that surprises almost every family: **things you receive in exchange for NIL work count as income at their fair market value.** If a brand sends you $200 shoes each month in exchange for posts, the IRS generally sees that the same way it sees $200 in cash. It can be reportable. It can be taxable. It absolutely belongs in your deal log (Chapter 5) and your tax conversation (Chapter 7).

Free is a price tag, not a category.

### Reading a deal in ninety seconds

Long before contract review — which still happens, every time — you can triage any offer with five questions, in order. Practice until it's reflex:

1. **Who** is paying? (A business you can verify? A collective? A stranger with a logo?)
2. **What** must I actually do, and by when?
3. **What rights** do they get to my name, image, and likeness — and for how long after the deal ends?
4. **When and how** do I get paid — cash, product, or both?
5. **What's it worth** — total value, including product at fair market value, divided honestly by the hours it will take?

That last division is the one athletes skip. A "$1,000 deal" requiring twelve hours of shoots, travel, and revisions is paying you about $83 an hour — great. A "$1,000 deal" requiring sixty hours is paying $16 and change — a job, not a windfall, and one that competes with practice, class, and sleep. Your time is the one asset every deal spends. Price it.

### Your first deal, specifically

Whatever your sport or platform, your first deal will probably look like this: local business, modest number, simple deliverables, offered through a DM, an email, or a collective's app. Treat it as your dress rehearsal, because that's exactly what it is. Run the full process at full seriousness — triage, compliance check, a real read of the contract (yes, even three pages for $400), the deal-log row, the W-9 copy, the set-aside on payment, the deliverables performed *early*. Two reasons. First, small deals with sloppy process become big deals with sloppy process; the habits you rehearse are the habits that show up under pressure. Second, local business owners talk to each other. The athlete who posted on time, showed up early, and was easy to work with just became the athlete the next three businesses call. In a real sense, your first deal's biggest payment is your reputation — and unlike the fee, that one compounds.

#### What this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Put Maya's two offers through the ninety-second triage. **The smoothie shop:** $400/month cash, eight posts and one event per semester — call it six hours a month. Verified local business, one-year term, rights limited to reposting her content while the deal runs. Value: $4,800/year, roughly $65/hour, clean rights. **The supplement brand:** no cash, product she estimates at $80/month retail, three posts monthly with their hashtag — about three hours. But the contract grants them use of her image in their marketing "in perpetuity," and supplements sit in a restricted category at many schools.

Same inbox, opposite deals. The first is a small, honest business arrangement. The second pays her about $27/hour in protein powder, wants her face forever, and might not even be permitted — three separate reasons the answer is a compliance call and an attorney read, not a shrug and a signature. Notice that Maya needed zero finance vocabulary to see all of this. She needed five questions and a calculator.

> ❝ *An NIL deal is a small business transaction in which you are the business.*

> **Ask your attorney:** before any signature — what exactly do the rights, exclusivity, and termination clauses commit me to, and what survives the end of the deal?

> **Family Conversation** — Parents and athlete separately triage the same (real or imagined) offer with the five questions, then compare notes. Where your answers differ is where your family's blind spots are.

#### What families should understand

Two things deserve a parent's attention here. First, **the permission half of these deals outlives the season.** A contract that grants a business broad, long-term rights to your child's image is a bigger decision than the dollar amount suggests, which is why unread contracts get their own chapter later in this series. Second, **small local deals are the training ground.** A $400-a-month smoothie deal handled well — logged, reported, taxed, delivered on time — teaches every skill a $40,000 deal requires. Treat the small ones seriously and the big ones stop being scary.

#### Mistakes to avoid

- Reading only the dollar amount and skipping what the business gets to *do* with your name and image, and for how long.
- Treating free product as free. It's compensation with a market value.
- Assuming a deal is fine because a teammate did something similar. Rules differ by school, state, and deal.
- Forgetting the deliverables. Unmet obligations are how deals turn into disputes.
- Saying yes fast to avoid awkwardness. Silence for a week beats a signature you regret for a year.

#### Questions to ask before acting

1. Exactly what am I being paid to *do*, by when, and how will "done" be measured?
2. Exactly what can this business do with my name, image, or likeness — and until when?
3. Is this cash, product, or both — and what is the product's fair market value?
4. Has my compliance office confirmed this deal type is allowed for me right now?
5. Who is reviewing this contract before I sign it?

## CHAPTER 3 — Revenue Sharing Is Not NIL

*Revenue-sharing structures are new and still evolving. This chapter is education only; contract decisions call for a qualified attorney.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Two teammates, two mail days. Marcus opened a revenue-sharing agreement from the university: a payment schedule, an amount, and several pages of clauses he skimmed. Deja opened an NIL offer from a regional sporting-goods chain: posts, appearances, a monthly fee.
>
> At dinner, they compared numbers and called it the same thing — "my deal." Then Marcus hit a paragraph about what happens to his payments if he transfers, and another about repayment in certain situations, and realized his "deal" wasn't a sponsorship at all. It was something closer to an employment-shaped contract with the school itself — and nobody in his family had ever seen one before, because until last year, these did not exist.

### Direct pay, defined

Revenue sharing is exactly what it sounds like: schools sharing a slice of their athletic revenue — media rights, tickets, sponsorships — directly with athletes. It became possible when the House settlement took effect on July 1, 2025. No third party. No brand. The school pays you, on a contract, to be part of its program while it uses athlete participation to generate revenue.

This is the single biggest structural change in college sports history, and it is *not* NIL. NIL money comes from outsiders in exchange for marketing work. Revenue-share money comes from the school under a settlement-created system with its own cap, its own paperwork, and its own enforcement.

### How the cap works

Each school that opts in gets an annual **pool limit** — a cap on the total it can share across all athletes. The cap started around $20.5 million per school in the first year and rose to roughly $21.5 million for 2026–27, with scheduled increases over the coming decade.

Three things about the cap that families routinely get wrong:

**It's a school-level ceiling, not a promise to anyone.** The cap is the most a school *may* distribute — not what it must, and not what any athlete is entitled to.

**Allocation is the school's call.** Schools decide how to divide their pool across sports and athletes. At most schools, football and basketball receive the large majority, reflecting where the revenue comes from. Amounts vary enormously by school, sport, and athlete.

**Opting in was optional.** Roughly 82 percent of Division I programs opted in for the first year. Opting in also meant accepting new **roster limits**, which replaced the old scholarship limits — a change that quietly reshaped walk-on and partial-scholarship life. If your school opted out, this lane simply isn't open there.

### The part that matters most: it's a contract

A revenue-sharing agreement is a real contract with the school, and the terms deserve more attention than the number. Reported agreements have included payment schedules, performance and conduct expectations, injury provisions, and — the clauses families most often miss — **transfer and buyout terms**. Some agreements address what happens to future payments if you leave, or whether any money must be repaid in certain situations.

None of this makes revenue sharing bad. It makes it *serious*. You would not sign a lease without reading the early-termination clause; do not sign a rev-share agreement without understanding what leaving costs. And because these agreements are new, even experienced adults around you have limited pattern recognition. That's not a reason to panic. It's a reason to have any agreement reviewed by a qualified attorney before signing — and to route your questions through your school's compliance office, whose job now includes this exact paperwork.

### Nothing here is guaranteed

The cleanest mental model: revenue sharing is opportunity, not entitlement. Amounts can differ between teammates. Offers can change year to year. Schools can structure agreements differently from one another. Appeals related to the settlement were still moving through the courts as this book went to print. Build your budget (Chapter 6) around money that has actually arrived, not money that has been mentioned.

### Anatomy of a rev-share agreement

Agreements differ school to school, but reported deals tend to share a recognizable skeleton. Here's the tour, in plain English — not so you can skip the attorney, but so you can follow along when the attorney talks:

- **Parties and term.** You, the school, and the dates the agreement covers. Check whether it's one year or several, and what renews automatically.
- **Compensation and schedule.** The amount and when it arrives — monthly, semesterly, lump sums. This section drives your entire Chapter 6 budget.
- **What the school gets.** Typically a license to use your name, image, and likeness in school and partner promotion. Read the breadth of it.
- **Expectations and conduct.** Participation requirements, conduct standards, social media clauses — the behavior side of the bargain.
- **Injury and roster provisions.** What happens to payments if you're hurt, redshirted, or your roster status changes.
- **Transfer, buyout, and repayment.** The clauses that make headlines: what leaving costs, what stops, and whether anything must be paid back.

Six sections, one honest hour with a qualified attorney. Against a multi-year, five-figure commitment, that hour is the cheapest protection you will ever buy.

#### What this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Marcus's agreement — the one he almost skimmed — turns out to pay $40,000 over the school year: $4,000 monthly from August through May. Three details change how his family handles it. First, **no withholding**: each $4,000 arrives gross, so his set-aside habit (Chapter 8) must fire ten times a year, automatically, or April becomes an ambush. Second, **the schedule creates a summer desert**: nothing arrives June or July, so his four-bucket budget has to stretch ten paychecks across twelve months. Third, **the transfer clause** ties any mid-year departure to forfeiting remaining payments — which doesn't make transferring wrong, but makes its true cost a number instead of a surprise.

None of this appeared in the group-chat version of his deal, which was just "Marcus is getting forty bands." The contract version — schedule, gross checks, desert months, exit price — is the one his actual life has to live inside.

> ❝ *Revenue sharing is opportunity, not entitlement.*

> **Ask your attorney:** walk me through the injury, transfer, and repayment provisions as stories — "if this happens, then what?" — until I can retell each one without notes.

> **Family Conversation** — Before anyone signs anything: what would our family need to understand about the exit clauses to feel calm, not just excited? Write the questions down; bring them to the review.

#### Before you sign — the standing checklist

For rev-share agreements and any contract with real money or long rights attached:

- [ ] I've read every page myself, slowly, once through with a highlighter
- [ ] A qualified attorney has reviewed it and walked me through the exit, injury, and repayment stories
- [ ] Compliance has confirmed anything requiring confirmation
- [ ] I can explain the deal to a family member in two minutes without notes
- [ ] The payment schedule is mapped into my budget — including the months it *doesn't* pay
- [ ] Nobody involved is rushing me; the 48-hour rule has run its course

#### What families should understand

If your athlete is offered a revenue-sharing agreement, you are effectively looking at the family's first major employment-adjacent contract — often before the athlete can legally rent a car. Slow the moment down. Ask for time to review; legitimate offers survive review. Get attorney eyes on transfer, buyout, injury, and repayment language specifically. And understand the tax picture early: schools generally issue tax forms for these payments but typically do **not** withhold taxes the way a regular employer would — which means the tax routine in Chapters 7 and 8 applies to this lane too. A large check with no withholding is a tax bill wearing a disguise.

#### Mistakes to avoid

- Calling rev-share money "my NIL." Different source, different rules, different paperwork.
- Reading the amount and skimming the clauses. The clauses are the contract.
- Assuming the school withholds your taxes. Generally, it doesn't.
- Treating a verbal mention of a number as money. Until it's signed and scheduled, it's a conversation.

## CHAPTER 4 — The Rules Around the Money

*Rules in this chapter are current as of July 2026 and change frequently. Your compliance office is the first phone call for anything specific.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> The deal was small — $750 from a barbershop chain for three posts — so Amara almost didn't mention it to anyone. A teammate happened to ask if she'd run it through the reporting app. What app? By the end of the week Amara had learned three things: deals over a certain amount had to be reported, hers cleared review without drama, and the teammate who asked the question had just quietly protected her eligibility.
>
> Nothing about Amara's deal was shady. That's the point. The rules aren't only for catching bad deals. They're the paperwork that makes good deals safe.
>
> What stayed with Amara wasn't the near-miss; it was how random her rescue had been. Her financial safety had depended on which teammate happened to sit next to her at lunch. She decided that was a terrible system, spent one evening actually learning the rule layers that applied to her, and became — by accident, then on purpose — the teammate who asks other people the question.

### The four layers of rules

Athlete money sits under four overlapping rulebooks. You don't need to memorize them. You need to know they exist, roughly what each covers, and who to ask.

**Layer 1 — The NCAA and the College Sports Commission.** The House settlement created a new enforcement body, the **College Sports Commission (CSC)**, which oversees revenue-share compliance and third-party NIL deals at opted-in schools. Its clearinghouse system is called **NIL Go** — and it produces the single most practical rule in this chapter: **third-party NIL deals totaling $600 or more must be reported through NIL Go**, generally within about five business days of signing. Report first, then perform the deal; retroactive honesty is a much harder conversation.

**Layer 2 — State law.** States passed their own NIL laws, and they differ. Florida's framework, for instance, shapes what schools and athletes in this state can do. If you transfer across state lines, the rulebook under your feet changes.

**Layer 3 — School policy.** Your school can layer its own requirements on top: disclosure procedures, category restrictions (many schools bar deals involving certain products), and brand-conflict rules. Two athletes at different schools can face different answers to the same deal.

**Layer 4 — Federal activity, unfinished.** As of this writing, there is **no federal NIL law**. A major House bill was pulled before a vote in late 2025; a bipartisan Senate bill was in hearings in mid-2026; and a 2026 executive order directed changes at large athletic programs starting August 2026. Translation for your household: the top layer of the rulebook is still being written. Date-stamp what you know, and re-verify yearly.

### Three phrases worth knowing in plain English

The enforcement system uses vocabulary that sounds intimidating and isn't.

**Associated entity.** A business or organization closely tied to a school — most importantly, significant boosters and booster-funded collectives. Deals from associated entities get extra review, because that's historically where pay-for-play hid.

**Valid business purpose.** The review question for those deals: is this business paying you for something a real business actually wants — content, promotion, appearances — or is the "deal" just a donation with your name on it? Real deliverables, real marketing goals, real answer.

**Range of compensation.** The second review question: is the pay roughly in line with what similar deals pay? A wildly above-market number from a booster-connected entity draws scrutiny; a market-rate deal with genuine work behind it clears.

The system has teeth — it has rejected deals, and its decisions have been upheld in arbitration — and it is also, day to day, mostly boring: tens of thousands of deals have moved through review. Boring is what you want.

### Locker-room myths vs. how it works

| The myth you'll hear | How it actually works |
|---|---|
| "Small deals don't need reporting" | The $600 threshold is aggregate per source — and small deals build the aggregate |
| "It's my money, the school can't ask" | Disclosure duties are conditions of eligibility, not favors |
| "Collectives found a loophole" | Collective payments are taxable and booster-connected deals get *extra* review |
| "There's a federal law now" | Not as of this writing — bills and orders are moving, nothing settled |
| "My cousin's state allows it, so mine does" | State laws and school policies differ; only *your* rulebook governs you |

### If you're in high school, or transferring in

Two rules reach backward in time. First, athletes entering Division I — from high school, junior college, or the transfer portal — must disclose qualifying NIL deals ($600+) from *before* enrollment. Keep records now; Chapter 5's deal log covers you. Second, **high school NIL runs on state association rules**, which are stricter. In Florida, the FHSAA allows high school NIL but requires deals to stay fully independent of your school — no school uniforms, logos, or facilities in your deal content, no deals arranged as recruiting inducements, and several restricted product categories. Violations can cost eligibility. Other states differ; check yours.

One more caution that belongs in bold for the families it affects: **international student-athletes face visa restrictions on NIL work.** If you are in the U.S. on a student visa, do not sign anything before speaking with your school's international student office and an immigration attorney. This is a place where a small deal can create a very large problem.

#### What this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Follow one deal through the machinery. A booster-owned car dealership — a classic **associated entity** — offers a starting linebacker $1,500 for two social posts and a Saturday appearance. Because the payer is an associated entity and the total clears $600, the deal goes into NIL Go promptly after signing, where two questions get asked. **Valid business purpose:** is the dealership buying real marketing — posts that run, an appearance that happens, content it can use — or writing a donation with a player's name on it? Here, the deliverables are real and the dealership genuinely advertises to that audience: yes. **Range of compensation:** is $1,500 roughly what comparable deals pay for comparable work? For a starter at that program with that following, it sits inside the band: yes. Cleared, performed, paid, logged.

Now run the failure version: same dealership offers $25,000 for one story post. Real business, real athlete — but the number is wildly outside the range for the work, which is precisely the pattern the review exists to catch, because an inflated "deal" from a booster entity is how pay-for-play dresses up as marketing. That deal gets stopped or sent back for restructuring. Notice what protected the first athlete and flagged the second: not luck — proportion, paper, and process. The system has rejected deals and been upheld when challenged. It is not decoration.

### The yearly rule refresh

Because the top layer is still being written, adopt one calendar ritual: **every summer, before the season, re-verify your rulebook.** Thirty minutes, four checks — your school's current policy, your state's law (or your state association's rules if you're in high school), the clearinghouse thresholds, and any federal change that actually took effect rather than just trended. Put it next to your physical on the calendar. Rules, like bodies, get an annual.

> ❝ *Reporting is protection. A cleared deal is a deal nobody can use against you.*

> **Family Conversation** — Who in this family owns the summer rule refresh, and where do we write down what changed? Assign it by name. "Somebody should check" is how nobody checks.

#### What families should understand

Compliance can feel adversarial — like the rules exist to catch your kid. Flip the frame: **reporting is protection.** A deal that's been disclosed and cleared is a deal nobody can later use against your athlete's eligibility. Build the family habit of treating the compliance office like a resource you use early, not an authority you hope to avoid. The question "should we run this by compliance?" should be as automatic as "did you finish your homework?" — and considerably less annoying, because the answer protects real money.

#### Mistakes to avoid

- Skipping the report because the deal is small or "obviously fine." The threshold is the threshold.
- Performing the deal first and reporting later.
- Assuming another state's (or another school's) rules match yours.
- Using school marks, uniforms, or facilities in high school deal content.
- Signing anything as an international athlete before getting visa guidance.

#### Questions to ask before acting

1. Does this deal cross the $600 reporting threshold — on its own or combined with others from the same source?
2. Is the payer an associated entity, and does the deal have real deliverables at a market rate?
3. What does my school's policy add on top — restricted categories, disclosure steps, brand conflicts?
4. If I'm in high school: does this comply with my state association's rules on independence from my school?
5. What changed in the rules since I last checked?

> **DUCAT WEALTH PRINCIPLE** — *If you can't explain the deal, you're not ready to sign it.*

#### Chapter checklist

- [ ] I can name the four rule layers and who to ask about each
- [ ] I know the $600 NIL Go reporting duty and its timing
- [ ] I can explain "associated entity," "valid business purpose," and "range of compensation" in my own words
- [ ] I know the pre-enrollment disclosure rule and (if applicable) my state's high school rules
- [ ] If international: I know not to sign before visa guidance

## CHAPTER 5 — Before the First Dollar: Build the System

*Banking products differ. This chapter describes an organizational framework, not specific accounts or institutions. Ducat Private Wealth is not a bank.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Devon's first NIL payment — $1,100 from a camp appearance — landed in the same checking account he'd had since ninth grade, mixed in with birthday money and his Chick-fil-A paychecks. Eight months later, his family's tax preparer asked a simple question: how much of the year's income was NIL? Devon spent a weekend scrolling through statements, guessing at which Zelle deposits were which, and reconstructing a year of his life from screenshots.
>
> His teammate Priya got her first payment the same month. Hers landed in an account labeled *Priya — NIL Income*, was logged in a spreadsheet the same day, and moved automatically into three buckets. Tax season took her twenty minutes.
>
> Devon asked her about it in the training room, half joking, half not: "Who taught you all that?" Nobody had, really — her aunt had once lost a small business to messy books, and the family had made organization a religion afterward. "It's not a money thing," Priya told him. "It's a Tuesday-afternoon thing. You just have to do the Tuesday before the money shows up instead of after." The difference between them wasn't money or intelligence. It was one afternoon, placed correctly in time.

### The one-afternoon setup

Everything in this chapter can be done in a single afternoon, most of it from a phone, and none of it requires having earned a dollar yet. That's the point — this is the *before* chapter.

**The three-account structure.** Open (or designate) three accounts and give them jobs:

1. **Income account.** Every athlete dollar — NIL, rev share, camp money — lands here and *only* here. Nothing else deposits into it. This single rule creates a clean record of athlete income that will make taxes, reporting, and any future review painless.
2. **Tax set-aside account.** A savings account where a fixed percentage of every deposit moves immediately (Chapter 8 covers the percentage conversation). This money is spoken for. It is not yours; you're just holding it for April.
3. **Personal spending account.** Where your actual spendable money lives, transferred deliberately from the income account after taxes and savings take their cut.

Use federally insured institutions — banks (FDIC) or credit unions (NCUA) — and turn on every alert they offer. Beyond insurance, evaluate like a business owner: no-fee or low-fee accounts (fees are a tax on disorganization you don't owe), instant transfer between your three accounts, sub-account or "bucket" features that make the Chapter 6 system automatic, and a real fraud line a human answers. Flashy apps aimed at athletes come and go; boring, insured, and automated wins this contest every time. If you're under 18, some accounts will require an adult co-owner; that's normal, and it makes the next section matter more.

### Who has access — and why this is a bigger question than it looks

Every account has an access list: owners, co-owners, people with the login. Decide this on purpose. A minor athlete typically needs a parent or guardian on the account — fine. But "on the account" should be a *decision*, not a default, and the family should say out loud what it means: who can move money, who monitors, who gets alerts. Chapter 10 handles the harder conversations underneath this; for now, the rule is simple: **you should always be able to see your own money, and nobody should be able to move it without your knowledge.** Sharing your login with teammates, girlfriends, boyfriends, or "managers" is how athletes end up surprised by their own balance.

### The deal log: your business's memory

Start a deal log the same afternoon — a simple spreadsheet works. One row per deal, with columns for: payer · what I'm doing (deliverables) · what they get (rights, and until when) · cash amount · non-cash value · dates signed/reported/performed/paid · reported to NIL Go? · contract file location.

Then make a folder — cloud storage is fine — and drop every contract, every W-9 you fill out, every payment confirmation, every screenshot of a completed deliverable into it. When a business asks you to complete a **Form W-9** before paying you, that's normal; it's how they collect your tax info to issue year-end forms. It's also your cue that the payment is going on record — which is exactly why your own records need to match.

Six months from now, this log answers every hard question in your athlete life: what you earned, what you owe, what you promised, what rights you gave away, and what you reported. Businesses keep books. You're a business now.

#### What this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Here's one filled row from a deal log, and the three future problems it quietly solves.

| Payer | Deliverables | Rights & until | Cash | Non-cash (FMV) | Signed / Reported / Paid | NIL Go? | File |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bayline Grill (local) | 4 IG posts, 1 event (10/12) | Repost my content thru 6/30/27 | $1,200 | Meals $300 | 9/2 / 9/5 / 11/1 | Yes — cleared 9/8 | /Deals/Bayline |

**Problem one, February:** the tax preparer asks for total athlete income and any non-cash compensation. Answer: read the columns — $1,200 cash, $300 in meals at fair market value, done in seconds instead of a weekend of screenshot archaeology. **Problem two, whenever:** compliance asks for confirmation that the deal was disclosed. Answer: reported 9/5, cleared 9/8, file attached. **Problem three, next fall:** the aid office needs income figures for the FAFSA. Answer: same row, same seconds. One minute of logging per deal buys back hours of reconstruction and — more valuable — removes the fear from every official question you'll ever be asked. People with records answer questions. People without records get investigated by their own bank statements.

### The money map

Once the accounts exist, write the one-page **money map**: every account, its institution, its job, who's on it, and what's automated — kept where you and your inner circle can find it. This is the page that turns a crisis (lost phone, fraud alert, a parent needing to act fast) from panic into a checklist. Update it whenever anything changes; review it at the annual family conversation.

> ❝ *People with records answer questions. People without records get interrogated by their own bank statements.*

> **Family Conversation** — Build the money map together in fifteen minutes, then answer one question honestly: if the athlete's phone disappeared today, could this family reconstruct the system from what's written down? If not, the map isn't done.

#### Before the money hits — the standing checklist

Run this list the day any new income source appears (first deal, rev-share agreement, camp season), before the first dollar arrives:

Before restructuring anything for aid reasons, talk to a qualified professional; well-meant moves made blind can backfire.

## CHAPTER 6 — Budgeting Money That Doesn't Arrive on Schedule

*Percentages in this chapter are illustrative frameworks for education, not recommendations. Your numbers depend on your facts.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Zoe's NIL year looked like this: $2,400 in September, nothing in October, $300 in November, $5,000 in December when a holiday campaign hit, then a four-month desert until camp season. Her cousin, who worked a salaried job, kept giving her budgeting advice built for paychecks that arrive every two weeks like sunrise. It kept not working.
>
> The December $5,000 was the dangerous month. It felt like wealth. It was actually four months of runway plus a tax bill wearing a bow. The version of Zoe who understood that finished the spring calm. The version who didn't would have spent March asking her mom to cover gas.
>
> Here's the detail that made the difference: Zoe never made a single monthly budget all year. She made one *decision* — what happens to a dollar the moment it arrives — and let twelve unpredictable months run through it. Her cousin kept asking how she stayed disciplined. She kept explaining that she didn't. The system was disciplined. She was just a person who had set it up on a quiet afternoon in August.

### Why normal budgets break here

Almost all budgeting advice assumes a salary: same amount, same schedule. Athlete income is the opposite — **lumpy**. Big months, zero months, seasonal surges, one-time windfalls. A fixed monthly budget breaks against lumpy income in both directions: in big months it whispers *you have extra*, and in zero months it accuses you of failure you didn't commit.

The fix is to stop budgeting the month and start budgeting **the dollar**. Every dollar that arrives gets split by percentage the day it lands, no matter when it lands or how large it is. Lumpy income, smooth system.

### Know your income's season

Before setting percentages, sketch your income's *shape*. Athlete money is not just lumpy — it's lumpy on a schedule that follows your sport. Fall-sport athletes often see deals cluster August through December, then thin out. Basketball money leans into winter and tournament season. Spring sports, camp instructors, and content creators each have their own tide chart. Rev-share agreements add their own rhythm — often academic-year payments with a summer desert.

None of the shapes is better; all of them are plannable. Take fifteen minutes and mark your likely high months, likely zero months, and any scheduled payments on one calendar page. That single page turns "my income is unpredictable" — which feels unmanageable — into "my income is seasonal," which is just farming. Farmers have been budgeting lumpy income for ten thousand years. The technology is buckets.

### The four buckets

When money hits your income account, it splits four ways, in this order:

**1. Taxes — first, always.** A fixed percentage moves to the tax set-aside account before anything else happens. This is non-negotiable and covered fully in Chapter 8. Taxes come first because they're the only bucket with a federal agency attached.

**2. Saving.** Your future options fund, beginning with the **emergency reserve**: money whose entire job is absorbing surprises — the injury, the dead laptop, the flight home, the deal that pays sixty days late. A common educational rule of thumb is a reserve covering three to six months of your essential expenses, built gradually. It sits in savings, boring on purpose. An emergency reserve is what makes every other financial decision calmer, because it removes the panic from surprise.

**3. Living.** The essentials your athlete income actually covers — your share of rent, food, phone, transportation. For many college athletes with scholarships covering the basics, this bucket is small. Keep it honest.

**4. Flexible.** Guilt-free spending — clothes, games, dates, generosity. Yes, on purpose. A budget with no room to be young gets abandoned by Thanksgiving. The flexible bucket isn't a leak in the system; it *is* the system, doing its job of making the other buckets sustainable.

What are the right percentages? That's a conversation, not a commandment — it depends on your tax situation, your scholarship coverage, and your goals. The worksheet in the back of this book walks you and your family through setting your starting split, and your tax percentage specifically should be sanity-checked with a tax professional once real money is moving.

### Windfall protocol

One-time large payments — the December $5,000, a signing payment, a settlement check — get their own rule: **split first, sit second.** Run the percentage split the day it arrives, then let the flexible portion sit for two weeks before major purchases. Windfalls distort judgment on a schedule: day one is euphoria, day three is a shopping cart, day fourteen is clarity. Buy at day fourteen.

#### What this means in real life

*Hypothetical, with illustrative percentages — not a recommendation.* Give Zoe a starting split of **25% taxes / 20% saving / 35% living / 20% flexible**, chosen with her family and sanity-checked with a tax professional, and rerun her lumpy year:

- **September, $2,400 arrives** → $600 tax · $480 saving · $840 living · $480 flexible.
- **October, $0 arrives** → nothing splits; living expenses draw on what September's living bucket and her reserve were built for. A zero month is now an event the system planned for, not a failure.
- **December, $5,000 arrives** → $1,250 tax · $1,000 saving · $1,750 living (which is exactly the runway that carries the spring desert) · $1,000 flexible — and the flexible thousand sits for fourteen days before anything major.

Across the year she banked ~$1,900 for taxes before ever being asked, built savings on autopilot, and turned the dangerous December high into four months of calm. Same income as the panicked version of her year. Different dollar-level operating system.

Reserve math is just as concrete. Suppose Zoe's honest essential costs — her share of rent, food, phone, gas — run $1,200 a month. A three-to-six-month reserve target is **$3,600 to $7,200**. At $480–$1,000 of saving in her good months, that's a one-to-two-season project: entirely real, entirely boring, and the single number that most changes how she sleeps.

> ❝ *A big month isn't a raise. It's runway.*

> **Ask your tax professional:** given my projected income, my state, and my family's return, what set-aside percentage should replace my placeholder — and when should we revisit it?

> **Family Conversation** — Everyone writes down what they think the athlete's essential monthly number is; then build the real list together. The gap between the guesses and the list is this family's financial honesty gap — close it here, where it's cheap.

#### What families should understand

Your athlete's broke months are not evidence of failure, and their big months are not evidence of wealth — with lumpy income, only the *year* tells the truth. Help by asking annual questions ("how's the year tracking?") instead of monthly ones ("why is your account low?"). And if the family relies on some of the athlete's income — a real situation in many households, handled with care in Chapter 10 — build that as a named percentage inside the system, not as unpredictable requests against it. A plan survives what pressure destroys.

#### Mistakes to avoid

- Budgeting monthly amounts instead of percentage splits.
- Treating a big month as a raise. It's runway.
- Building a budget with zero flexible spending, then abandoning it entirely.
- Letting the emergency reserve wait until "after I'm earning more."
- Making the tax split last, or making it optional.

#### Questions to ask before acting

1. What are my four starting percentages, and who helped me sanity-check the tax number?
2. How many months of essentials is my reserve target, and what counts as "essential" honestly?
3. What's my windfall protocol, in writing, before the windfall?
4. If income stopped for four months today, what would carry me?
5. What does my family actually expect from my income — and is it a percentage or a pressure?

- [ ] Split automated (or calendared) for the day money lands
- [ ] Emergency reserve target set and first transfer made
- [ ] Windfall protocol written down

1. What did your last "big money month" (any size) actually get spent on?

## CHAPTER 7 — Taxes, Part One: What the IRS Sees

*This chapter explains general tax concepts for education only. Your outcome depends on your facts — work with a qualified tax professional.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Nobody told Tristan he had a business. He'd made $6,800 across the year — a collective payment, two local sponsorships, some camp money — and in his mind he was a college sophomore with a nice side income. Then February arrived carrying two 1099 forms and a group-chat rumor that "you don't owe taxes under ten grand," which a teammate swore was true and which was not true at all.
>
> The tax preparer his mom found explained it in one sentence that reorganized Tristan's whole understanding: *the IRS doesn't see a student-athlete; it sees a self-employed small business owner — and it's been seeing one all year.*
>
> On the drive home his mom was quiet, then said the honest part out loud: "I didn't know either. Nobody in this family has ever been self-employed." That sentence is worth pausing on, because it describes most athlete households in this era — full of smart, hardworking people whose entire tax experience is W-2 jobs where the math happens automatically, invisibly, to someone else. Nothing about self-employment taxes is harder than what they already do. It's just *manual*, and nobody handed them the manual. These two chapters are the manual.

### You're probably self-employed

Here is the central fact of athlete taxes, the one that explains everything else: **NIL income is generally independent-contractor income, not employee wages.** The brands, collectives, and businesses paying you are not your employers. Nobody withholds taxes from your checks. Nobody sends your share to the IRS during the year. In the government's eyes, you're running a small business — call it *You, Inc.* — and businesses handle their own taxes.

Revenue-share payments from schools have generally been treated the same way — reported to you on tax forms, but paid **without withholding**. (This is a young system and treatment may evolve; it's a specific question for your tax professional each year.)

This one fact re-labels everything: the money hitting your account is **gross**, not yours-to-spend. Some slice of nearly every check is already owed. Chapter 8 builds the routine that handles it. This chapter makes sure nothing on a tax form ever surprises you again.

### The forms, decoded

**W-2** — the employee form, from jobs with withholding (your campus job, your Chick-fil-A summers). If your athlete income shows up here, you're an employee for that payer; that's the exception, not the rule.

**1099-NEC** — the independent-contractor form. Businesses that pay you for services report it here. Starting with tax year 2026, a business must send this form when it pays you **$2,000 or more** in the year. Read the next sentence twice: **that threshold decides whether you get a form, not whether you owe tax.** Income below $2,000 from a payer is still taxable income. The form is paperwork; the tax exists either way.

**1099-K** — from payment platforms and apps, at much higher volume thresholds. If deal money flows through payment apps, this form can appear too — one more reason your own deal log, not a pile of forms, is your source of truth.

**1099-MISC** — a catch-all you may see for certain payments, including some royalty-type income.

### Four surprises to defuse now

**Non-cash compensation is taxable.** The free gear, the free meals-for-a-year, the courtesy vehicle — compensation you receive for NIL work is generally income at fair market value, whether or not any form ever arrives. Log it with a dollar value.

**Collective money is taxable.** Some collectives were organized as nonprofits, and some athletes concluded payments from them were tax-free gifts. The IRS has said plainly otherwise: payments from collectives for NIL activities are taxable compensation.

**Self-employment tax exists, and it starts low.** Beyond income tax, self-employed people pay **self-employment tax** — the self-funded version of Social Security and Medicare, roughly 15.3% on net self-employment earnings — and it kicks in once net earnings reach just **$400** for the year. This is the tax that ambushes athletes, because it applies even in years when income is too low to owe much income tax.

**Scholarships can have a taxable slice.** Amounts covering tuition, required fees, and required course materials are generally tax-free for degree candidates — but amounts covering room, board, and certain stipends can be taxable. If your aid package is generous, this belongs on your tax professional's desk.

One more note for the whole household: the IRS has publicly identified NIL income as an area it's watching closely. This is not a corner of the tax world where casual works. The good news is that none of it is hard once it's a routine — and the routine is the entire next chapter.

#### What this means in real life

*Hypothetical, simplified for education — real numbers belong with a professional.* Decompose Tristan's $6,800 year and watch the paperwork sort itself:

- **$3,000 from the collective** → taxable compensation (never a "gift," whatever the collective's structure), reported to him on a **1099-NEC** because it clears the $2,000 form threshold.
- **$2,600 from one local sponsorship** → also a **1099-NEC**.
- **$800 in camp appearance money, paid by a camp that never crossed $2,000** → *no form arrives.* Still income. Still goes on the return, straight from his deal log.
- **$400 in free gear** for posts → *no form, no cash.* Still income at fair market value, logged the day it shipped.

So Tristan's return reflects $6,800 of self-employment income, while his mailbox only ever showed $5,600 of it — which is exactly why the deal log, not the stack of forms, is the source of truth. From there, self-employment tax applies to his net earnings (income minus any legitimate, documented business expenses) at roughly 15.3% — on the order of a thousand dollars in his ballpark, illustratively, *before* any income tax — and it applies even in a year when his income tax alone would have been small. That's the ambush this chapter exists to spring in print instead of in April: not the tax anyone expected, arriving on the income everyone treated casually.

One more household layer: if Tristan is still his parents' dependent, his filing interacts with theirs — dependency status, education credits, and, for some kinds of income, kiddie-tax rules that can tax a dependent's income at the parents' rate. Which return claims what is a fifteen-minute professional question and a season-long family argument. Choose the fifteen minutes.

> ❝ *The checks arrive with the taxes still inside them.*

> **Ask your CPA:** which of my income streams are self-employment income, what's my realistic combined federal, state, and self-employment picture — and does my dependency status change any of it?

> **Family Conversation** — Parents: share (at whatever level of detail feels right) how your own taxes work — withholding, filing, refunds. Athletes: explain gross-versus-net back. Two generations, one vocabulary, before the forms arrive.

#### What families should understand

If your athlete is your dependent, their income creates *family* tax questions: whether they still qualify as your dependent, how their return and yours interact, and how certain kinds of a minor's income are taxed — sometimes at the parents' rate under the so-called kiddie-tax rules. Bring both generations' facts to one tax professional rather than solving the returns separately. And keep this chapter's biggest sentence pinned to the refrigerator: *the checks arrive with the taxes still inside them.*

#### Mistakes to avoid

- Believing "no form = no tax." The $2,000 form threshold changes paperwork, not what you owe.
- Ignoring non-cash compensation because no money moved.
- Treating collective payments as gifts.
- Forgetting self-employment tax — the one that starts at $400 of net earnings.
- Getting tax "facts" from group chats. The rumor is free; the penalty isn't.

#### Questions to ask before acting

1. Which of my income streams are self-employment income, and which (if any) are wages?
2. What non-cash compensation did I receive this year, and what's its fair market value?
3. Will my school withhold anything from revenue-share payments, and what forms will it issue?
4. Am I still my parents' dependent, and how do our returns interact?
5. When does a tax professional join my team — and why not now?

> **DUCAT WEALTH PRINCIPLE** — *Taxes are only a crisis for people who treat them like a surprise.*

- [ ] Non-cash compensation is in my deal log with dollar values
- [ ] I understand self-employment tax and its $400 trigger
- [ ] My family has discussed the dependent question

## CHAPTER 8 — Taxes, Part Two: The Athlete's Tax Routine

*This chapter explains general tax concepts for education only. Percentages and dates are illustrative; your obligations depend on your facts. Work with a qualified tax professional.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Same amount of income, two different Aprils. Kayla earned about $14,000 in deals last year; so did her friend Bri at a rival school. Kayla's April: her tax account already held the money her CPA had helped her set aside all year, her quarterly payments were in, and filing was an errand between practices. Bri's April: a five-figure combined tax number, an empty account where the set-aside should have been, a penalty for missing estimated payments she'd never heard of, and a payment plan that followed her into summer.
>
> Nothing separated them except a routine — four habits, none of them complicated, all of them boring, every one of them worth more than the smartest investment tip either of them would hear all year.
>
> The unfair part, Bri would tell you, is that she *worked harder* on her money than Kayla did. She researched investments, followed finance creators, could explain three kinds of crypto. She'd simply spent all that effort on the interesting layer while the boring layer — the one with a federal agency attached — sat unattended. Athlete taxes don't reward the most knowledgeable. They reward the most routine.

### Habit 1 — The set-aside, every check, no exceptions

The day money lands, a fixed percentage moves to the tax set-aside account. Automatic if your bank allows it; a calendar ritual if not. What percentage? It genuinely depends — on your total income, your state, your family's tax picture, whether you're a dependent. Families and tax professionals often discuss starting figures in the 20–30% range for self-employment income as a *conservative placeholder*, then tune it once a professional runs your real numbers. The precise figure matters less than the iron rule: **the split happens before the money feels like yours.** Over-saving for taxes produces a refund-like bonus in April. Under-saving produces Bri's April.

### Habit 2 — Quarterlies: the four-date calendar

Employees pay taxes all year through withholding. Self-employed people are expected to do the same thing manually, through **quarterly estimated tax payments** — four payments across the year, generally due in mid-April, mid-June, mid-September, and mid-January. Miss them while owing enough, and penalties can apply *even if you pay everything at filing time*, because the system wants its money as you earn, not in one spring apology.

Whether you owe quarterlies, and how much, is exactly the kind of question a tax professional answers in one short conversation. Your jobs are simpler: put the four dates in your phone with alerts, keep the set-aside account funded so the payments are painless, and never let a due date be news.

### Habit 3 — Expenses, documented like you mean it

Businesses deduct **ordinary and necessary** business expenses, and *You, Inc.* may have some: content equipment, deal-related travel, camp supplies, professional fees. Two disciplines make this real instead of risky. First, **pay business expenses from the income account**, keeping the trail clean. Second, **document everything** — receipt, date, deal it connects to — in the same folder as your contracts. What counts as deductible in your situation is a professional's call, and gray areas (the car, the wardrobe, the phone) are famous for a reason. Your job isn't to know the answer; it's to arrive at the professional's desk with records so complete the answer is easy.

### Habit 4 — The annual sit-down

Once a year — ideally *before* year-end, when there's still time to act — sit down with a qualified tax professional. Bring the deal log, the forms, the expense folder, and your parents' basic situation if you're a dependent. This is also where the complications that outgrow this book get handled properly:

- **Multi-state income.** Earn money for work performed in other states — an appearance here, a campaign there — and those states may want a return. Athletes travel; athlete income does too.
- **The financial-aid interaction.** NIL and rev-share income must be reported on the **FAFSA** and can affect need-based aid, including Pell Grants. For some families, a deal's *aid* cost can rival its tax cost — run both numbers before relying on either.
- **The family return.** Dependency, education credits, and kiddie-tax questions from Chapter 7, solved with both generations' facts on one desk.

When does hiring a CPA make sense? An honest rule of thumb: the year athlete income becomes real money to your family — or the moment forms, states, or aid questions multiply — the fee stops being an expense and starts being insurance. Interview them with the money-team questions in Chapter 12.

#### What this means in real life

*Hypothetical, illustrative only.* Here is Kayla's tax year as a calendar instead of a concept. She projects roughly $14,000 of deal income; her tax professional helps her set a 25% set-aside and confirms she should make quarterly payments.

- **Winter (Jan–Mar):** $4,000 arrives across two deals; $1,000 moves to the tax account on arrival. Mid-April, she files last year's return *and* makes this year's first estimated payment — from an account that already holds the money.
- **Spring (Apr–May):** $2,000 arrives; $500 set aside. Mid-June, second payment. Painless.
- **Summer:** camp season — $5,000 across three checks; $1,250 set aside. Mid-September, third payment.
- **Fall:** the desert. $3,000 in November; $750 set aside. Mid-January, fourth payment closes the year.

Four dates, four transfers, zero drama — and notice the zero-income stretches didn't break anything, because the payments draw on the set-aside account, not on whatever's in checking that week. Now add one wrinkle worth seeing early: in July, Kayla did a paid appearance in another state. Florida has no state income tax, but the state where she *performed the work* may tax that income and expect a return — small dollars, real obligation, exactly the kind of thing her preparer handles in minutes because the deal log shows where every dollar was earned.

And the FAFSA wrinkle, made concrete: aid formulas look back at income from a prior year. Money Kayla earns *this* year shows up in an aid calculation about two years down the road — so a strong deal year as a sophomore can echo into her senior-year package. Not a reason to earn less; a reason to see the whole board with a professional before assuming the aid letter stays still.

> ❝ *Four boring habits beat one brilliant tip, every single year.*

> **Ask your CPA:** do I owe quarterlies this year, in which states, and what should each payment be based on my actual projections — and will you recalculate with me mid-year if the projections change?

> **Family Conversation** — Put the four quarterly dates in the *family* calendar, not just the athlete's. The job isn't paying — that's the athlete's — the job is making sure a due date is never a surprise in this house.

#### What families should understand

Your athlete has homework, practice, film, travel, and class. The tax routine survives that schedule only if it's *systematized* — automatic transfers, phone alerts, one shared folder — and only if a parent plays calendar-keeper without becoming bookkeeper-in-chief. Also, put the FAFSA interaction on your radar early: aid formulas look back at income from prior years, so this year's deals can echo into future aid packages. That's a planning conversation with a professional, not a reason to fear income — just a reason to see the whole board.

#### Mistakes to avoid

- Setting aside taxes "when the big check comes." The habit only works as *every check*.
- Learning about quarterlies from a penalty notice.
- Deducting aggressively on vibes, or deducting nothing out of fear — both are records problems in disguise.
- Forgetting the states you performed work in.
- Discovering the FAFSA effect after the aid letter changes.

#### Questions to ask before acting

1. What set-aside percentage fits my actual projected income and state — says a professional, not a group chat?
2. Do I owe quarterly estimates this year, and are all four dates in my phone?
3. Which of my expenses are legitimately deductible, and is my documentation complete?
4. Did I earn money for work performed in other states this year?
5. How will this year's income affect my FAFSA and any need-based aid?

#### Chapter checklist

- [ ] Set-aside percentage chosen, confirmed with a professional, automated
- [ ] Four quarterly dates in my calendar with alerts
- [ ] Expenses paid from the income account and documented
- [ ] Annual tax sit-down scheduled before year-end
- [ ] FAFSA/aid interaction reviewed with a professional if aid applies to me

1. Which of the four habits would break down first in your real life — and what would prevent that?
2. What would a completely calm April feel like, specifically?

## CHAPTER 9 — Lifestyle Creep and the Pressure to Look the Part

> **Fictional Example**
>
> The first thing DeAndre bought was sneakers, and honestly, fine — he'd earned it. The problem arrived quietly across the next six months. New teammates with bigger deals. Instagram full of athletes his age with cars and chains. A cousin's comment at a cookout: "All that NIL money and you still driving *that*?" Each purchase after the sneakers wasn't really for him. The designer bag for his girlfriend, the rims, the bottle-service birthday — each one was an answer to a question someone else had asked.
>
> At the end of the year, his deal log said he'd earned more than 80 percent of his teammates. His account balance said he'd kept less than almost any of them. Nobody had scammed him. He'd simply been spending someone else's opinion of his life.

### The creep, explained

**Lifestyle creep** is the near-universal pattern where spending rises to meet income — automatically, invisibly, and permanently. Earn more, spend more, feel exactly as broke as before. It happens to surgeons and software engineers. It happens *faster* to athletes, for reasons worth naming out loud:

**Your money is public.** Deals are announced. Rankings are published. Strangers have opinions about your balance sheet. Most twenty-year-olds get to be broke in private; you don't get to be careful in private.

**Your peer group escalates.** Locker rooms compress the normal range of spending into one room. Somebody's always got the newer car, and the room recalibrates around it.

**Your window is short.** The professional earning years most athletes are unconsciously imitating — the pros on their feeds — are themselves famously brief. College earning years are briefer still. Spending like the money is permanent is the one mistake this book most wants to un-teach, and it gets a full chapter in Book Three.

### Looking wealthy vs. building options

Here is the reframe that does the most work: every dollar can buy one of two things — **appearance** or **options**.

Appearance is the car, the fits, the table at the club. It buys a real thing: how this season looks. Options are the funded emergency reserve, the untouched savings, the tax account that's never short. They buy a different thing: the ability to say no — to a bad deal, a bad situation, a bad year — and the ability to say yes when something worth saying yes to shows up.

Nobody photographs options. That's the whole problem, and the whole opportunity: while everyone in your feed performs wealth, you can quietly accumulate the thing wealth is actually *for*.

### The comparison diet

One practical addition to the speed limit: manage the inputs, not just the outputs. Comparison is the engine of creep, and your feed is a comparison machine running twenty-four hours a day — showing you the best purchase of everyone you follow, on their best day, with the financing cropped out. You cannot out-discipline an environment you soak in nightly.

So edit the environment. Mute the accounts that reliably make you want things you didn't want an hour earlier — you know exactly which ones. Follow a few builders: athletes and others whose content is about what they're *keeping* and constructing. And run one honest audit: after twenty minutes of scrolling, write down what you suddenly want. If the list is long and expensive, that's not a character flaw. That's the machine working as designed — on you, for free. Charge it rent or evict it.

### The spending speed limit

You don't beat creep with willpower; you beat it with a rule set in advance. Pick a **personal spending speed limit**: a dollar amount above which any purchase waits 72 hours, and a second, higher amount above which it also requires a conversation with one chosen person. Your numbers, your person — the worksheet in back walks you through it. The limit isn't there to say no. It's there to make sure the *you* deciding is the day-three you, not the cookout you.

#### What this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Run DeAndre's near-miss in numbers. His year will bring roughly $28,000, lumpy. The car he almost financed after his December check: $650 a month, six years, insurance pushing the real cost past $800 monthly. The purchase math said *I can afford this* — because purchase math always consults your best month. The **worst-month test** consults reality: in his four zero-income months, an $800 fixed obligation devours his living bucket and starts eating the reserve. Same car, two verdicts — and only one of the tests is honest about how lumpy income works. (The test isn't anti-car. A car his *worst* month carries passes it.)

Now watch the speed limit run in the wild. DeAndre's numbers: anything over **$300** waits 72 hours; anything over **$1,000** also gets a conversation with his older sister — his chosen person, picked for her immunity to hype. Day one: sneaker drop, $480, cart loaded, adrenaline high. The rule doesn't argue; it just starts the clock. Day three: still wants them — buys them, zero guilt, that's the flexible bucket doing its job. Two weeks later: limited watch, $1,900, a teammate's plug, "price goes up Friday." The clock starts, the sister call happens, and her one question — *would you buy this if nobody ever saw it?* — ends the transaction. The system didn't make DeAndre disciplined. It made him *slow*, and slow was enough.

> ❝ *Nobody photographs options. That's the whole problem — and the whole opportunity.*

> **Family Conversation** — What does this family celebrate out loud? For one month, try celebrating an options milestone — a funded reserve, an untouched savings goal — with the exact energy usually reserved for purchases. What families celebrate, athletes repeat.

#### What families should understand

Family can be creep's accelerant or its brake. Accelerant sounds like pride with a price tag — *my kid can afford it* — and, harder to hear, like the cousin at the cookout who frames restraint as embarrassment. Brake sounds like a household that treats visible spending as neither achievement nor shame, and treats the funded reserve as the flex it actually is. Ask about the options bucket with the same energy others ask about the car. What families celebrate, athletes repeat.

#### Mistakes to avoid

- Upgrading your fixed costs — car payment, apartment, subscriptions — after one good month. Fixed is forever; months aren't.
- Spending to answer someone else's comment.
- Confusing the flexible bucket (planned, guilt-free) with creep (unplanned, permanent).
- Measuring yourself against the locker room's loudest spender.
- Having no speed limit, so every decision happens at day-one euphoria.

#### Questions to ask before acting

1. Is this purchase for me, or for an audience — and could I name the audience?
2. Does this raise my *fixed* monthly costs, and can my worst realistic month carry that?
3. What would this money buy in options if it sat for a year?
4. What are my two speed-limit numbers, and who's my conversation person?
5. Would day-fourteen me still buy this?

> **DUCAT WEALTH PRINCIPLE** — *The goal is not to look wealthy. The goal is to build options.*

#### Chapter checklist

- [ ] I can explain lifestyle creep and why athletes face it faster
- [ ] I've named the appearance-vs-options tradeoff in my own spending
- [ ] Two speed-limit numbers chosen and written down
- [ ] My conversation person knows they're my conversation person
- [ ] One recent purchase honestly re-examined

#### Reflect

1. What's the last thing you bought mostly because of who would see it?
2. What do the people you most respect financially *not* spend money on?

## CHAPTER 10 — Family Money: Expectations, Boundaries, and Honest Conversations

*Account ownership and entity questions in this chapter are education only; specifics belong with a qualified attorney and tax professional.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> The text from his mother was four words — *car note is due* — and Andre paid it without a conversation, the way he had the month before, and the month before that. He loved her. She had carried the family alone for years. Nothing about helping was wrong.
>
> What was wrong was that nothing had ever been *said*. There was no number, no plan, no end date — just a widening set of assumptions. His mother assumed the help was permanent. His uncle assumed he was next in line. Andre assumed he could stop whenever it got heavy, and was discovering that assumption was false. By spring he was hiding deal announcements from his own family, not out of greed, but because every announcement raised the invisible tab.
>
> The money wasn't the problem. The silence was.

### The conversation nobody schedules

When an athlete starts earning, every family — loving, struggling, wealthy, complicated, all of them — inherits the same unscheduled conversation: *what does this money mean for us?* Most families never hold it out loud. The expectations form anyway, in the dark, where they grow teeth.

This chapter's whole argument is one sentence: **have the conversation on purpose, early, before the first request, while everyone can still be generous and honest at the same time.**

### Generous and sustainable

For many athletes — maybe you — helping family isn't a burden to be managed; it's a core reason the money matters at all. This book will never talk you out of that. It will insist on one adjective: *sustainable*.

Unsustainable generosity is Andre's: undefined, open-ended, growing, secret. Sustainable generosity is designed: **a named percentage or amount, inside the budget from Chapter 6, decided in calm, communicated in daylight.** "I move $300 a month to help with the house, and that's the number this year" is generosity with a spine. It can be planned around, on both sides. It survives a bad income month because it was sized for one. And it protects the relationship, because nobody has to guess, hint, or hide.

The uncomfortable math belongs on the table too: college athlete money is usually smaller and shorter than families imagine. A percentage of lumpy, temporary income is what exists. A family plan built on it being large and permanent is a plan for two heartbreaks.

### When the need is real

Everything above gets harder — and matters more — when the family's need isn't optional. Rent behind, lights at risk, a parent between jobs: for plenty of athletes, "help" isn't a generosity question; it's triage. This book won't pretend a boundary speech fixes that. It will say three true things. First, **even triage works better as a number**: "$400 a month until March" keeps a struggling household plannable and keeps the athlete's system alive, where "whatever's needed, whenever" quietly destroys both. Second, **the athlete cannot be the family's entire plan** — income this lumpy, temporary, and taxed cannot safely be a household's only pillar, and a family financial conversation (sometimes with a professional, sometimes with a campus financial-aid or community resource) is not a betrayal of anyone; it's load-bearing honesty. Third, **guard the tax and reserve buckets even in triage** — help paid out of the set-aside account isn't generosity, it's borrowing from a collector who always comes. The goal in hard seasons isn't perfect boundaries. It's *named* ones, sized to survive.

### Scripts for the hard versions

Words for moments that need them — adjust to your family's voice:

**Setting the number:** *"I want to help, and I want to still be helping two years from now. Here's what I can do every month without breaking my system: ___. That's a real commitment, so I need it to be the whole commitment."*

**The new request:** *"I hear you, and I'm not saying no because I don't care. Everything I can give is already going out. If this is more important than what I'm covering now, let's trade — but I can't add."*

**The relative outside the circle:** *"My money runs through a system my family and I set up, and I don't make same-day decisions. Send me the details and I'll look at it with my people."* (Notice this script also happens to be scam armor — Chapter 11.)

**Declining the entity/control ask:** *"I'm not signing anything that puts my money in someone else's name — that's a rule I follow with everyone, not a judgment about you. If there's a real reason for a structure, let's take it to an attorney together and let them explain it to both of us."*

**When you're the parent hearing a boundary:** the strongest thing you can say is *"that's a good system — I'm glad you have one."* An athlete whose boundaries are respected at home never has to practice hiding.

### Whose name is on what

The legal layer of family money is unglamorous and decisive.

**Accounts.** Minors generally need an adult on their accounts — sometimes as co-owner, sometimes through custodial arrangements (you may hear "UTMA," a common custodial account type where an adult manages a minor's money until adulthood). Whatever the structure: know who legally controls the money, and know when control transfers.

**Deals and signatures.** Contracts signed by minors involve parents; money from a minor's deals is still, in principle, the *athlete's* money. Families blur this line with the best intentions and the worst records.

**Entities.** Somewhere along the way, someone will suggest an LLC "for the NIL money," possibly with a relative as owner or manager. Entity questions get a real treatment in Book Two; for now, one bright line: **who owns the entity owns the money.** Never put your income into a structure you don't control without qualified legal advice explaining exactly why.

None of this is about distrusting family. It's about the version of this story where everything stays loving *because* the paperwork matched the intentions.

#### What this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Here's the Andre story rewritten with a system. Projected income: about $18,000 this year, lumpy. After his set-aside and savings splits, roughly $950 a month of livable money in an average month. He and his mother sit down — on purpose, in daylight — and design the help: **$250 a month toward the household, reviewed every August.** Why $250? Because he stress-tested it against his *worst* realistic months and it survives; because it's a number his mother can actually plan around, which unpredictable generosity never is; and because saying it out loud converted an invisible, growing tab into a commitment with edges.

Then February brings the test: his uncle needs $400 for a transmission. Old Andre pays it quietly and resents it privately. System Andre runs the **trade-don't-add rule**: "Everything I can give is already going out. If this matters more than what I'm covering, we can trade a month — but I can't add." The uncle grumbles. The system holds. And notice the quiet win underneath: Andre's mother backs the rule, because she was in the room when it was built. Boundaries survive exactly as long as the family that set them defends them together.

One more real-life layer: the **whose-name audit**. Andre's list took ten minutes — checking account (him + mom, decided), savings (his, mom has view-only alerts), one brand deal signed while he was seventeen (mom co-signed; money is his, and both of them can say so), no entities. Ten minutes, zero ambiguity, and every future conversation about "your money versus our money" now starts from a written page instead of a memory contest.

> ❝ *Generosity with a spine can be planned around — on both sides.*

> **Family Conversation** — This one *is* the chapter: run Worksheet 5 together, out loud, phones down. If the conversation gets hard, that's not the system failing. That's the system finding the exact spot where silence was charging interest.

#### What families should understand

This chapter reads differently from the parent's chair, so let's sit in it. You have sacrificed — driven the miles, paid for the camps, held things together. Wanting the era of sacrifice to end isn't greed; it's exhaustion, and it's human. Two truths can hold hands: your athlete's gratitude can be real, *and* their income can be smaller, shorter, and more taxed than it looks from outside. The gift only a parent can give here is making the honest conversation *safe* — asking for the number instead of resenting one, keeping the athlete's finances private from extended family, and never, ever using guilt as a payment method. The families that get this right aren't the ones without money tension. They're the ones who said the tension out loud while it was still small.

#### Mistakes to avoid

- Helping by reflex instead of by plan — every yes silently pricing the next one.
- Letting expectations form in silence and calling it keeping the peace.
- Announcing deals to extended family before the boundaries exist.
- Putting money or entities in anyone's name "for convenience" without legal advice.
- Treating the family conversation as one-and-done. It's annual, minimum.

#### Questions to ask before acting

1. What does my family actually expect from my income — has anyone said it out loud?
2. What monthly help is genuinely sustainable across my *worst* realistic months?
3. Who is in my inner money circle, and what does everyone outside it get told?
4. Whose names are on my accounts, deals, and any entities — and why, specifically?
5. When is our next family money conversation on the calendar?

## CHAPTER 11 — First-Money Mistakes (A Field Catalog)

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Coach Rivers had been around long enough to see the whole catalog. Twenty-two years, three schools, and now the NIL era, which hadn't invented any new mistakes — just added zeros to the old ones. When freshmen asked her what to watch out for, she'd pull out a legal pad and draw the same map every year: *here's where the ones before you fell.*
>
> "None of these people were dumb," she'd say, tapping the pad. "Every one of them was busy, young, generous, and rushed. Which is exactly what you are. So read the map."
>
> One freshman always asked the same follow-up: which mistake is the worst? Coach Rivers never hesitated. "Whichever one you're sure you'd never make. That's the one with your name on it — because it's the only one you're not watching for." 

What follows is the map — the recurring, documented, boringly predictable ways first money goes wrong. Every profile is fictional; every pattern is real. You are not doomed to any of them. You are also not exempt from any of them.

### 1. The Car

The classic, because it attacks from three directions at once: it's public (Chapter 9), it converts a windfall into a *permanent fixed cost* (Chapter 6), and it depreciates while you finance it. The pattern isn't "athlete buys car." It's "athlete converts one good season into five years of payments sized for their best month." **Counter-move:** windfall protocol, speed limit, and the fixed-cost question — *can my worst month carry this?*

### 2. The Friend Loan

"Loan" is usually the wrong word; these are gifts with resentment attached. The pattern: a friend needs money, you have money, the word *loan* lets everyone pretend, repayment never comes, and you lose the money *and* the friend. **Counter-move:** decide your policy before the ask. Many people use: *I don't loan; I sometimes give, at sizes I can afford to never see again.* Then the script from Chapter 10.

### 3. The Unvetted "Adviser"

He appears after your best game. He's got a hoodie-and-watches aesthetic, screenshots of returns, a "team" of athletes, and urgency. Sometimes he's a scammer; often he's just unqualified, which loses your money almost as efficiently. **Counter-move:** nobody touches your money without verification — Chapter 12 gives you the exact free lookup tools and the questions that make pretenders evaporate. Real professionals survive being checked. Fakes get offended by it.

### 4. The "Guaranteed" Investment

Crypto plays, sports-betting syndicates, a cousin's clothing brand, real estate courses — the packaging rotates; the tell never changes: **guaranteed returns, urgency, and exclusivity are the three-note chord of every scam.** Legitimate investing never guarantees, rarely rushes, and doesn't recruit twenty-year-olds by DM. **Counter-move:** the sentence *"I don't do same-day money decisions"* — then watch how they react to it. Pressure in response to patience is a confession.

### 5. The Ignored Quarterlies

Not dramatic, no villain — just Bri's April from Chapter 8, arriving on schedule for every athlete who treated set-asides as optional. This mistake is the most common in the whole catalog and the only one with a federal agency attached. **Counter-move:** the four habits. They're boring. So is not owing anybody anything in April.

### 6. The Mixed Accounts

One account for everything: deal money, birthday money, rent, splurges. Painless for months — then tax season, or a compliance question, or an aid form turns your finances into an archaeology dig. **Counter-move:** the Chapter 5 afternoon. Three accounts, one log, done.

### 7. Cash-App Chaos

Related but distinct: real income flowing through payment apps with no records kept, screenshots as receipts, memo lines as accounting. Apps are fine as *pipes*; they're terrible as *books*. **Counter-move:** everything lands in the income account, everything gets a deal-log row, every payment confirmation gets saved to the folder.

### 8. The Unread Signature

Contracts signed at the pace of a group photo — long rights windows, exclusivity clauses, auto-renewals, buried obligations, all discovered later, all binding. In the new era this includes rev-share agreements with transfer and repayment terms (Chapter 3). **Counter-move:** the no-same-day-signature rule, plus attorney review for anything with real money or long rights attached. The deal that can't wait 48 hours is telling you something. Believe it.

### 9. The Shared Login

A girlfriend "helps manage" the account. A "manager" friend has the passwords. A parent moves money without conversation. Most versions start with love and end with a balance nobody can explain. **Counter-move:** the access rule from Chapter 5 — everyone on the account is there by decision, nothing moves without your knowledge, and *nobody* outside the decided circle ever holds a login.

### 10. The Rush Itself

Underneath all nine: speed. Every entry in this catalog required a decision made faster than it needed to be — usually to avoid awkwardness, please someone, or catch a "closing window." The windows are almost never real. The awkwardness always passes. **Counter-move:** make slowness your reputation. Let everyone in your world learn that you check, verify, sleep on it, and run things by your people. The right opportunities will wait. The wrong ones will out themselves by refusing to.

> ❝ *Being hard to rush is a financial skill.*

#### What this means in real life

*Hypothetical — a composite of real scam patterns, dissected.* Here's the DM, more or less as a thousand athletes have received it:

> "Yo champ 🔥 been watching your season. I run a private investment group — **only working with 5 athletes** this cycle. Last cohort saw **guaranteed 40% returns**. Spots close **Friday**. Don't let your teammates eat without you 💰 Send $2k to lock in."

Now read it like a coroner. **"Guaranteed 40%"** — legitimate investing never guarantees returns; the word *guaranteed* attached to any market return is a confession, not a pitch. **"Only 5 athletes" / "spots close Friday"** — manufactured exclusivity and urgency, the other two notes of the chord, both designed to outrun your verification reflex. **"Send $2k to lock in"** — payment by app, to a person, with no firm, no documents, no registration to check. And the flourish — *don't let your teammates eat without you* — is the tell that he's run this exact play on your locker room before.

The **ten-minute verification reflex** kills it every time: search the name at adviserinfo.sec.gov and BrokerCheck (Chapter 12) — nothing; ask for the firm's Form ADV and registration — deflection; reply "I don't do same-day money decisions, send documents and I'll review with my people" — and watch urgency curdle into pressure or silence. Pressure in response to patience is a confession. Ten minutes, zero dollars, one deleted DM.

> **Family Conversation** — Agree on the household pact: *"let me check with my people" is always a complete sentence*, and no family member ever undercuts it — including when the opportunity comes *from* family. Then trade phones for five minutes and find the chord — guaranteed, urgent, exclusive — in the wild. It's rarely more than a few scrolls away.

#### What families should understand

Read the catalog as a household and notice something: a family can *run* several of these plays on its own athlete — the reflex loan, the shared login, the pressure purchase, the relative's guaranteed investment. Nobody means harm. Everybody means help. The families that avoid the catalog are the ones willing to see themselves in it. Post the three-note chord — guaranteed, urgent, exclusive — where everyone can quote it, and agree that in this house, *"let me check with my people"* is always a complete sentence.

#### Questions to ask before acting

1. Which entries in this catalog am I most built to fall for — honestly?
2. What's my loan/gift policy, decided today, before anyone asks?
3. Does this opportunity feature any of the three notes — guaranteed, urgent, exclusive?
4. Has anyone qualified and independent looked at this?
5. Why can't this wait 48 hours — and is the answer a reason or a red flag?

> **DUCAT WEALTH PRINCIPLE** — *You don't need to catch every opportunity. You need to keep the right ones.*

#### Chapter checklist

- [ ] I've identified my two most likely catalog entries
- [ ] Loan/gift policy decided and phrased
- [ ] The three-note scam chord memorized (guaranteed · urgent · exclusive)
- [ ] No-same-day-signature rule adopted, family informed
- [ ] Access list audit complete — every login accounted for

#### Reflect

1. Which mistake in the catalog have you already watched happen to someone?
2. What makes you rushable — awkwardness, loyalty, excitement, fear of missing out?
3. What do you want your reputation to be when someone brings you an "opportunity"?

## CHAPTER 12 — Your Money Team, Starter Version

*This chapter describes professional roles for education. Ducat Private Wealth is an investment adviser — a conflict worth naming when a firm writes about hiring professionals. Use every question in this chapter on anyone you consider, including us.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> When Sofia's NIL income crossed into real money, her father did what he did before any big purchase: he built a list. Not of things to buy — of people to interview. A CPA recommended by two families he trusted. An attorney who reviewed contracts for small businesses. Later, a financial adviser — three of them, actually, interviewed with the same printed sheet of questions.
>
> One adviser talked for forty minutes and asked Sofia nothing. One promised returns that made her father put a small star next to the name — the kind of star that means *no*. One asked about her season, her family, her plans after volleyball, and answered the fee question in one clear sentence. The sheet of questions cost nothing. It was the best financial decision they made all year.
>
> Sofia's father wasn't a finance person. He managed a warehouse, and he interviewed professionals the way he interviewed forklift vendors: assume nothing, verify everything, and pay attention to who gets irritated by ordinary questions. It turns out that skill transfers perfectly. You don't need financial expertise to hire well. You need a process and the nerve to use it on charming people.

### Why a team, and why now

Every chapter so far has ended the same way: *ask a qualified professional*. This chapter is the directory. Not because you need to hire everyone tomorrow — most athletes start with one or two — but because knowing **who does what** is itself protection. Most catalog mistakes from Chapter 11 begin with the wrong person doing the wrong job: the "adviser" reviewing contracts, the agent doing taxes, the cousin doing everything.

### The roster

**CPA / tax professional.** Handles what Chapters 7–8 set up: your filing, your quarterlies, your deductions, the multi-state and family-return questions. *Cannot do:* legal work, contract review, investment advice. *When:* the first year athlete income is real money — often the first hire an athlete makes.

**Attorney.** Reads what you sign — NIL contracts, rev-share agreements, entity documents — and represents *your* interests in them. *Cannot do:* taxes, investments. *When:* before any signature with real money or long rights attached. Many families use attorneys per-contract rather than on retainer; that's normal.

**Financial adviser.** Helps with planning, saving, and eventually investing — the Book Three material. One word to learn now, years before you need it: **fiduciary**. A fiduciary adviser is obligated to put your interests first. It is the first word in the interview, and Book Three devotes a full chapter to choosing well. *Cannot do:* taxes, legal work, deal negotiation. *When:* when there's actually money to plan — and never under pressure.

**Agent / NIL representative.** Negotiates and sources deals. Agents are regulated — most states require athlete-agent registration, and schools maintain their own rules — so *verify registration* before anyone represents you. In Florida, even professionals advising high schoolers on NIL fall under registration rules. *Cannot do:* everything else on this list, no matter how full-service the pitch. *When:* if and when deal flow genuinely exceeds what you and your family can handle.

**School compliance office.** The free one. Confirms rules, receives disclosures, answers the "is this allowed?" question before it becomes expensive. *Cannot do:* negotiate for you, do your taxes, or protect your interests against the school itself (they work for the school — friendly, useful, and not your agent). *When:* constantly.

**Parents and family.** The default team until the professionals arrive, and the permanent conveners afterward. The healthiest version of the parent role: *chief question-asker and calendar-keeper* — present in every meeting, in control of none of the athlete's signatures. Help without taking over; the system is the athlete's to own, because someday soon it has to be.

### How to verify anyone

Free, fast, and non-negotiable:

- **Investment advisers:** look them up at **adviserinfo.sec.gov** — registration, experience, and disciplinary history. Any legitimate adviser expects you to check.
- **Brokers:** **BrokerCheck** (brokercheck.finra.org).
- **CPAs:** your state's board of accountancy license lookup.
- **Attorneys:** your state bar's directory.
- **Agents:** your state's athlete-agent registration and your school's approved-agent process.

If a person resists verification, gets vague about credentials, or answers the fee question with a story instead of a number — that's your answer, delivered early and free.

### The interview sheet

Take these to *every* candidate, in every role. (Yes — including any firm whose name is on this book.)

1. Exactly what services do you provide, and what do you *not* do?
2. How are you paid — by me, by commissions, by third parties? What's the total, in dollars?
3. Are you a fiduciary at all times when working with me? *(for advisers)*
4. Who else on your team touches my information or money?
5. What's your experience with athletes or irregular income?
6. Can I have that in writing?

The professionals worth hiring answer these quickly, clearly, and without offense. The ones who bristle are saving you money.

#### What this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Here's Sofia's three-adviser interview sheet after the meetings, compressed to the two questions that did the sorting:

| | **"How are you paid?"** | **"What did they ask Sofia?"** |
|---|---|---|
| Adviser A | Twenty minutes of story, no number | Nothing — talked the whole time |
| Adviser B | "Returns will cover my fee, guaranteed" | Her follower count |
| Adviser C | One sentence: an annual fee, stated in dollars and percent, paid by her, no commissions | Her season, her family, her plans after volleyball |

Adviser A failed the clarity test: anyone who can't state their own compensation in one sentence is telling you how every future hard conversation will go. Adviser B failed on a single word — *guaranteed* — the same three-note chord from Chapter 11, wearing a blazer. Adviser C passed not by being impressive but by being *checkable*: verified at adviserinfo.sec.gov before the meeting, fiduciary answer in writing after it, fee math Sofia's father could repeat back. The sheet didn't find a genius. It filtered for honesty and let the family's judgment do the rest — which is all an interview sheet is for.

For the adviser role specifically, know what a healthy engagement even looks like, so the unhealthy version is recognizable. A legitimate advisory relationship starts with *your* facts — goals, income shape, family situation — moves at your pace, puts fees and scope in writing you can keep, and leaves you in control of your accounts (advisers manage or advise; they don't become your login). You should leave early meetings knowing more, not feeling smaller. And no legitimate adviser needs an answer today — a sentence you now know is a full diagnostic test.

When is it time to make these hires? Honest, education-only triggers most families use: a **tax professional** the first year athlete income is real money to your household, or the moment forms, states, or aid questions multiply. An **attorney**, per-contract, the first time a signature carries real money or long rights. An **adviser**, only once there's genuinely money to plan — and never, under any income, in response to someone else's urgency. The order isn't a rule; the pattern is: *paper first, planning second, and verification always.*

> ❝ *The interview sheet doesn't find geniuses. It filters for honesty.*

> **Family Conversation** — Build the family's referral bench before you need it: each adult names one professional they'd trust with a hard question, and why. A list built in calm beats a search conducted in crisis.

#### What families should understand

Hiring professionals can feel like an expense a young athlete shouldn't need, or a status symbol they shouldn't want. It's neither — it's role clarity, purchased. The parent's contribution is process: build the interview list, attend the meetings, insist on verification, and model comfort with the awkward questions, especially the fee question. One more guardrail that belongs to you: **no professional, ever, gets discretionary control of accounts, logins, or signing power as a condition of "helping."** Real professionals build guardrails. Pretenders ask for keys.

#### Mistakes to avoid

- One person doing every job — especially an unlicensed person.
- Hiring the loudest recruiter instead of the clearest answerer.
- Skipping verification because they came recommended, seem successful, or "work with athletes."
- Letting any professional bypass the family's no-rush rule.
- Treating compliance as adversaries and salespeople as friends. Often it's the reverse.

### The team's annual rhythm

A money team isn't a one-time assembly; it's a calendar. The light-touch version that fits an athlete's life: **tax professional** — one sit-down before year-end, plus quick contact around the quarterly dates. **Attorney** — per-signature, no standing schedule. **Compliance** — before every deal, every time, as reflex. **Family** — the annual money conversation from Chapter 10, where the whole system gets reviewed: accounts, percentages, boundaries, and whether the team still fits the season you're in. **Adviser** — when one eventually joins, an annual review at minimum. Put the recurring pieces on the calendar the day you finish this book, and the team stops being a chapter you read and becomes a rhythm you keep.

#### Questions to ask before acting

1. Which role does my situation actually need *next* — and which am I being sold?
2. Has every candidate been verified through the official lookup, not their own website?
3. How does this person get paid, and did they say it in one clear sentence?
4. What did they ask *me* — about my life, family, and goals — in the first meeting?
5. Would I hire them if they weren't charming?

> **DUCAT WEALTH PRINCIPLE** — *Advice is only as good as the incentives behind it.*

- [ ] Verification links saved: adviserinfo.sec.gov, BrokerCheck, state boards

### The whole book on two pages — chapter summaries

**1 · The New Money Era.** Since 2021 (NIL) and 2025 (the House settlement), athlete money runs in three lanes — scholarships, school revenue sharing, third-party NIL — each with different rules, taxes, and paperwork. Sort every dollar by lane, and remember the advantage isn't the amount; it's building the system early.

**2 · NIL, Explained.** An NIL deal is a small business transaction: permission (use of your name, image, likeness) plus deliverables (real work), from payers who all expect something back. Free product counts as income at fair market value. Triage every offer with five questions before anyone reviews anything.

**3 · Revenue Sharing Is Not NIL.** Direct school payments under a school-level cap (~$21.5M in 2026–27) — discretionary, uneven, and governed by real contracts with transfer, buyout, and injury clauses. Generally paid with no tax withholding. Read the clauses, not just the number; attorney review before signing.

**4 · The Rules.** Four layers — NCAA/CSC, state law, school policy, unfinished federal activity. Third-party deals of $600+ get reported through NIL Go, promptly. Booster-connected deals face valid-business-purpose and range-of-compensation review. Reporting is protection. Refresh your rulebook every summer.

**5 · Build the System.** One afternoon, before any money: three accounts with jobs (income, tax set-aside, spending), alerts on, access decided on purpose, a deal log with full columns, and one folder holding every contract and W-9. Businesses keep books; you're a business now.

**6 · Budgeting Lumpy Income.** Budget the dollar, not the month: every deposit splits by percentage into taxes, saving, living, and flexible — taxes first, always. Build the emergency reserve before anything clever. Windfalls: split first, sit two weeks.

**7 · Taxes, Part One.** Athlete income is generally self-employment income — gross, unwithheld, taxable with or without a form. The $2,000 form threshold changes paperwork, not what you owe. Non-cash compensation and collective payments are taxable. Self-employment tax starts at $400 of net earnings.

**8 · Taxes, Part Two.** Four habits: set aside a professional-confirmed percentage of every check; hit the four quarterly dates; document expenses and pay them from the income account; hold an annual pre-year-end sit-down covering multi-state income and the FAFSA effect.

**9 · Lifestyle Creep.** Spending rises to meet income unless a rule stops it — and athletes face the pattern publicly, in escalating peer groups, on short timelines. Every dollar buys appearance or options. Set two speed-limit numbers and a conversation person; edit the feed that manufactures the wanting.

**10 · Family Money.** Have the conversation on purpose, early, in daylight. Convert help into a named, sustainable number inside the budget. Trade, don't add. Know whose name is on every account, deal, and entity — and why. Even triage works better with edges.

**11 · First-Money Mistakes.** The field catalog: the car, the friend loan, the fake adviser, the "guaranteed" investment, ignored quarterlies, mixed accounts, cash-app chaos, unread signatures, shared logins — and underneath all of them, the rush. The scam chord is guaranteed–urgent–exclusive. Be hard to rush.

**12 · The Money Team.** CPA, attorney, adviser (learn the word *fiduciary*), registered agent, compliance office, family — each with a lane and a list of things they cannot do. Verify everyone through official lookups. Six interview questions, used on every candidate, including any firm whose name is on this book.

### Glossary

**1099-K** — Tax form from payment platforms reporting payment volume above federal thresholds.
**1099-NEC** — Tax form businesses use to report payments of $2,000+ (tax year 2026 forward) to independent contractors. The threshold governs the form, not whether tax is owed.
**Associated entity** — A business or organization closely tied to a school (notably significant boosters/collectives); its athlete deals receive additional review.
**Buyout clause** — Contract term requiring payment (or forfeiting payments) to exit an agreement early.
**Cap / pool limit** — The annual maximum an opted-in school may distribute to athletes through revenue sharing (~$21.5M in 2026–27). A school-level ceiling; not an individual promise.
**College Sports Commission (CSC)** — Enforcement body created after the House settlement to oversee revenue-share and third-party NIL compliance.
**Collective** — A fan/donor-funded organization arranging NIL opportunities around a school. Payments from collectives are taxable compensation.
**Compliance office** — School office that interprets rules, receives disclosures, and confirms whether activities are permitted.
**CPA** — Certified Public Accountant; licensed tax and accounting professional.
**Deal log** — Your running record of every deal: payer, deliverables, rights, values, dates, reporting status.
**Deliverables** — The work a deal obligates you to perform (posts, appearances, content).
**Emergency reserve** — Savings whose only job is absorbing surprises; commonly sized at three to six months of essential expenses.
**Estimated taxes (quarterlies)** — Four payments self-employed people generally make across the year (mid-April, June, September, January) in place of employer withholding.
**Fair market value** — What something would sell for between willing parties; the value at which non-cash compensation is generally counted as income.
**FAFSA** — Free Application for Federal Student Aid. NIL/rev-share income is reported on it and can affect need-based aid, including Pell Grants.
**FDIC / NCUA** — Federal insurers of deposits at banks (FDIC) and credit unions (NCUA).
**Fiduciary** — A professional obligated to act in your best interest. The first vocabulary word of Book Three.
**Fixed costs** — Recurring obligations (car note, rent, subscriptions) that continue regardless of income.
**Gross vs. net** — Gross is the check as it arrives; net is what remains after taxes. Athlete checks arrive gross.
**House settlement** — The 2025 court-approved settlement (House v. NCAA) creating back payments to former athletes and permitting direct school-to-athlete revenue sharing from July 1, 2025.
**Independent contractor** — A self-employed person paid for services without employer withholding; the usual tax status of NIL income.
**Kiddie tax** — Rules under which certain income of dependents can be taxed at the parents' rate. A professional's question.
**Lifestyle creep** — The pattern of spending rising automatically to meet income.
**LLC** — Limited liability company; a business entity type. Who owns the entity owns the money.
**NIL** — Name, image, and likeness; the rights businesses pay to use in third-party athlete deals.
**NIL Go** — The CSC's clearinghouse where third-party deals of $600+ (aggregate) must be reported, generally within about five business days.
**Range of compensation** — Review standard asking whether deal pay is roughly in line with comparable deals.
**Revenue sharing** — Direct school-to-athlete payments under the House settlement's capped system. Not NIL.
**Roster limits** — Post-settlement team-size caps that replaced scholarship limits at opted-in schools.
**Self-employment tax** — Social Security and Medicare tax (~15.3%) paid by the self-employed on net earnings of $400+.
**Set-aside** — The fixed percentage of every check moved immediately to the tax account.
**UTMA account** — A custodial account where an adult manages a minor's assets until adulthood.
**Valid business purpose** — Review standard asking whether a deal reflects genuine business goals and real deliverables rather than disguised pay-for-play.
**W-2** — Employee wage form; reflects withholding.
**W-9** — Form you give a payer providing your tax information so they can report payments.
**Windfall** — A large one-time payment. Split first, sit second.
