▶ FACEBOOK CAPTION
When you contribute to a 457(b) or 403(b), you usually get to choose pre-tax or Roth — and most people pick pre-tax by default without weighing it. Here’s the trade-off: pre-tax contributions lower your taxable income today and are taxed when you withdraw; Roth contributions are made with after-tax dollars and generally come out tax-free in retirement. The right choice often comes down to whether you expect your tax rate to be higher now or later. One wrinkle worth knowing: an FRS pension provides taxable income in retirement, so having some Roth money can add useful flexibility. It’s rarely all-or-nothing — a mix of both gives you more control. This is a tax-planning question best modeled with your CPA.

▶ LINKEDIN CAPTION
The Roth-versus-pre-tax decision in a 457(b) or 403(b) is, at its core, a bet on the direction of your future tax rate — and one many savers make by default rather than by design. The trade-off: pre-tax contributions reduce taxable income today and are taxed later; Roth contributions are made with after-tax dollars, and qualified withdrawals are generally tax-free. The right choice hinges largely on whether you expect to be in a higher tax bracket now or in retirement. A consideration specific to FRS members: the pension itself provides taxable income in retirement, which can push retirement-year income higher than expected. Holding some Roth money alongside pre-tax savings creates valuable flexibility to manage taxable income year to year. This is rarely all-or-nothing — a deliberate blend often serves best, and the optimal mix is best modeled with your CPA.
