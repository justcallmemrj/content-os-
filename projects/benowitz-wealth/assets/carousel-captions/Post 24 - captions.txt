▶ FACEBOOK CAPTION
Here’s the financial logic behind Special Risk. First responders — law enforcement, firefighters, correctional officers, certain EMS — often retire younger than the average worker, because the careers are physically demanding. So the pension is designed to build faster: Special Risk members accrue 3% of their Average Final Compensation per year of service, compared with 1.6% for Regular Class. The result is significant — 25 years of Special Risk service accrues 75% of AFC, while the same 25 years at Regular Class accrues about 40%. One important caveat: only the years you actually worked in a Special Risk position earn the 3% rate. If your duties shifted over your career, it’s worth confirming exactly how each period is classified with MyFRS.

▶ LINKEDIN CAPTION
The 3% accrual rate is the financial core of the Special Risk Class — and it exists for a clear reason. First responders — law enforcement officers, firefighters, correctional officers, and certain EMS personnel — typically have physically demanding careers and often retire younger than the average worker. A standard, slowly-accruing pension wouldn’t fit that reality. So Special Risk members accrue 3% of Average Final Compensation per year of service, nearly double the 1.6% Regular Class rate. The difference compounds meaningfully: 25 years of Special Risk service accrues 75% of AFC, versus roughly 40% for the same tenure in Regular Class. An important qualification: only service actually worked in a Special Risk position accrues at 3%. For members whose duties — and therefore classification — changed, confirming how each period is classified with MyFRS is worthwhile.
