▶ FACEBOOK CAPTION
A Medicare rule that catches people off guard: the Part B late-enrollment penalty is permanent. If you don’t sign up for Part B when you’re first eligible — and you don’t have qualifying employer coverage that lets you delay — you’ll pay a penalty of 10% of the premium for each full 12-month period you waited. That penalty is added to your premium for as long as you have Part B. On the 2026 base premium of $202.90, delaying two years adds roughly $40 a month, for life. There are legitimate reasons to delay Part B, chiefly having active employer coverage that grants you a Special Enrollment Period later. But outside of those situations, waiting rarely pays off. Check your specific circumstances before deciding to skip it. (We’re a fiduciary firm, not an insurance agency — our role is to explain, not to sell.)

A Medicare rule worth flagging because it’s both costly and permanent: the Part B late-enrollment penalty. If you don’t enroll in Part B when first eligible — and you lack qualifying employer coverage that would grant a Special Enrollment Period — the penalty is 10% of the premium for each full 12-month period you delayed, added to your premium for as long as you have Part B. Applied to the 2026 standard premium of $202.90, a two-year delay adds roughly $40 per month — indefinitely. There are legitimate reasons to delay Part B, most commonly active employer coverage (for you or a spouse). Outside those circumstances, the math rarely favors waiting.
