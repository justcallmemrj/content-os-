▶ FACEBOOK CAPTION
A common mix-up: 65 is when Medicare starts, but for Social Security, your “full retirement age” is likely 67. If you were born in 1960 or later, 67 is the age your full benefit is payable. You can start as early as 62 (smaller check) or wait until 70 (larger check) — but everything is measured from your full retirement age. It’s a simple fact that quietly shapes a big decision.

▶ LINKEDIN CAPTION
A small but consequential point of confusion in retirement planning: full retirement age for Social Security is not 65. For anyone born in 1960 or later, it is 67. (Age 65 is the Medicare eligibility age — a separate milestone.) Claiming before full retirement age — as early as 62 — produces a permanently reduced benefit. Delaying past it — up to age 70 — increases the benefit. Knowing your full retirement age is the foundation; the strategy question of where, between 62 and 70, to claim comes next, and depends on your broader income picture, health, and whether a spouse is involved.
