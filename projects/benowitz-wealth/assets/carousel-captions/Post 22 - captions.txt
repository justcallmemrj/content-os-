▶ FACEBOOK CAPTION
If you claim Social Security before your full retirement age and keep working, there’s a rule worth understanding: the earnings test. For 2026, if you’re under full retirement age for the whole year, Social Security withholds $1 in benefits for every $2 you earn above $24,480. In the year you reach full retirement age, the threshold jumps to $65,160 and the withholding is $1 for every $3 — and only until the month you reach FRA. Once you’re at full retirement age, there’s no earnings limit at all, and the amounts withheld earlier are credited back to you through a higher benefit. Two things to remember: the test applies only to wages and self-employment income (not your pension, IRA withdrawals, or investment income), and it’s really about timing rather than a permanent loss. Still, if you plan to work while claiming early, run the numbers first.

▶ LINKEDIN CAPTION
For those considering claiming Social Security before full retirement age while continuing to work, the earnings test is essential to understand — and frequently misunderstood as a permanent penalty. The 2026 mechanics: under full retirement age all year, $1 in benefits is withheld for every $2 earned above $24,480; in the year you reach full retirement age, $1 is withheld for every $3 above $65,160, applied only until the month you reach FRA; at and after full retirement age, there is no earnings limit. Two clarifications: the test applies only to wages and self-employment income — not pensions, IRA distributions, or investment income — and withheld benefits aren’t lost: once you reach full retirement age, your benefit is recalculated upward to credit back the withheld months. The earnings test is better understood as a timing mechanism than a true reduction.
