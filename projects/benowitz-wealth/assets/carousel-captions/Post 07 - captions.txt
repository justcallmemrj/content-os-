▶ FACEBOOK CAPTION
One of the most important retirement decisions comes down to timing: when do you claim Social Security? Here’s the spread for someone whose full retirement age is 67. Claim at the earliest age, 62, and your benefit is permanently reduced to roughly 70%. Wait until 70 and it grows to about 124%. Same earnings record — very different lifetime income. There’s no one right answer; it depends on your health, your other income, taxes, and whether a spouse depends on the benefit. But it’s a decision worth running the numbers on, not guessing at.

▶ LINKEDIN CAPTION
The Social Security claiming decision produces strikingly different outcomes from an identical earnings record — and it’s too often made on instinct rather than analysis. For an individual whose full retirement age is 67: claiming at 62 yields roughly 70% of the full benefit, a permanent reduction; claiming at 67 yields 100%; delaying to 70 yields approximately 124%, due to delayed retirement credits. That’s a wide band of lifetime income, fixed for life at the moment you claim. Earlier claiming isn’t a mistake, and delaying isn’t universally optimal — the right choice depends on health and longevity, other income, the tax picture, and survivor considerations for a spouse.
