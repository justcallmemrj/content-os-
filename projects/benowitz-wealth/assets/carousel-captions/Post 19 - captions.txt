▶ FACEBOOK CAPTION
If you’re approaching the end of DROP, here’s a decision worth preparing for: what to do with your accumulated balance. When you separate, you typically have 60 days to either take the money as a lump sum — fully taxable in that year, which can push you into a higher tax bracket — or roll it into a tax-deferred account like an IRA, which preserves the tax deferral until you withdraw it later. It’s the same money either way, but the tax consequences can differ dramatically. There’s no one right answer; it depends on your bracket, your income needs, and your overall plan. The key is to decide deliberately — ideally with a tax professional — rather than by default. Confirm the rollover details with MyFRS.

▶ LINKEDIN CAPTION
For members completing DROP, the final step — what to do with the accumulated balance — is where an avoidable tax outcome most often occurs. Upon separation, you generally have 60 days to either take the balance as a lump sum (fully taxable in the year received, which can push you into a higher marginal bracket) or roll it into a tax-deferred account such as an IRA (preserving tax deferral until you withdraw). It’s the same money; the tax consequences are not. A large lump sum recognized in a single year can have ripple effects beyond income tax — potentially affecting Medicare IRMAA two years later, for instance. There’s no universally correct choice; it depends on your tax bracket, income needs, and broader plan. What matters is that the decision is made deliberately and with a tax professional’s input — before the 60-day window closes.
