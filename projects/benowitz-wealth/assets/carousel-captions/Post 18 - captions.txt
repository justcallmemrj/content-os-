▶ FACEBOOK CAPTION
A reminder that retirement readiness is about more than your portfolio. Everyone — regardless of wealth — should have a few core documents in place: a will (who receives what), a durable power of attorney (someone you trust to handle financial matters if you’re unable to), a health care surrogate and living will (who makes medical decisions, and your wishes), and current beneficiary designations on your accounts. For some families, a revocable living trust helps avoid probate. These documents exist to make sure your wishes are honored and your loved ones aren’t left untangling a mess during a hard time. Because drafting them is the practice of law, this is a job for a qualified Florida attorney — we’re glad to coordinate.

▶ LINKEDIN CAPTION
Retirement readiness is frequently equated with portfolio readiness. But a sound plan also requires a basic estate toolkit — and it’s an area where otherwise well-prepared people often have gaps. The essentials, regardless of estate size: a will (directs who receives your assets); a durable power of attorney (authorizes someone you trust to manage finances if you become unable to); a health care surrogate and living will (designates who makes medical decisions and records your wishes); and current beneficiary designations on retirement accounts and life insurance (these pass outside the will). For some families, a revocable living trust adds probate avoidance, privacy, and incapacity management. These documents aren’t about wealth; they’re about ensuring your wishes are followed. Because drafting them is the practice of law, this work belongs with a qualified Florida estate attorney. As a fiduciary, our role is to coordinate the financial picture alongside the legal and tax professionals you engage.
