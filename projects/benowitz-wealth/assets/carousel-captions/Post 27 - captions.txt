▶ FACEBOOK CAPTION
A retirement reality that catches people off guard: a portion of your Social Security benefits can be subject to federal income tax. Whether it is — and how much — depends on your “combined income,” which is your adjusted gross income plus any nontaxable interest plus half of your Social Security benefits. For single filers, combined income between $25,000 and $34,000 makes up to 50% of benefits taxable, and above $34,000 up to 85%. For married couples filing jointly, the thresholds are $32,000 to $44,000 (up to 50%) and above $44,000 (up to 85%). Here’s the kicker: those thresholds have not been adjusted for inflation since 1993, so more retirees cross them every year. Because other income — your pension, IRA withdrawals, Roth conversions — raises your combined income, the timing and sequence of how you draw income can influence your Social Security taxes. One bright spot: Florida has no state income tax, so this is purely a federal consideration. Worth modeling with a tax professional.

▶ LINKEDIN CAPTION
A frequently underappreciated aspect of retirement income: a portion of Social Security benefits can be federally taxable, and the share rises as other income increases. Taxation is based on “combined income” — adjusted gross income, plus nontaxable interest, plus half of Social Security benefits. Single filers: $25,000–$34,000 → up to 50% of benefits taxable; above $34,000 → up to 85%. Married filing jointly: $32,000–$44,000 → up to 50%; above $44,000 → up to 85%. Critically, these thresholds have not been indexed for inflation since 1993 — so an increasing share of retirees becomes subject to them over time. The planning relevance: because other income sources raise combined income, the timing and sequencing of withdrawals can influence how much of your Social Security is taxed. Florida’s lack of a state income tax means this is a federal-only consideration for residents. Given the interactions, it’s worth modeling with a tax professional.
