▶ FACEBOOK CAPTION
Most of the public employees we talk to have been quietly funding a 457(b) or 403(b) for years — often without a clear strategy for it. Here’s the framing that helps: your FRS pension (or Investment Plan) is the foundation of your retirement income. Deferred comp is the flexible layer you build on top — voluntary, self-directed, and yours to coordinate. You can contribute pre-tax or Roth, up to $24,500 in 2026 (more with catch-up contributions at 50+). The pension gives you a floor; deferred comp gives you options. It just deserves a real plan.

▶ LINKEDIN CAPTION
For most Florida public employees, retirement income starts with the FRS — either the Pension Plan or the Investment Plan. Deferred compensation is the layer built on top of that foundation. A 457(b) (common for state, county, and municipal employees) or a 403(b) (common for educators) is voluntary, self-directed savings that supplements the pension. You choose pre-tax or Roth treatment. The 2026 elective deferral limit is $24,500, with additional catch-up contributions available at 50+ (and an enhanced catch-up for ages 60–63). In practice, many employees fund these accounts for years without a coordinated plan — a missed opportunity, since the pension provides a guaranteed floor while deferred comp provides the flexibility that matters for bridging early-retirement years, managing taxes, and estate goals.
