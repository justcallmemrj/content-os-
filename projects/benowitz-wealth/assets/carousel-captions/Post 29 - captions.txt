▶ FACEBOOK CAPTION
Here’s something that surprises a lot of people: your will does not control where your retirement accounts and life insurance go. Those assets — IRAs, 457(b) and 403(b) accounts, life insurance policies — pass directly to whoever is listed on the beneficiary designation form, regardless of what your will says. That means an outdated form can quietly override your current wishes. We’ve seen designations that still name an ex-spouse, or a relative who has passed away, or simply no one at all. The fix is simple and quick: review your beneficiary designations after any major life event — marriage, divorce, a death in the family, the birth of a child or grandchild. It takes five minutes and can prevent a heartbreaking mistake. (Estate documents are legal matters, so coordinate with a qualified attorney.)

▶ LINKEDIN CAPTION
A deceptively important detail in estate planning: beneficiary designations override your will. Retirement accounts (IRAs, 457(b)s, 403(b)s) and life insurance pass directly to the individuals named on their beneficiary forms — independent of, and superior to, the instructions in your will. The consequence is that an outdated designation can entirely override your current intentions. Forms naming a former spouse, a deceased relative, or no one at all are more common than people expect, and they can direct significant assets contrary to your wishes. The remedy is straightforward: review beneficiary designations after every major life event — marriage, divorce, a death, the birth of a child or grandchild — and confirm both primary and contingent beneficiaries are current. As always, estate matters are legal in nature; coordinate document decisions with a qualified Florida attorney.
