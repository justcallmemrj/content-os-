### [SLIDE 5 — What DROP Actually Is] · 5:30–11:00 · Teaching Section 1

Alright. Let's define the thing.

DROP stands for the **Deferred Retirement Option Program**. And the name is honestly a little intimidating, so let me translate it into a sentence you'd actually say out loud.

Here's DROP in plain English: **you officially "retire" on paper — but you keep working and keep getting your regular paycheck.** And while you're doing that, the monthly pension you *would* have started collecting gets set aside for you in a special account, where it sits and earns interest until you actually leave.

*[Light humor]* So in a sense, you're getting paid twice for the same job — your salary, plus your pension piling up in the background. It's one of the few times in life the math works in your favor, so let's make sure we don't fumble it at the end.

Let me make it concrete with a simple picture.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 5:]** "The DROP Bucket." Show a paycheck arrow continuing on one side, and a bucket labeled "DROP Account" filling up monthly on the other, with a small "+ interest" tag.

Think of two streams. Stream one is your salary — that keeps coming, same as always. Stream two is your monthly pension benefit — but instead of going to *you*, it drips into that DROP bucket, month after month, and earns interest along the way.

A couple of things worth knowing about that bucket:

First — **the time limit.** When DROP started, members could stay in for up to five years. Under more recent legislation, that window was **extended to up to eight years — 96 months** — for many members. *[Verify-on-MyFRS beat]* But the exact rules can depend on when you entered and your role, so confirm your own window on MyFRS.com.

Second — **the interest.** That DROP money earns a set rate while it sits. That rate was raised in recent years, which is good news for the bucket. But — again — the current rate and how it applies is something to confirm for your specific situation, because these numbers do get adjusted by the legislature.

*[Takeaway]* So the takeaway from this section is simple: **DROP lets you keep working while your pension accumulates in a separate, interest-earning account.** It's a benefit. The trick is knowing what to do when the clock runs out — which is exactly where we're headed.

### [SLIDE 6 — The Clock] · 11:00–15:30 · Teaching Section 2

Let's talk about timing, because DROP is, at its heart, a program about time.

There are really three moments that matter.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 6:]** A horizontal timeline / clock graphic: **Entry → The DROP Years (up to 8) → Termination Date.** Put a gold marker on "Termination Date" labeled "the one you can't miss."

**Moment one — entry.** You generally become eligible to enter DROP once you reach your normal retirement date. There's a window around that eligibility, and entering on time matters, because the clock on your maximum participation starts ticking from your entry.

**Moment two — the DROP years.** This is the stretch where you're working and the bucket is filling. Up to eight years for many members. You can choose to stay the full period or leave earlier.

**Moment three — the termination date.** This is the big one. At the end of DROP, your participation *ends*, and to receive your benefits, you generally must **terminate FRS-covered employment.** That's a real, hard requirement. This is the date everything points to.

*[Stress-relief beat]* Now, that can sound a little dramatic — "the date everything points to." But here's the reassuring part: it's a *known* date. You're not getting ambushed. You can see it coming from years away, which means you have time to plan. And planning ahead of a known date is just about the most controllable situation in all of financial planning.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **DROP has a defined start and a defined, non-negotiable end.** Mark your termination date, and work backward from it. Everything we talk about next is about being ready for that day.

### [SLIDE 7 — What Happens to the Money] · 15:30–20:00 · Teaching Section 3

So the clock runs out, your termination date arrives, and two things happen.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 7:]** Two arrows leaving the DROP bucket: one labeled "Monthly pension begins (for life)," one labeled "DROP lump sum (one-time)."

**Thing one:** your monthly pension finally turns on. All that benefit that was dripping into the bucket? Now it starts coming to *you*, every month, for the rest of your life. That part is relatively automatic.

**Thing two:** that bucket — all the accumulated payments plus interest — becomes available to you as a **one-time lump sum.** And depending on your years of service and your benefit, this can be a *significant* amount of money. For a lot of FRS retirees, it's the single largest sum they'll ever have in their hands at one time.

*[Light humor]* Which is exciting — and also exactly the kind of moment where it's easy to make a quick decision you'll be living with for twenty years. So let's slow down and make a *good* one instead.

*[Stress-relief beat]* And remember — **the goal here isn't perfection. It's clarity.** You don't need to optimize every dollar to the penny. You just need to understand your options well enough to choose with confidence.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **at DROP exit, your monthly pension begins and your accumulated DROP money becomes a one-time lump sum.** That lump sum is the decision. Let's look at what you can actually do with it.

### [SLIDE 8 — Your Options for the Lump Sum] · 20:00–27:00 · Teaching Section 4 *(the core)*

This is the heart of the whole webinar, so let's take our time.

When that DROP lump sum becomes available, you generally have two broad paths.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 8:]** A fork in the road. Left path: "Take it as cash." Right path: "Roll it over." Use a simple decision-tree style.

**Path one — take it as cash.**

You can have the money paid out to you directly. Simple, and the money's in your hands.

But there's a catch worth understanding, and it's all about **taxes.** This DROP money is generally **pre-tax** — meaning it hasn't been taxed yet. So when you take it as cash, the IRS generally treats it as **ordinary income in that year.**

*[Plain-English example]* Here's why that matters. Imagine your DROP lump sum is a large number — let's just say it's a six-figure amount. If you take it all as cash in one year, that entire amount can get *stacked on top* of your other income for the year. And because our tax system has brackets, stacking a big number on top can push a chunk of it into much higher tax rates than you normally pay. There's also typically mandatory withholding taken right off the top.

*[Light humor]* So "take all the cash" can quietly turn into "take all the cash, and give a surprisingly large slice to the IRS, and possibly bump yourself into a tax bracket you've never visited before." Not ideal.

**Path two — roll it over.**

The other path is to move the money — through what's called a **direct rollover** — into a retirement account like an **IRA** or, in some cases, another qualified plan.

When you do a direct rollover correctly, the money generally **keeps its tax-deferred status.** It's not taxed in the year you move it. It stays invested and keeps growing, and you only pay taxes later, as you withdraw it over time — ideally in smaller, more manageable amounts that you control.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 8b:]** Side-by-side: "Cash now → taxed now, possibly at higher rates" vs. "Roll over → stays tax-deferred, you control the timing of taxes."

Now — here's a nuance I want to be honest with you about, because it matters and a lot of people don't hear it until it's too late.

**If you roll the money into an IRA, *where* that IRA lives is its own decision.**

Broadly, an IRA can be held at a **brokerage custodian** — think of a transparent account where you can see your holdings, the fees are generally clear, and you have flexibility — or it can be structured inside an **annuity-based product**, which is a different animal with its own features, its own cost structure, and sometimes **surrender periods** — meaning your money can be locked up for a number of years with penalties to access it early.

I'm not here to tell you one is always right. *[Compliance beat]* What I'll say, in general terms: for many FRS retirees, a **transparent, lower-cost, flexible** home for the rollover tends to fit well. An **annuity-based IRA** can make sense in specific cases — usually when a **guaranteed lifetime income stream** is something you specifically want and it fits your overall plan. The key word is *compare.* Before money that took you a whole career to earn goes into a product with a multi-year surrender period, you should understand exactly what you're getting and what you're giving up.

*[Stress-relief beat]* If that felt like a lot — that's okay. You don't have to memorize it. The one thing to take with you is this: **you have options, the options have trade-offs, and the trade-offs are knowable.** That's a manageable situation.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **cash now means taxes now, possibly at higher rates. A direct rollover preserves tax deferral and lets you control the timing. And if you roll over, where the IRA lives — and what it costs — is a decision worth comparing carefully.**

### [SLIDE 9 — How It All Connects] · 27:00–32:30 · Teaching Section 5

DROP doesn't happen in a vacuum. Your exit collides with a few other big pieces, and a little coordination goes a long way.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 9:]** A simple puzzle graphic with four interlocking pieces: "DROP Exit," "Taxes," "Social Security," "Medicare." Show them fitting together.

**Piece one — taxes and timing.** We just covered this, but zoom out: the *year* you exit and what you do with the lump sum can affect your tax picture. Sometimes there's value in being thoughtful about timing — what income lands in which year. This is a great example of something to map out *before* the year arrives, not after.

**Piece two — Social Security.** Your new monthly pension is income. Social Security is income. How and when you turn on Social Security can interact with your pension and your taxes. We have a whole separate session on Social Security timing, but for now, just know: **don't make your Social Security decision in isolation from your DROP exit.**

**Piece three — Medicare.** If you're turning 65 around the same time you're leaving, Medicare enrollment has its own clock and its own rules — and getting the timing wrong can cost you for life. Again, separate session, but flag it now: **65 has its own to-do list.**

**Piece four — the Health Insurance Subsidy and later RMDs.** There's a Health Insurance Subsidy many retirees receive that helps with coverage costs, and down the road, money sitting in tax-deferred accounts will eventually face Required Minimum Distributions — rules about taking money out later in life. None of this is urgent today; it's just on the radar.

*[Stress-relief beat]* Here's the comforting truth underneath all of this: **these pieces connect in predictable ways.** When you see them as one picture instead of five separate fires, the stress usually starts to come down. That's the whole point of planning ahead — turning a pile of unknowns into a sequence of knowns.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **your DROP exit touches taxes, Social Security, Medicare, and more — and it pays to look at them together.**

### [SLIDE 10 — Common Mistakes] · 32:30–38:00 · Teaching Section 6

Let me save you some pain. Here are five patterns I see that quietly cost FRS retirees real money — and how to avoid each one.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 10:]** Traffic-light graphic, or a "five red flags" list with gold warning icons. One line each.

**Mistake one — taking the whole lump sum as cash without comparing.** As we discussed, this can trigger a big, avoidable tax bill in one year. *The fix:* understand the rollover option before you decide.

**Mistake two — rolling into a high-cost or surrender-locked product without comparing alternatives.** Sometimes the right fit, often not — but you can't know without comparing. *The fix:* know the fees and the surrender terms before you sign anything.

**Mistake three — bad tax-year timing.** Stacking too much into one year when spreading it out, or coordinating with other income, might have served you better. *The fix:* think about the calendar, not just the dollars.

**Mistake four — not planning for the income transition.** Your paycheck stops; your pension and other income start. There can be a gap or a change in cash flow. *The fix:* map your monthly income before and after, so there are no surprises.

**Mistake five — forgetting the 65 / Social Security coordination.** Treating DROP as a standalone event and missing Medicare windows or Social Security timing. *The fix:* look at the whole picture, not just the DROP money.

*[Light humor]* If you avoid those five, you're already ahead of a *lot* of very smart people who just didn't know what they didn't know. And now you do.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **most DROP-exit regret comes from rushing the lump-sum decision and ignoring the connected pieces.** Slow down, compare, and zoom out.

### [SLIDE 11 — How to Decide / Questions to Ask] · 38:00–40:30 · Recap + Framework

So how do you actually make a good decision? Start with a few honest questions.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 11:]** Four questions, gold numbers, plenty of white space.

1. **What does my income look like the month after I leave** — and is there a gap?
2. **If I roll over the lump sum, what will it cost me, and how flexible is it?**
3. **What's the tax impact of my choice this year, and could timing help?**
4. **How does this fit with my Social Security and Medicare decisions?**

If you can answer those four with confidence, you're in great shape. If you can't yet — that's completely normal, and it's exactly what a good review is for.
