### [SLIDE 5 — What the 2nd Election Is] · 5:30–10:30 · Teaching Section 1

Let's define it clearly, because the name is a little confusing.

When you first enrolled in FRS, you made — or were defaulted into — a choice between the **Pension Plan** and the **Investment Plan.** That was your *first* election.

The **2nd Election** is exactly what it sounds like: a *second*, one-time opportunity to **switch** from whichever plan you're in to the other one. Most active members get this option once during their working career.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 5:]** A single switch/lever graphic, with a gold tag: "You get this ONCE." On either side: "Pension Plan ⇄ Investment Plan."

Two things to burn into memory:

**First — it's generally one-time, one-and-done.** Use it to move from Pension to Investment, or from Investment to Pension — but generally, *once you've used it, that's it.* You don't get to keep flipping back and forth. *[Verify-on-MyFRS beat]* The exact eligibility and process can depend on your circumstances, so confirm yours on MyFRS.com.

**Second — you have to be *active* to use it.** This is a tool for people *still working* in an FRS-covered job. It's not a retirement-day decision; it's a career decision. Which means if it's ever going to be relevant to you, the time to *understand* it is well before you retire — not the week you're walking out the door.

*[Light humor]* So think of the 2nd Election like a "get out of one plan free" card — except you only have one, you can't trade it, and you really want to read the fine print before you play it. No pressure.

*[Stress-relief beat]* And to be clear — *most members never use it, and that's completely fine.* The default isn't a mistake. The point of today isn't to convince you to switch. It's to make sure that *if* a switch would genuinely serve you, you actually know the option exists and how to think about it.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **the 2nd Election is a one-time, generally one-and-done chance to switch plans while you're still working. Most people never use it — but everyone should understand it.**

### [SLIDE 6 — A Quick Refresher: The Two Plans] · 10:30–15:30 · Teaching Section 2

To weigh a switch, you have to understand what you'd be switching *between.* We have a whole separate session on this, so I'll keep it tight here.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 6:]** Two columns. Left: "Pension Plan — guaranteed lifetime paycheck." Right: "Investment Plan — your own account."

**The Pension Plan** is a **defined benefit** plan. Plain version: a **guaranteed monthly paycheck for life**, calculated by a formula — your years of service, your class's accrual rate, and your final-years salary. You don't manage investments. The **state carries the market risk.** The check shows up every month, for life, no matter how long you live.

**The Investment Plan** is a **defined contribution** plan. Plain version: **your own account**, like a 401(k), with a balance you direct and can see. **You carry the market risk — and keep the upside.** It vests faster, it's **portable** if you leave, and it's **inheritable** — whatever's in it passes to your family. But there's **no guaranteed lifetime income;** making it last is your responsibility.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 6b:]** A simple seesaw labeled "Who bears the investment risk?" — Pension: "The state." Investment: "You."

So the cleanest way to hold it in your head: one plan hands you a **paycheck you can't outlive but can't control.** The other hands you a **pot of money you control but could outlive.** That tension *is* the 2nd Election decision.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **Pension = guaranteed income, state bears the risk. Investment = your own portable, inheritable balance, you bear the risk. Switching means trading one set of trade-offs for the other.**

---

### [SLIDE 7 — The Core Trade-Off] · 15:30–20:00 · Teaching Section 3

Let's frame the actual decision, because a switch is really a choice between two very different *kinds* of security.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 7:]** A balance scale. One side: "Certainty — a check for life." Other side: "Control — a balance that's yours."

Here's the core trade-off in one sentence: **switching to the Pension Plan buys you certainty and gives up control; switching to the Investment Plan buys you control and gives up certainty.**

Let me make both sides real.

**Why someone might switch *toward* the Pension Plan:** they've realized they value a **paycheck they never have to manage or worry about**, they expect a **long retirement**, they have a spouse to protect with survivor options, and the idea of watching a balance rise and fall in retirement gives them heartburn. For that person, the guaranteed-income side is deeply appealing.

**Why someone might switch *toward* the Investment Plan:** they want **flexibility and control**, they may **not stay in FRS-covered work** for a full career, they want to be able to **leave a balance to their family**, and they're **comfortable with markets** and want the potential upside. For that person, portability and ownership win.

*[Plain-English framing]* Notice these aren't "smart vs. dumb" profiles. They're *different people with different lives.* The whole question is: **which kind of security helps *you* sleep at night — a paycheck you can't outlive, or a balance that's truly yours?**

*[Stress-relief beat]* If you don't know your answer yet, that's not a problem to fix in five minutes. It's a question to sit with. The goal isn't certainty today — it's clarity over time. And it's a *lot* easier to find that clarity when you understand the trade-off as well as you do right now.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **a switch trades certainty for control, or control for certainty — and the right direction depends entirely on which kind of security fits your life.**

### [SLIDE 8 — How a Switch Works (and the "Buy-In")] · 20:00–25:30 · Teaching Section 4

Now the mechanics — including one piece that surprises people and genuinely matters: the **buy-in.**

> **[VISUAL — Slide 8:]** Two arrows. Arrow 1: "Pension → Investment" with a note "your benefit's present value transfers." Arrow 2: "Investment → Pension" with a gold flag: "may require a BUY-IN."

When you use your 2nd Election, the money has to move between two very different structures, and the direction changes what happens.

**Switching from the Pension Plan *to* the Investment Plan:** generally, the calculated **present value** of your accumulated pension benefit transfers into your new Investment Plan account as your opening balance. You're essentially converting a future stream of guaranteed payments into a lump-sum balance you now control.

**Switching from the Investment Plan *to* the Pension Plan:** this is the one to understand carefully. Because the Pension Plan promises a guaranteed lifetime benefit, **buying into it can require paying a "buy-in" amount** — an actuarially-determined cost to purchase that pension benefit. If your Investment Plan balance doesn't fully cover what it costs to buy the pension service, you may need to **make up the difference out of pocket.** That can be a significant number, and it catches people off guard.

*[Plain-English framing]* Think of it this way: a guaranteed lifetime income isn't free — *someone* has to fund the promise. When you switch *into* the Pension Plan, FRS calculates what that promise is worth, and your account has to cover it — or you do. It's not a reason *not* to switch. It's just a number you absolutely want to know *before* you decide, not after.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 8b:]** Screenshot placeholder of the **MyFRS 2nd Election Choice calculator** with a caption: "This tool estimates your cost and outcome — run it before deciding."

And here's the good news on the mechanics: **MyFRS has a dedicated 2nd Election calculator** built for exactly this. It estimates what a switch would mean for *you* — including any buy-in. *[Compliance beat]* Reading that output correctly is genuinely where a lot of people get stuck, and it's one of the most useful things to walk through with someone before you make a one-time, permanent decision.

*[Stress-relief beat]* I know "actuarially-determined buy-in" is about the least friendly phrase in this whole webinar. *[Light]* But you don't have to *calculate* it — you just have to *know to look for it.* The tool does the math. Your only job is to not be surprised, and to understand what you're looking at.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **switching to the Investment Plan generally transfers your benefit's present value; switching to the Pension Plan may require a buy-in cost. Run the MyFRS 2nd Election calculator to see your real numbers before you decide.**

### [SLIDE 9 — When a Switch Might Be Worth Considering] · 25:30–31:00 · Teaching Section 5

So when does this one-time card actually make sense to play? Let me give you the honest landscape, and then an illustrative example.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 9:]** A short "considerations" list with gold check icons: Life stage · Health & longevity · Family situation · Control vs. guarantee · Career plans.

A switch tends to deserve a serious look when **something real has changed** — not a market mood, but a *life* fact:

- Your **career plans** changed — maybe you're now likely to leave FRS-covered work, which makes portability more valuable.
- Your **health or longevity expectations** changed — which affects how much you value a guaranteed lifetime check.
- Your **family situation** changed — a desire to leave a balance to heirs, or a need to protect a spouse.
- Your **relationship to risk and control** has clarified — you've learned, with age, which kind of security actually lets you sleep.

*[Illustrative example — education only]* Let me paint a composite picture — and to be clear, this is a *made-up illustration*, not a recommendation. Imagine a mid-career teacher, originally defaulted into one plan years ago without thinking much about it. Life has shifted: they now expect a long, healthy retirement, they have a spouse they want to protect, and the idea of managing investments in their seventies genuinely stresses them out. For *that* person, the guaranteed-income side of the trade-off might be worth a hard look — *if* the numbers, including any buy-in, support it. A *different* composite — say, someone who's likely to leave public employment in a few years and wants a portable, inheritable balance — might lean the other way entirely.

Same election, opposite answers. **Because it was never about the plan — it's about the person.**

*[Stress-relief beat]* If you're listening and thinking, "I'm honestly not sure something's changed enough to justify using my one switch" — that's a *perfectly good* conclusion. Doing nothing is a legitimate, often wise choice. The election doesn't expire into a mistake just because you didn't use it.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **a switch is worth considering when a real life change — career, health, family, or risk comfort — shifts which kind of security fits you, and the numbers support it. Same election, different right answers for different people.**

### [SLIDE 10 — Common Mistakes] · 31:00–36:00 · Teaching Section 6

Let me save you some pain. Five patterns that quietly cost FRS members — and how to avoid each.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 10:]** "Five red flags" list, gold warning icons.

**Mistake one — using your one switch impulsively** — reacting to a scary market headline or a coworker's strong opinion. *The fix:* switch for a *life* reason, not a mood. It's permanent.

**Mistake two — never even considering it** — not realizing the option exists until it's irrelevant. *The fix:* understand it now, so you can choose on purpose, even if the choice is to do nothing.

**Mistake three — misreading the calculator output** — especially missing or misunderstanding the buy-in. *The fix:* get a second set of eyes on the numbers before you act.

**Mistake four — ignoring longevity and family** — switching to the Investment Plan for the upside while forgetting how long retirement lasts or that a spouse needs protection. *The fix:* weigh the survivor and longevity angle, not just the balance.

**Mistake five — going it completely alone on a permanent decision.** *The fix:* for a one-and-done choice this consequential, it's worth talking it through with someone before you finalize.

*[Light humor]* You only get one of these elections in your entire career. That's not a decision to make on a Tuesday because you saw a headline you didn't like. Treat it with the weight it deserves and you'll be just fine.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **most 2nd Election regret comes from acting on emotion or never understanding the option at all. Switch for a real reason, read the numbers carefully, and don't rush a permanent decision.**
