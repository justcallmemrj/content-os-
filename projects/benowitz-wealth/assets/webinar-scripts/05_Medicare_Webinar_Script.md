### [SLIDE 5 — The Four Parts of Medicare] · 6:00–11:30 · Teaching Section 1

Let's demystify the alphabet, because once you see it, it's honestly not that complicated.

Medicare comes in four parts, and the easiest way to remember them is by what each one *does.*

> **[VISUAL — Slide 5:]** A four-piece puzzle, each piece labeled: **A — Hospital · B — Medical · C — All-in-one (Advantage) · D — Drugs.**

**Part A — Hospital.** This covers inpatient hospital stays, some skilled nursing, hospice. For most people who've worked and paid Medicare taxes long enough, **Part A is premium-free** — you've already paid for it through your career. Think: "A is for *Admitted* to the hospital."

**Part B — Medical.** This covers doctor visits, outpatient care, preventive services, lab work. Part B has a **monthly premium.** Think: "B is for *Being* at the doctor's office."

**Part C — Medicare Advantage.** This is an *alternative* way to get your Medicare, offered by private insurers, that bundles A and B — and often D — into one plan, frequently with extra features. Think of C as "an all-in-one *combo* option." *[Compliance beat]* Whether a Part C plan fits you is exactly the kind of choice a **licensed Medicare specialist** helps you make — not me.

**Part D — Drugs.** This covers prescription medications. Think: "D is for *Drugs.*"

And you'll also hear about **Medicare Supplement plans**, sometimes called "Medigap" — private plans that help cover costs Original Medicare leaves to you. *[Compliance beat]* Again — whether you want a Supplement, and which one, is a **specialist** conversation.

*[Light humor]* So that's the whole alphabet: A admits you, B is the doctor, C combines things, D is drugs. Four letters, and you already understand them better than half the people walking into Medicare blind.

*[Stress-relief beat]* And notice — you don't have to *choose* between these letters in some confusing way today. The point right now is just to know what each one *is.* When you understand the moving pieces, the stress comes down.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **A is hospital, B is medical, C is an all-in-one private alternative, D is drugs — and which specific plans fit you is a licensed specialist's job, not mine.**

### [SLIDE 6 — The Enrollment Windows] · 11:30–16:30 · Teaching Section 2

Now timing — because *when* you enroll is where the real money is, and where the real mistakes happen.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 6:]** A 7-month clock/timeline centered on the 65th-birthday month: 3 months before · birthday month · 3 months after. Label it "Initial Enrollment Period."

The main window is your **Initial Enrollment Period.** It's **seven months long**, centered on the month you turn **65** — the three months before, your birthday month, and the three months after. For most people, this is *the* window to get enrolled without hassle or penalty.

There are a couple of other windows worth naming:

**The General Enrollment Period** — for people who missed their initial window. It exists, but enrolling late this way can come with **penalties and gaps in coverage**, which is exactly what we want to help you avoid.

**A Special Enrollment Period** — this is for people who delayed Medicare because they had **active, current employer coverage** through a job. *And this is where I need to slow way down*, because it's the single most misunderstood point in all of Medicare for retirees — and it's coming up on the very next slide.

*[Stress-relief beat]* Here's the reassuring part about all of this: your 65th birthday is not a surprise. You can see this window coming from years away, which means you have time to coordinate it properly. A known deadline is a *manageable* deadline.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **your Initial Enrollment Period is a seven-month window around your 65th birthday — and it's the cleanest time to enroll. Missing it can cause penalties and gaps.**

### [SLIDE 7 — THE Big Mistake] · 16:30–22:00 · Teaching Section 3 *(the most important slide)*

This is the most important five minutes of the whole session. If you remember nothing else, remember *this.*

> **[VISUAL — Slide 7:]** A bold warning slide. Headline: "FRS retiree health coverage does NOT let you delay Medicare." A red "STOP" or warning icon. Keep it stark and clear.

Here's the mistake I see cost FRS retirees real money, over and over:

People assume that because they have **retiree health coverage** through FRS or their former employer, they can safely **wait** to sign up for Medicare Part B — the way someone with an *active* job can. And that assumption is, for most people, **wrong.**

Let me explain the trap. The rule that lets you *delay* Medicare without penalty is generally tied to having **active, current employment coverage** — coverage from a job *you or your spouse are still working at.* **Retiree** coverage is a *different* animal. In most cases, **retiree coverage does *not* count** as the kind of active coverage that lets you delay Part B.

So what happens? Someone retires, keeps their FRS retiree health plan, figures "I'm covered, I'll deal with Medicare later," lets their seven-month window slip by — and then discovers two painful things: a **lifelong late-enrollment penalty**, and potentially a **gap in coverage** while they wait for the next enrollment window.

*[Emphasis — say it slowly]* So let me say it as plainly as I can: **having FRS retiree coverage is generally *not* a reason to delay enrolling in Medicare at 65.** For most retirees, you should plan to enroll during your Initial Enrollment Period *even if* you still have retiree coverage. *[Compliance beat]* But — because your specific coverage and situation matter enormously here — **confirm exactly how your coverage coordinates with Medicare with a licensed Medicare specialist and your plan administrator before you decide to delay anything.**

*[Light humor]* I know I just said the same thing about four times. That's on purpose. This is the one that gets people, and I would much rather over-say it now than have you learn it the expensive way later.

*[Stress-relief beat]* And here's the genuinely good news: this mistake is **completely avoidable.** It only happens to people who don't know the rule. You now know the rule. So for you, this is simply a checked box, not a landmine.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **retiree coverage is not active employer coverage. For most FRS retirees, it does *not* let you delay Medicare — so plan to enroll at 65, and confirm your situation with a Medicare specialist.**

### [SLIDE 8 — Late-Enrollment Penalties] · 22:00–26:00 · Teaching Section 4

Let's look at *why* that timing matters so much — the penalties — because understanding the cost makes the deadline feel real.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 8:]** A simple graphic showing a premium amount with a growing "penalty" stacked on top, with a "for life" tag.

Here's the part that surprises people: some Medicare late penalties aren't one-time fees — they're **permanent.**

**The Part B late-enrollment penalty:** if you don't sign up for Part B when you're first eligible — and you don't qualify for a valid delay — your **monthly Part B premium can be permanently increased.** The penalty generally grows the longer you wait, and then it's added to your premium **for as long as you have Part B** — which, for most people, is the rest of their life.

**The Part D late-enrollment penalty:** there's a similar idea for prescription drug coverage. Go too long without creditable drug coverage, and a penalty can be tacked onto your Part D premium, also on an ongoing basis.

*[Plain-English framing]* So a late enrollment isn't like a parking ticket you pay once and forget. It's more like a small, permanent surcharge that rides along with your premium for decades. Individually each month it might look modest — but stretch it across a long retirement and it adds up to real money, all of it avoidable.

*[Stress-relief beat]* Which is *exactly* why we spend time on timing. Not to scare you — but because this is one of those rare cases where simply *knowing the deadline* protects you completely. The penalty only exists for people who didn't see it coming. You see it coming.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **late enrollment in Part B and Part D can create permanent, ongoing premium increases — which is why hitting your enrollment window matters so much.**

### [SLIDE 9 — IRMAA: The Income Surcharge] · 26:00–31:30 · Teaching Section 5 *(FRS-specific)*

Now here's a piece that's *especially* relevant to FRS retirees, and it connects directly to something we cover in our DROP webinar. It's called **IRMAA.**

> **[VISUAL — Slide 9:]** A staircase graphic where higher income tiers = higher premium surcharges. Label it "IRMAA — higher income, higher Part B & D premiums." Add a callout: "Based on income from ~2 years prior."

IRMAA stands for the **Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount.** Plain version: **if your income is above certain levels, you pay a *surcharge* on top of your normal Part B and Part D premiums.** Higher income, higher premium. It's a tiered staircase.

And here's the twist that catches retirees off guard — two of them, actually.

**Twist one — it looks back about two years.** IRMAA is generally based on your income from roughly **two years prior.** So the premium you pay this year is often based on a tax return from a couple of years ago.

**Twist two — and this is the FRS connection — a big one-time income event can spike it.** Remember the **DROP lump sum** we talk about? Or a large withdrawal from a tax-deferred account? If that lands in a single tax year and pushes your income up, it can **bump you into a higher IRMAA tier** — meaning higher Medicare premiums, showing up about two years later, sometimes for just that period.

*[Plain-English framing]* So picture this: you exit DROP, take a big chunk as taxable income in one year without planning around it — and two years later, you open your Medicare statement and your premium is *higher* than you expected, and you have no idea why. That "why" is IRMAA. **This is precisely why the DROP decision and the Medicare decision shouldn't be made in separate rooms.** They talk to each other, even if you don't realize it.

*[Compliance + stress-relief beat]* Now — there *are* ways to plan around income timing, and in certain life-change situations IRMAA can even be appealed. The *planning* around your income — what lands in which year — is squarely in my lane, and it's a great example of where coordinating your DROP exit, your withdrawals, and your Medicare timing together can genuinely save money. The Medicare *enrollment* itself stays with your specialist. Two lanes, working together.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **IRMAA is an income-based surcharge on Part B and D, based on income from about two years prior — and a DROP lump sum or large withdrawal can trigger it. Coordinating your income timing is exactly the kind of planning worth doing in advance.**

### [SLIDE 10 — Coordinating With Your FRS Picture] · 31:30–36:00 · Teaching Section 6

Let's connect Medicare to the rest of your FRS retirement, because that's where I'm genuinely useful.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 10:]** A puzzle with interlocking pieces: "Medicare," "Social Security," "Pension," "HIS (Health Insurance Subsidy)."

**Piece one — Social Security auto-enrollment.** Here's a handy interaction: if you're already **receiving Social Security** when you turn 65, you generally get **automatically enrolled** in Medicare Part A and Part B — it just happens. If you're *not* yet drawing Social Security, you typically have to **enroll yourself.** That's a key distinction — because if you've used our Social Security webinar's strategy and *delayed* your benefit, that means you can't rely on auto-enrollment, and you'll need to sign yourself up on time. The strategies connect.

**Piece two — the Health Insurance Subsidy, or HIS.** Many FRS retirees receive a monthly **Health Insurance Subsidy** to help offset the cost of health coverage, including in retirement. It's a per-year-of-service benefit, up to a cap. *[Verify-on-MyFRS beat]* The amount and rules are set by the legislature and can change, so confirm your figure on MyFRS.com — but it's a real piece of your coverage budget worth knowing about.

**Piece three — timing retirement around 65.** If your retirement date and your 65th birthday are near each other, the *sequence* matters — when coverage ends, when Medicare begins, how you avoid a gap. Lining those up is coordination work, and it's worth mapping in advance.

*[Stress-relief beat]* Here's the underlying comfort, the same one that runs through all our sessions: **these pieces connect in predictable ways.** Social Security, your pension, the subsidy, Medicare — they're one picture, not four separate emergencies. When you see them together, the stress comes down. That's the whole value of planning ahead.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **Medicare coordinates with Social Security auto-enrollment, your Health Insurance Subsidy, and your retirement timing — and lining them up in advance is exactly the planning work worth doing.**

### [SLIDE 12 — Common Mistakes] · 38:30–41:30 · Teaching Section 8

Let me save you some pain. Five patterns that quietly cost FRS retirees — and how to avoid each.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 12:]** "Five red flags" list, gold warning icons.

**Mistake one — assuming retiree coverage lets you delay Part B.** The big one. *The fix:* plan to enroll at 65 unless a specialist confirms a valid delay.

**Mistake two — missing your Initial Enrollment Period.** *The fix:* mark the seven-month window around your 65th birthday now.

**Mistake three — ignoring IRMAA when taking the DROP lump sum.** *The fix:* coordinate your income timing across DROP, withdrawals, and Medicare.

**Mistake four — assuming auto-enrollment when you've delayed Social Security.** *The fix:* if you're not drawing Social Security at 65, plan to enroll in Medicare yourself.

**Mistake five — picking a plan without a licensed specialist** — or taking plan advice from someone who isn't licensed. *The fix:* enrollment and plan choice belong with a licensed Medicare specialist.

*[Light humor]* Avoid those five and you'll glide into Medicare while half your former coworkers are still trying to figure out what the letters stand for.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **most Medicare regret comes from delaying when you shouldn't and ignoring how income and timing connect. Enroll on time, coordinate the income, and use a licensed specialist for the plan.**
