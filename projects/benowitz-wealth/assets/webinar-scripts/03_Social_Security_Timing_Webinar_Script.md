### [SLIDE 5 — The Basics] · 5:30–11:00 · Teaching Section 1

Let's start by clearing up the single most common confusion I hear.

A lot of public employees assume, "I have a pension, so I probably don't get Social Security." And in some states, for some workers, that's been partly true. But here's the key fact for **most FRS members:**

> **[VISUAL — Slide 5:]** Big, reassuring statement: "Most FRS members DO pay into Social Security — which means you likely have a benefit."

**Most FRS-covered employment *does* pay into Social Security.** Which means you've likely been earning Social Security credits your whole career — *and* building an FRS pension. Two streams, not one.

*[Light humor]* So if you've been quietly assuming Social Security forgot about you — good news, it didn't. You've probably got a benefit waiting. We just want to claim it at the smartest moment.

Now let's cover the three numbers that matter.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 5b:]** A simple staircase / ladder graphic: **Age 62 (smaller) → Full Retirement Age (your "100%") → Age 70 (largest)**, each step taller.

**Number one — your Full Retirement Age, or FRA.** This is the age at which you get your "full" benefit — your hundred-percent number. For most people retiring today, it's in the **66-to-67** range, depending on your birth year.

**Number two — age 62.** This is the *earliest* most people can claim. But claiming early comes at a cost: your check is **permanently reduced** — often by around **25 to 30 percent** compared to your full benefit. Permanently. Not just until FRA — for life.

**Number three — age 70.** This is the latest it makes sense to wait. For every year you delay *past* your Full Retirement Age, your benefit grows by about **eight percent per year**, up until 70. That's a guaranteed, inflation-adjusted increase you can't easily get anywhere else.

*[Plain-English framing]* So picture a staircase. Claim at 62, you stand on a lower step — for life. Claim at your Full Retirement Age, you're on your "100%" step. Wait until 70, you're on the highest step — a check that can be **substantially** larger than the age-62 version, every month, for the rest of your life, with cost-of-living raises layered on top.

*[Stress-relief beat]* Now, I want to be clear — *waiting isn't automatically "right."* We'll talk about that. The point right now is just to *see the staircase*, so you understand that this is a real choice with real consequences. When you understand the moving pieces, the stress comes down.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **most FRS members have a Social Security benefit, and *when* you claim — anywhere from 62 to 70 — permanently changes its size.**

### [SLIDE 6 — The Big Lever: Timing] · 11:00–16:00 · Teaching Section 2

Let's go deeper on timing, because it's the whole game.

The classic way people think about this is **"break-even."** The logic: if I claim early, I get smaller checks but *more* of them. If I wait, I get bigger checks but *fewer.* At some age, the totals cross — that's your break-even point.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 6:]** Two lines on a simple chart that cross at a "break-even" point — one labeled "Claim early," one "Claim later." Keep it clean.

And break-even is useful. But honestly, it's only *part* of the picture, and leaning on it alone leads people astray. Here's the bigger way to think about it.

Delaying Social Security isn't really a bet on *living past a break-even age.* It's **buying yourself the largest possible guaranteed, inflation-adjusted income for the rest of your life — and for your spouse's life too.** It's longevity insurance. And the thing about longevity is: the *risk* isn't dying early — financially, that takes care of itself. The risk is living a *long* time and running short. A bigger Social Security check is one of the best protections against exactly that.

So who might lean toward claiming *earlier?* In general — someone in poor health, someone who genuinely needs the income now, or someone without a spouse to protect. Who might lean toward *waiting?* Someone in good health, with **other income to live on in the meantime**, and a spouse they want to protect.

And that phrase — *other income to live on in the meantime* — is the magic phrase for FRS retirees. Which brings us to the most powerful idea in this whole session.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **timing isn't just a break-even bet — it's about locking in the biggest lifetime, inflation-protected income. And the ability to wait depends on having income to bridge the gap.**

### [SLIDE 7 — How Your Pension Can Fund a Delay] · 16:00–21:00 · Teaching Section 3 *(the key insight)*

This is the part I most want FRS retirees to hear, because almost nobody connects these two dots.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 7:]** A bridge graphic: on the left, "Retirement Day." On the right, "Age 70 — biggest Social Security check." The bridge spanning the gap is labeled "Your FRS pension + DROP money."

Here's the move. Most retirees feel they *have* to claim Social Security the day they stop working, because they need the income. That pressure is what pushes people to claim early and lock in a smaller check forever.

But you — as an FRS retiree — often have something most people don't: **a pension that starts paying you right away.** And maybe a **DROP lump sum** on top of it. That income can act as a **bridge** — it carries your living expenses for a few years, which *frees you up* to delay Social Security and let it grow to that bigger, age-70 number.

*[Plain-English example]* Think of it like this. Imagine you retire and your pension covers most of your monthly needs. Instead of grabbing Social Security at 62 at a permanent discount, you live on the pension — and let Social Security keep climbing about eight percent a year. By the time you turn it on, you've locked in a *much* larger check, for life, with cost-of-living raises compounding on the bigger number. You essentially used your pension to *buy* a bigger Social Security benefit.

*[Stress-relief beat]* Now — does this work for everyone? No. It depends on your health, your full income picture, your spouse, and your comfort. *[Compliance beat]* I'm not telling you to do it; I'm showing you it's *possible*, because most people don't even know it's on the menu. And you can't choose an option you don't know exists.

*[Light humor]* It's one of the rare cases where having a pension doesn't just give you income — it gives you *strategy.* Two superpowers for the price of one career.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **your FRS pension and any DROP money can bridge your expenses, giving you the option to delay Social Security and lock in a larger lifetime check — a move most retirees can't make.**

### [SLIDE 8 — Spousal & Survivor Benefits] · 21:00–26:00 · Teaching Section 4

Now let's talk about the person sitting next to you — because Social Security isn't just an individual decision when you're married. It's a *team* decision.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 8:]** Two figures with their benefits; an arrow showing the higher benefit "continuing" to the survivor.

Here's the rule that drives everything for couples: **when one spouse passes away, the survivor generally keeps the *higher* of the two Social Security benefits — not both.** The smaller one goes away.

Sit with that for a second, because it has a huge implication.

It means the **higher earner's claiming decision doesn't just affect them — it sets the floor for the survivor for the rest of their life.** If the higher earner claims early and locks in a smaller check, and then passes away first, the surviving spouse is left with that smaller amount — *permanently.* But if the higher earner *waits* and locks in the biggest possible check, that larger amount is what protects the survivor.

*[Plain-English framing]* So one of the most loving, practical things a higher-earning spouse can do is treat their own claiming decision as **survivor protection.** Waiting isn't just about you — it's about making sure that if you go first, the person you love isn't suddenly living on a much smaller check during an already hard time.

There are also **spousal benefits** while both are living — in some situations a lower-earning spouse may be able to claim a benefit based on the higher earner's record. The rules here have gotten simpler over the years but still have nuance, so it's worth checking your specific situation.

*[Stress-relief beat]* I know this touches something tender — none of us love thinking about who goes first. But planning it *now*, calmly, together, is a gift. It takes a frightening unknown and turns it into a handled thing. That's what good planning does — it lets you stop worrying about it.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **for couples, the survivor keeps the higher benefit — so the higher earner's timing is really survivor protection for the spouse who's left.**

### [SLIDE 9 — Taxes & the Earnings Test] · 26:00–31:00 · Teaching Section 5

Two practical things people get tripped up on: taxes, and working while claiming.

**First — taxes.** A lot of people are surprised to learn Social Security can be *partly taxable.* Here's the plain version: depending on your total income, **up to 85 percent of your Social Security benefit can be subject to federal income tax.** Not 85 percent *taken* — up to 85 percent *counted* as taxable income.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 9:]** A simple "thermometer" of provisional income with thresholds, showing more of the benefit becoming taxable as other income rises.

And this is where your **pension and any IRA withdrawals matter** — because *they* count toward the income that determines how much of your Social Security gets taxed. So a retiree with a healthy pension plus large withdrawals from tax-deferred accounts can find more of their Social Security taxed than they expected. The pieces interact. *[Florida note]* The good news: Florida has **no state income tax**, so we're only talking federal here — one less thing to juggle than retirees in many other states.

**Second — the earnings test.** If you claim Social Security *before* your Full Retirement Age **and** you're still working, there's an **earnings test**: earn above a certain annual limit and Social Security will temporarily **withhold** part of your benefit. Here's the part people don't realize, though — it's not gone forever. Those withheld amounts are generally **credited back** to you later, once you reach Full Retirement Age, in the form of a higher benefit. And once you're *past* FRA, the earnings test disappears entirely — you can work and earn as much as you want with no reduction.

*[Light humor]* So if you're planning to "retire" but secretly keep working — and let's be honest, a lot of public servants can't sit still — just know the earnings test exists, so it's not a nasty surprise on your first statement.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **up to 85% of your Social Security can be taxable depending on your other income — your pension and IRA withdrawals affect it — and claiming early while working can temporarily reduce benefits under the earnings test.**

### [SLIDE 10 — A Recent Rule Change: Good News] · 31:00–34:00 · Teaching Section 6

Here's a genuinely encouraging update for public servants.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 10:]** A clean "good news" slide: "Social Security Fairness Act — WEP & GPO repealed."

For decades, there were two provisions — the **Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP)** and the **Government Pension Offset (GPO)** — that could *reduce* Social Security benefits for some people who had a government pension from work that *didn't* pay into Social Security.

In **January 2025**, the **Social Security Fairness Act** went into effect and **repealed both WEP and GPO.**

Now — let me be precise here, because precision matters. As we discussed, **most FRS members already paid into Social Security**, so for the majority, WEP and GPO were never the issue to begin with. But if *you* — or your **spouse** — had government work somewhere that *didn't* pay into Social Security, or had a pension that triggered the offset, and you saw your Social Security or spousal benefit reduced because of it — that reduction has generally been **removed.** Full benefits restored.

*[Stress-relief beat]* So if you've been carrying around an old assumption that some provision was going to chip away at your benefit — it's worth re-checking, because the landscape changed in your favor. This is the kind of detail that's easy to miss in the headlines but can mean real money for the right household.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **the 2025 Social Security Fairness Act repealed WEP and GPO — and if you or your spouse were ever affected by those, your benefit may now be higher.** Verify your specific situation with Social Security.

### [SLIDE 11 — Common Mistakes] · 34:00–38:30 · Teaching Section 7

Let me save you some pain. Five patterns that quietly cost retirees — and how to avoid each.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 11:]** "Five red flags" list, gold warning icons.

**Mistake one — claiming at 62 by default**, just because you can, without considering the lifetime cost. *The fix:* see the staircase before you choose.

**Mistake two — the higher earner claiming early and shortchanging the survivor.** *The fix:* treat the higher earner's timing as survivor protection.

**Mistake three — not using the pension as a bridge** to delay, when delaying would have served you better. *The fix:* look at your whole income picture, not just Social Security.

**Mistake four — getting surprised by taxes or the earnings test.** *The fix:* coordinate Social Security with your pension and withdrawals, and know the earnings limit if you're still working.

**Mistake five — assuming an old rule still applies** — like thinking WEP or GPO still reduces your benefit when it's been repealed. *The fix:* re-check with current information.

*[Light humor]* Dodge those five and you're handling Social Security better than a lot of people who treated the most important income decision of retirement like picking a number out of a hat.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **most Social Security regret comes from claiming on autopilot. Slow down, see the staircase, protect your spouse, and coordinate with your pension.**

### [SLIDE 12 — How to Decide / Questions to Ask] · 38:30–40:30 · Recap + Framework

So how do you actually decide *when* to claim? Start with four questions.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 12:]** Four numbered questions, white space.

1. **What's my benefit at 62, at my Full Retirement Age, and at 70** — side by side?
2. **Could my pension or DROP money bridge my expenses** so I can wait?
3. **As a couple, how does my timing protect my spouse** if I'm the higher earner?
4. **What's the tax impact**, given my pension and any withdrawals?

Answer those four honestly and the right *range* usually comes into focus. And if it doesn't — that's exactly what a good review is for.
