### [SLIDE 5 — The Two Plans in Plain English] · 5:30–11:00 · Teaching Section 1

Let's define both, simply.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 5:]** Two columns / two paths. Left: "Pension Plan — a guaranteed paycheck for life." Right: "Investment Plan — your own retirement account."

**The Pension Plan** is what's called a **defined benefit** plan. That's a fancy term, so here's the plain version: *the benefit is defined.* You're promised a specific **monthly paycheck for the rest of your life**, calculated by a formula. You don't manage any investments. You don't watch a balance go up and down. When you retire, a check shows up every month, for life — and it keeps showing up no matter how long you live or what the stock market does.

**The Investment Plan** is what's called a **defined contribution** plan. Plain version: *the contribution is defined* — money goes into an account for you — but the *outcome* depends on how it's invested and how the markets do. Think of it like a 401(k). It's **your own account**, with a balance you can see, that you direct among investment options. When you retire, the balance is *yours* — to draw down, to leave to your family, to do with as you choose.

So here's the single most important difference, and if you remember one thing today, remember this:

> **[VISUAL — Slide 5b:]** A simple seesaw / balance graphic labeled "Who bears the investment risk?" — Pension side: "FRS." Investment side: "You."

**In the Pension Plan, the *state* carries the investment risk.** Markets go up, markets go down — your monthly check is the same. **In the Investment Plan, *you* carry the investment risk — and the reward.** If your investments do well, that's yours. If they don't, that's yours too.

*[Light humor]* So in one plan, the market is *somebody else's problem.* In the other, it's *your* problem — but also your opportunity. Neither is wrong. It just depends on which kind of problem you'd rather have.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **Pension Plan = a guaranteed lifetime paycheck where the state bears the risk. Investment Plan = your own account where you bear the risk and keep the upside.** Everything else flows from that.

### [SLIDE 6 — How the Pension Benefit Is Calculated] · 11:00–16:30 · Teaching Section 2

Let's open up that Pension Plan "formula," because it sounds mysterious and it really isn't.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 6:]** The formula displayed clearly:
> **Years of Service × Accrual Rate × Average Final Compensation = Annual Benefit**

Three ingredients. Let's take them one at a time.

**Ingredient one — Years of Service.** Exactly what it sounds like: how long you worked in an FRS-covered job. More years, bigger benefit. Simple.

**Ingredient two — the Accrual Rate.** This is the percentage you earn *per year of service*, and it depends on your **class** of employment. For most general employees — the **Regular Class** — it's around one-point-six percent per year. For **Special Risk** members — think law enforcement, firefighters, certain first responders — it's higher, around three percent per year, in recognition of the demands of those careers. *[Verify-on-MyFRS beat]* Your exact rate depends on your class and service period, so confirm yours on MyFRS.com.

**Ingredient three — Average Final Compensation, or AFC.** This is the average of your highest-earning years near the end of your career — the highest five or eight years, depending on when you were hired. It's meant to capture your peak earning power.

Let me make it real with a simple, round-number example.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 6b:]** Worked example with friendly round numbers.

*[Illustrative example — education only]* Imagine a Regular Class employee with **30 years** of service, an accrual rate of about **1.6%**, and an Average Final Compensation of, say, **$50,000.** Multiply it out: 30 years, times 1.6 percent, times fifty thousand — that comes to roughly **$24,000 a year**, or about **$2,000 a month, for life.** A Special Risk member, with a higher accrual rate, would land at a meaningfully larger number for the same service and salary.

*[Stress-relief beat]* Now — you do *not* need to run this math yourself. MyFRS has calculators that do it for you. The point of showing you the formula isn't to make you a human calculator. It's so the number doesn't feel like magic. When you understand the moving pieces, the stress usually comes down.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **your pension is just three ingredients — years, your class's rate, and your final-years salary — multiplied together into a lifetime monthly check.**

### [SLIDE 7 — The Pension Payout Options] · 16:30–21:30 · Teaching Section 3

Here's a piece that doesn't get nearly enough attention, and it's one of the most important choices a retiree makes. When you start your pension, you choose **how** it pays out — and that choice affects your spouse for the rest of *their* life.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 7:]** Four options as cards, with a small "for you" / "for your survivor" icon on each.

There are generally four options. Let me give you the plain-English version of each.

**Option 1 — the maximum benefit, for your life only.** This pays the *highest* monthly amount — but here's the catch: when you pass away, the payments **stop.** Nothing continues to a spouse or beneficiary. Highest check, zero survivor protection.

**Option 2 — a guaranteed period.** You get a slightly reduced amount, but if you pass away within a set number of years of retiring, your beneficiary keeps receiving payments for the remainder of that period.

**Option 3 — 100% joint survivor.** A reduced monthly amount while you're alive — but when you pass, your spouse or designated beneficiary continues to receive **the same amount** for *their* life. Full protection for a surviving spouse.

**Option 4 — roughly two-thirds joint survivor.** A middle path: a reduced amount, and when *either* of you passes, the survivor's benefit adjusts to about two-thirds.

*[Plain-English framing]* So think of it as a dial between **"biggest check for me"** and **"protection for the person I love."** Option 1 is all check, no protection. Option 3 is the strongest protection, at the cost of a smaller monthly amount.

*[Stress-relief beat]* This is *exactly* the kind of decision that feels heavy — because it touches your family. But it's also a *knowable* decision. There's a number attached to each option, and there's a way to think it through clearly with your spouse. You don't have to guess.

*[Light humor]* And please — this is not a decision to make in the parking lot five minutes before you sign. I've seen people pick "biggest check" on instinct and not realize until later it leaves their spouse with nothing. Worth slowing down for.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **the payout option is a trade-off between a bigger check for you and protection for your survivor — and it's one of the most consequential, and most overlooked, choices you'll make.**

### [SLIDE 8 — How the Investment Plan Works] · 21:30–26:30 · Teaching Section 4

Now let's give the Investment Plan equal time, because for the right person, it's a genuinely great fit.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 8:]** A personal account graphic — a balance with a few investment "slices," labeled "Yours."

In the Investment Plan, contributions go into **your own account.** You choose from a menu of investment options — typically a range of funds across different risk levels, often including simple, professionally-built "do-it-for-me" options if you don't want to be hands-on.

A few features worth knowing:

**It vests quickly.** Generally, you become vested — meaning the money is fully yours — after a relatively short period of service, much shorter than the Pension Plan's vesting. *[Verify-on-MyFRS beat]* Confirm the current vesting rules for your situation on MyFRS.com.

**It's portable.** If you leave FRS-covered employment, the account generally goes with you. You can roll it over and keep it growing. The Pension Plan, by contrast, is built to reward a long career *within* the system.

**It's inheritable.** Whatever's in the account when you pass generally goes to your beneficiaries. That's a meaningful difference from a pure life-only pension, where payments stop at death.

But — and this is the honest other side — **there's no guaranteed lifetime paycheck.** The balance is yours, but it's also *finite.* If you spend it down, or if markets fall right as you start drawing on it — a risk called **sequence of returns** — you have to manage that. The Investment Plan gives you control, but control comes with responsibility.

*[Light humor]* It's the difference between a meal that's served to you every month, guaranteed — versus a well-stocked pantry that's entirely yours, but that you have to manage so it lasts. Some people love the pantry. Some people just want dinner to show up.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **the Investment Plan is your own portable, inheritable account that you control — with no guaranteed lifetime income, which means the responsibility for making it last is yours.**

### [SLIDE 9 — The Core Trade-Off] · 26:30–31:00 · Teaching Section 5

So let's put them side by side, because this is really one decision wearing two outfits.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 9:]** Clean side-by-side comparison table:
> | | Pension Plan | Investment Plan |
> |---|---|---|
> | Type | Guaranteed lifetime income | Your own account balance |
> | Who bears risk | The state | You |
> | Best at | Longevity protection, simplicity | Control, portability, inheritance |
> | Vesting | Longer | Shorter |
> | At death | Depends on payout option | Balance passes to heirs |

Here's the core trade-off in one sentence: **the Pension Plan trades control for certainty, and the Investment Plan trades certainty for control.**

So who tends to lean which way? In general terms — *and these are tendencies, not rules:*

People who deeply value **a paycheck they never have to think about**, who expect a **long retirement**, or who simply **don't want to manage investments**, often gravitate toward the **Pension Plan.**

People who value **flexibility**, who might **not stay in FRS-covered work for a full career**, who want to **leave a balance to family**, or who are **comfortable with markets and want the upside**, often find the **Investment Plan** appealing.

*[Stress-relief beat]* And if you're sitting there thinking "honestly, I see myself in *both* of those" — that's normal, and it's *okay.* This is a real decision with real trade-offs, which is exactly why it deserves a real conversation, not a coin flip. The goal isn't to be certain in five minutes. The goal is clarity over time.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **Pension trades control for certainty; Investment trades certainty for control. The right fit depends on your timeline, your risk comfort, your family situation, and how much you want to be involved.**

### [SLIDE 10 — A Note on the 2nd Election] · 31:00–33:00 · Teaching Section 6

One quick, important note, because people always ask: **"What if I pick one and change my mind?"**

FRS gives most members a **one-time** opportunity — called the **2nd Election** — to switch between the two plans during their career. *One time.* Generally one-and-done.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 10:]** A single "use it once" switch/lever graphic.

I'm not going to go deep on it here, because we have a whole separate session devoted to the 2nd Election. But I want you to know it *exists*, so you don't feel like today's understanding has to be permanent. There's a built-in second look — it's just a *single* one, so it's worth using thoughtfully rather than impulsively.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **you get one chance to switch plans later — the 2nd Election — so today's job is understanding, not panicking.**

### [SLIDE 11 — Common Mistakes] · 33:00–38:00 · Teaching Section 7

Let me save you some pain. Five patterns I see that quietly cost FRS members — and how to avoid each.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 11:]** "Five red flags" list, gold warning icons.

**Mistake one — choosing on gut or peer pressure.** "My coworker picked the Investment Plan, so I did too." Your coworker isn't you. *The fix:* decide based on *your* timeline and situation.

**Mistake two — ignoring the survivor option's cost.** Picking the biggest pension check on instinct and leaving a spouse unprotected. *The fix:* weigh the payout options *with* your spouse.

**Mistake three — underestimating how long retirement lasts.** People plan for twenty years and live thirty. *The fix:* respect longevity — it's a bigger factor than most expect.

**Mistake four — misjudging your own risk comfort.** Choosing the Investment Plan and then panicking in the first downturn — or choosing the Pension Plan and feeling trapped. *The fix:* be honest about how you actually react when markets drop.

**Mistake five — not using the free tools.** MyFRS has a **Choice Service** and calculators built for exactly this decision, and many people never touch them. *The fix:* run the numbers before you choose.

*[Light humor]* Avoid those five and you're already ahead of a *lot* of people who picked their entire retirement based on a five-minute conversation by the copier.

*[Takeaway]* The takeaway: **most plan regret comes from deciding on instinct instead of on your own facts. Use the tools, respect longevity, and protect your spouse.**

### [SLIDE 12 — How to Decide / Questions to Ask] · 38:00–40:30 · Recap + Framework

So how do you actually choose? Start with four honest questions.

> **[VISUAL — Slide 12:]** Four numbered questions, lots of white space.

1. **How long do I expect to stay in FRS-covered employment** — a full career, or maybe not?
2. **How do I really feel when my account balance drops** — calm, or anxious?
3. **How important is leaving a balance to my family** versus a paycheck I can't outlive?
4. **Do I *want* to manage investments**, or would I rather it just be handled?

If you can answer those four honestly, the right fit usually starts to reveal itself. And if it doesn't — that's exactly what a good review is for.
