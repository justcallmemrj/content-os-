### Introduction: Nobody explained this to you

There's a moment that happens in almost every FRS household, usually somewhere past the twenty-year mark. A form arrives, or a coworker retires, or a spouse asks a simple question over dinner — *so how much will your pension actually be?* — and the honest answer is: *I'm not sure.*

Not because anyone was careless. Because the system was never really explained. Florida's retirement system covers more than 650,000 working members across nearly a thousand employers — teachers and troopers, librarians and lieutenants, clerks and corrections officers — and it runs on rules that changed significantly in 2011, changed again in 2023, and changed again this year. Depending on the date you were hired, you and the coworker at the next desk can be working under genuinely different retirements. The paperwork assumes you know things nobody taught you. The deadlines assume you're watching clocks nobody showed you.

Meanwhile, the decisions coming toward you are not small. Whether to stay in the Pension Plan or use your one-time switch. Whether and when to enter DROP — a program that now runs up to eight years and pays 4% interest, under rules that are barely three years old. What to do with the 403(b) a salesman sold you in the teachers' lounge, or the 457(b) you signed up for and forgot. How your pension, Social Security, and savings are supposed to become one paycheck. And eventually — Book Two's territory — choices about pension options and DROP money that are permanent the moment you sign.

This book's argument is simple: **the years before retirement are when understanding is cheap.** Right now, every one of these topics is just information. Learn it at your own pace, ask questions with no deadline breathing on you, run estimates, change your mind twice. Wait until the exit meeting, and the same topics become a stack of forms, a thirty-minute appointment, and a signature line. Same decisions. Wildly different conditions for making them.

So we'll take them in order: how the system is built, the two plans, the second election, DROP in two chapters (what it is, and why timing matters), the side accounts, the income estimate, the household readiness work, the mistakes catalog, and finally a countdown that turns all of it into a calendar — ten years out, five, three, one, and the last ninety days.

A word on what this book will not do. It will not tell you which plan to pick, when to enter DROP, or which pension option protects your family best — not because those answers don't exist, but because they don't exist *in general*. They exist in your service record, your hire date, your class, your health, your marriage, and your bank statements, which is why every chapter ends by routing you to the people and agencies who can see those facts: MyFRS and the Division of Retirement, your HR office, a tax professional, an attorney, a fiduciary adviser. This book's job is to make you the best-prepared person in every one of those conversations. It will also not sell you anything. There is no product in these pages, no fear, and no pitch — just the belief printed on everything our firm builds: understanding comes before action.

No hype. No products. No pressure. Just the explanation you should have gotten with the parking decal.

>
> Maria Alvarez could explain photosynthesis to a room of seventh graders at 1:45 on a Friday afternoon. Twenty-two years in a Duval County classroom will build that kind of skill. What she could not explain — she discovered at her niece's graduation party, when her brother-in-law asked a friendly question — was her own retirement.
>

Could she say what was in the formula? She could not. Her brother-in-law, who worked in logistics and had a 401(k) he checked on his phone, looked genuinely puzzled — not at Maria, at the situation. "Twenty-two years in, and nobody's ever walked you through it?"
>
> Driving home, Maria realized the answer was no. Nobody ever had. And she'd been too busy taking care of everyone else's children to demand it.

### Your retirement is built differently — on purpose

If you've spent your career in public service, your retirement doesn't work like the retirements you see in commercials, and the sooner that's said plainly, the sooner everything else makes sense.

Private-sector retirement in America mostly runs on one machine: an account. A 401(k), owned by the worker, filled by contributions, riding the market. Simple to explain, easy to check on a phone, entirely the worker's problem.

Your retirement runs on a *system* — the Florida Retirement System, one of the largest public retirement systems in the country, covering state agencies, school districts, counties, most municipalities, universities, and colleges. Inside it, you have not one machine but several, and they interlock: a core plan (the Pension Plan **or** the Investment Plan — Chapters 3 and 4), a possible bonus round called DROP (Chapters 6 and 7), a health insurance subsidy for retirees, voluntary side accounts like 403(b)s and 457(b)s (Chapter 8), and, for most FRS members, Social Security on top — because most FRS-covered jobs do pay into Social Security, a fact that surprises people who've heard scary stories from teachers in other states.

The system's strength is real: much of your retirement can arrive as promised monthly income, backed by a formula rather than a market. The system's weakness is also real: it is governed by the Florida Legislature, which means the rules can and do change — and it communicates in forms, deadlines, and statute language rather than plain English.

### The 2011 line running through every break room

Here is the single most important fact in this book, and the source of most break-room confusion: **there are two versions of the FRS Pension Plan, and your hire date decides which one is yours.**

If you first enrolled **before July 1, 2011**, you're in the older tier: vesting after 6 years, benefits calculated on your highest 5 years of pay, normal retirement (for Regular Class) at 62 or 30 years of service, and a cost-of-living adjustment tied to your pre-2011 service. If you enrolled **on or after July 1, 2011**, you're in the newer tier: vesting after 8 years, highest 8 years of pay, normal retirement at 65 or 33 years — and, for now, no COLA on that service. Chapter 2 unpacks all of it.

Why does this matter on page one? Because it means the veteran at the next desk is not a reliable manual for your retirement. Two teachers in the same hallway, two deputies in the same squad, can be working under different vesting rules, different formulas, different retirement dates — and neither of them was ever told. Half the confident retirement advice traded in Florida break rooms is accurate for the person giving it and wrong for the person receiving it.

### The rules moved again — recently, and in your favor

The other reason yesterday's knowledge fails: the Legislature has been busy, and for once the changes mostly help. In 2023, a major bill rebuilt DROP — entry windows eliminated, participation extended to 8 years (10 for classroom instructional personnel, for now), interest raised from 1.3% to 4% — raised the retiree Health Insurance Subsidy by half, boosted Investment Plan contributions, and restored Special Risk normal retirement to age 55 or 25 years for everyone in that class, including post-2011 hires. And in 2026, lawmakers moved to restore a minimum cost-of-living adjustment for eligible Special Risk retirees — with strings worth understanding (Chapter 3).

### The system in six dates

Tape this timeline to the inside of your planning folder — it's the whole modern history, and the reason "current as of" matters:

- **1970** — the FRS consolidates Florida's patchwork of public pensions into one system.
- **2002** — the Investment Plan opens: for the first time, members choose between a promise and an account.
- **July 1, 2011** — the recession-era reset: 3% employee contributions begin, vesting stretches to 8 years for new hires, AFC moves to highest-8, normal retirement dates push out, and the COLA stops accruing on new service. The line through every break room is drawn.
- **July 1, 2023** — SB 7024 rebuilds DROP (8 years, 4% interest, no entry window), raises the HIS by half, boosts Investment Plan contributions, and gives Special Risk back its 55/25 date.
- **January 2025** — Congress repeals the WEP and GPO, the Social Security offsets that haunted some public-sector households for decades.
- **July 2026** — a minimum COLA floor arrives for eligible Special Risk retirees, with conditions.

Six dates, one lesson: this system moves — usually slowly, occasionally all at once — and it never mails you a personal summary of what changed. The members who do well aren't the ones who memorized the rules once. They're the ones who date-stamp what they know and check it yearly.

Translation: if your retirement knowledge was formed before 2023, parts of it are obsolete — possibly the parts you were counting on, possibly parts that just got better. This book is current as of July 2026 and says so everywhere it matters, because the honest posture toward this system is *date-stamped knowledge, refreshed regularly*.

### The parts list

Strip away the acronyms and the FRS Pension Plan is a machine with five parts. Learn these five and the rest of the book gets easy.

**Service credit.** Your years and partial years of covered employment — the raw material of everything. It accrues while you work for an FRS employer, and in some situations (certain leaves, prior service, military time) it can be purchased or claimed; whether that's worth it is real math for a professional, but *knowing your exact total* is free and takes five minutes on MyFRS.

**Vesting.** The service required before the benefit is yours to keep: **6 years** if you enrolled before July 1, 2011; **8 years** if on or after. Leave before vesting and the employer-funded benefit stays behind (your own 3% contributions can be refunded — usually a poor trade, covered in the mistakes catalog).

**Average Final Compensation (AFC).** The salary the formula uses: your highest **5** fiscal years (pre-2011 tier) or highest **8** (post-2011 tier). Not your final salary — your highest years, which for most careers cluster at the end but don't have to.

**The percentage value (multiplier).** Each year of service earns a slice of your AFC. Regular Class earns about **1.6% per year** (slightly more if you work past your normal retirement date); **Special Risk Class earns 3% per year** — nearly double, reflecting the physical reality of law enforcement, firefighting, corrections, and EMS careers. Other classes (Senior Management, Elected Officers) have their own values.

**Your contribution.** Since 2011, members contribute **3% of pay, pre-tax**, every paycheck. It's automatic and invisible, which is why half the workforce forgets they're paying it. You are. It's your money in the machine.

**The formula, assembled:** *Years of service × percentage value × AFC = annual benefit at normal retirement (before any option or DROP decisions).* Chapter 3 runs the numbers.

### Normal retirement: the dates the formula respects

"Normal retirement" is the point where your full formula benefit is payable without early-retirement reduction:

| Class & tier | Normal retirement (earlier of) |
|---|---|
| Regular, enrolled before 7/1/2011 | Age 62 (vested) · or 30 years of service |
| Regular, enrolled on/after 7/1/2011 | Age 65 (vested) · or 33 years of service |
| Special Risk — **all members** (post-SB 7024) | Age 55 (vested) · or 25 years of Special Risk service |

That Special Risk row is recent and worth reading twice: the 2023 legislation restored **55 or 25** for everyone in the class, including post-2011 hires who'd been told 60 or 30 their whole careers. If you wear a badge, turn out to fires, work the pods, or run calls — and you last checked your retirement date before 2023 — your date may have moved closer.

Retiring before normal retirement is possible once vested, but the benefit is reduced for each year you're early — a permanent haircut that deserves real math, not a shrug, before anyone chooses it.

### Early retirement: the haircut, quantified

Because "reduced" hides the stakes, here's the shape of it: the early-retirement reduction runs on the order of **5% for each year** you begin benefits before your normal retirement age — and it is permanent. Start five years early and the check that arrives for the rest of your life is roughly a quarter smaller than the formula built, before any option elections shrink it further. Sometimes early is still right — health, family, a finished mission all outvote arithmetic sometimes — but it should be chosen with the haircut priced in writing, never discovered in the first deposit. An official MyFRS estimate run at both dates makes the price visible in fifteen minutes. This is exactly the kind of decision this book keeps insisting deserves *real numbers*: the difference between "I retired a little early" and "I permanently reduced a thirty-year income stream by a quarter" is one estimate nobody ran.

### Classes, briefly

**Regular Class** is most of the system — teachers, staff, clerks, maintenance, administration. **Special Risk** covers law enforcement, firefighters, corrections, EMS, and certain related roles, with the 3% value and the 55/25 date. Smaller classes (Senior Management Service, Elected Officers) have their own rates. Two career notes: class can change when jobs change (a deputy who moves to a civilian role stops earning Special Risk credit going forward), and mixed-class careers produce blended benefits — a calculation worth confirming with the Division of Retirement rather than estimating on a napkin.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical, illustrative only.* Deputy Reyes, Special Risk, 25 years of service, AFC of $72,000: 25 × 3% = 75% of AFC ≈ **$54,000/year** at normal retirement, before options and taxes. His neighbor Ms. Tran, Regular Class teacher, 30 years, AFC $58,000: 30 × 1.6% = 48% ≈ **$27,800/year**. Same county, similar tenure, very different formulas — by design, and by class.
>
> Now the tier effect, isolated: Marcus (post-2011) versus Dale (pre-2011), identical careers, identical pay. Dale's AFC uses his best 5 years; Marcus's uses his best 8, which pulls in three lower-paid years and lowers the average — call it $61,000 vs. $58,500 on the same career. Dale's COLA applies to all his service; Marcus's applies to none of his. Nobody chose any of this; a hire date chose it. Which is exactly why "run *your* estimate" beats "ask Dale" — and MyFRS will run it for free.

> ❝ *Ten months of hire date can move a retirement three years.*

#### What can go wrong

- Guessing your service total instead of pulling it. Purchased time, leaves, and breaks in service make memories unreliable.
- Assuming "final salary" when the formula says highest-5 or highest-8 *average*.
- Special Risk members planning around the old 60/30 date they memorized before 2023.
- Forgetting a class change mid-career quietly changed your accrual going forward.
- Cashing out your 3% contributions after leaving a job — and unknowingly surrendering a vested benefit.

## CHAPTER 3 — The Pension Plan, Explained

*Benefit figures are hypothetical illustrations. COLA provisions described reflect law as of July 2026, including 2026 legislation; confirm current status with the Division of Retirement.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> The thing that finally made the pension real to Deputy Reyes wasn't a seminar. It was his father-in-law's mailbox.
>
> Ray had retired from a private logistics company with a 401(k) — a respectable one, built over decades. But every month, Ray *managed* it: watched the market, adjusted withdrawals, worried through downturns, recalculated how long it had to last. Meanwhile Ray's brother, retired twenty years from a sheriff's office up north, got a deposit on the first of every month, rain or shine, market up or market down, and had not once in twenty years done anything to earn it except stay alive.
>
> "Which one do you want to be?" Ray asked. Reyes understood the question was more complicated than it sounded — his brother-in-law's deposit never grew, either, and inflation had been quietly eating it for two decades. But he finally understood what he owned: not an account. A promise. And promises have their own fine print.

### What a defined benefit actually is

The FRS Pension Plan is a **defined benefit** plan: the *benefit* is defined — the formula from Chapter 2 — and the state carries the job of funding it, investing it, and paying it for as long as you live. You never pick investments, never watch a balance, never bear a market crash personally. In exchange, you give up ownership, flexibility, and growth: there is no account with your name on it to spend, borrow, or leave in a lump to your kids (survivor protection exists, but through the option elections covered in Book Two).

The plan's personality, honestly stated: it rewards **long service** (the formula multiplies years), **staying put** (vesting takes 6 or 8 years, and the biggest payoffs come deep in a career), and **people who value certainty over control**. It is a poor fit for short stays and job-hoppers — which is exactly the gap the Investment Plan exists to fill (next chapter).

Say plainly, too, what the pension cannot do, because families discover these edges at the worst times. It cannot hand your children a balance — when the covered lifetimes end (yours, or yours-plus-survivor under Book Two's options), the payments simply stop; there is no residual account. It cannot be tapped early for an emergency, borrowed against, or accelerated — its rigidity is the price of its reliability. And it cannot flex with a great market year or a bad one; the deposit is the deposit. None of these are flaws. They are the design — and they're the reason the side accounts of Chapter 8 and the reserve of Chapter 10 exist: the pension supplies the floor, and everything flexible in your plan has to come from somewhere else.

Three practical mechanics complete the picture. **It pays for life** — longevity risk, the scariest risk in private-sector retirement, is the plan's problem, not yours. **It can pay for two lives** — the survivor options in Book Two can extend payments across a spouse's lifetime, at a cost. **It is legislated** — funded through employer contributions and your 3%, governed by statute, and changeable by the Legislature, which history says cuts in hard times (2011) and restores in good ones (2023, 2026).

### The COLA — read this section twice

A pension's quiet enemy is inflation: a fixed deposit buys less every year. Cost-of-living adjustments exist to fight that, and the FRS COLA is the most misunderstood rule in the system.

Here is the current structure, plainly. Your annual COLA percentage is **3% multiplied by the fraction of your service earned before July 1, 2011**. All your service pre-2011? Full 3%. Half of it? Roughly 1.5%. Hired after July 1, 2011? Under the general rule, **your COLA is zero** — your pension starts strong and then stands still while prices don't.

One meaningful 2026 change: the Legislature moved to restore a **minimum 1.5% annual COLA for eligible Special Risk retirees**, beginning July 1, 2026 — with two conditions that headlines skipped: service requirements apply, and the retiree must have been **retired at least five years** before the adjustment begins. It's a real improvement for badge-and-hose careers, it is *not* the old 3%, it does not start at retirement, and — as with everything recent — confirm the final details for your situation before building on it.

Why hammer this? Because COLA status changes retirement math more than almost any single input. A post-2011 Regular Class member planning a 30-year retirement on a flat pension needs *other* growth somewhere — savings, Investment Plan dollars, Social Security's own COLA — doing the inflation-fighting the pension won't. That's not a flaw to panic over. It's a design fact to plan around, preferably a decade early. Book Three returns to it with tools.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Two Regular Class retirees, each starting at $30,000/year. Retiree A earned all service before 2011: full 3% COLA. Retiree B, hired 2012: no COLA under current law. Year one, identical checks. Year ten, A receives roughly $40,300; B still receives $30,000 — and if prices rose ~3% annually, B's check now buys about what $22,300 bought at retirement. By year twenty the gap is a canyon. Same formula, same service, same starting dollar — the COLA fraction was the whole difference.
>
> Now the planning flip: B isn't doomed; B is *informed*. Knowing at 45 that her pension will be flat, she has twenty years to point her 457(b), her raises, and eventually her Social Security timing at the inflation problem — Chapters 8 and 9 begin that work. The retirees who get hurt aren't the ones with a fraction. They're the ones who never learned they had one.

> ❝ *A pension is a promise — and inflation reads the fine print.*

#### What can go wrong

- Discovering your COLA fraction at retirement instead of fifteen years before.
- Treating the new Special Risk 1.5% as immediate, universal, or equal to the old 3%.
- Confusing "pays for life" with "keeps up with life." Longevity protection isn't inflation protection.
- Assuming the pension includes a death benefit shaped like an account. Survivor protection runs through Book Two's options — decided at retirement, not by default.

#### What to check before deciding

Compute your own COLA fraction (pre-2011 service ÷ projected total service — MyFRS has both numbers). If you're Special Risk, confirm the 2026 provision's final status and your eligibility path. Then ask the honest planning question: *what, in my plan, grows?*

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. What is my COLA fraction, computed from my actual service dates?
2. If my pension is flat or nearly flat, which parts of my plan are assigned to fight inflation?
3. What does "pays for life" cover in my household — one life, or two? (Book Two preview.)
4. Special Risk: what exactly did the 2026 change give me, and when?
5. What would my pension buy in year fifteen if prices rise 3% a year and it doesn't?

> **Ask your team:** MyFRS/Division of Retirement to confirm your COLA fraction from your official service dates; a fee-only adviser for the "what grows in my plan" assignment.

## CHAPTER 4 — The Investment Plan, Explained

*Investing involves risk, including possible loss of principal. Past performance does not guarantee future results. Nothing in this chapter is a recommendation.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Tanya had worked three jobs in eleven years — university program coordinator, then a nonprofit stint out of state, now back with a county agency — and somewhere in the shuffle she'd absorbed the idea that public retirement wasn't really *for* people like her. Pensions were for the thirty-years-in-one-place crowd. She'd never last long enough to matter.
>
> Then an HR onboarding slide stopped her: her FRS Investment Plan account from the university years was still hers. It had vested after one year. It had been sitting there, invested, the entire time she was in Atlanta — portable, owned, growing and dipping with the markets, waiting. She hadn't lost it by leaving. She'd just never been told she had it.
>
> "So the system *does* have a version for people with my kind of career," she said to the benefits coordinator. It does. This chapter is that version.
>
> That night Tanya did something she'd never done in eleven years of public employment: she logged in and looked at her own retirement money. The balance was smaller than she'd have liked and larger than she'd feared — but the feeling that stayed with her wasn't about the number. It was ownership. Nobody could freeze it, forfeit it, or make her wait eight years to keep it. It had followed her to Atlanta and back without being told. "Okay," she said to the screen, "let's figure out what you actually are." 

### The other machine

The FRS Investment Plan is a **defined contribution** plan — the mirror image of Chapter 3. Here, the *contributions* are defined; the eventual benefit is not. You and your employer pay a set percentage of your salary into an individual account with your name on it (your same 3%, plus employer contributions that were raised by two percentage points across all classes in the 2023 legislation). You choose investments from a menu of funds. The balance rises and falls with markets. At retirement — or departure — the account is simply *yours*: to keep invested, roll over, draw down, or eventually leave to heirs.

The trade is the exact reverse of the pension's. You gain **ownership** (a real balance, inheritable), **portability** (this is the headline), and **control** (you direct the investments). You give up the **guarantee**: no formula, no promised monthly check, no state absorbing the market's bad years. Longevity risk — the money outlasting you, or not — moves onto your shoulders, along with its management.

Two mechanics carry most of the plan's practical weight:

**Vesting: one year.** Not six, not eight — one. For mobile careers, this single number changes everything. Tanya's three-employer decade, which would have produced *zero* vested pension benefit, produced a real, owned account instead. This is precisely who the plan was built for.

**No DROP — ever.** The Deferred Retirement Option Program (Chapters 6–7) belongs exclusively to Pension Plan members. Choosing the Investment Plan closes that door, and with DROP's 2023 upgrades — eight years at 4% interest — the door is worth more than it used to be. Any honest comparison (next chapter) prices it in.

### Being your own pension manager

Because the account is yours, three responsibilities are, too — none complicated, all consequential.

**Fund choices and fees.** The plan offers a menu built for two kinds of members, and knowing which kind you are is half the job. The ready-made route — target-date-style funds that hold a diversified mix and adjust it as your retirement year approaches — exists for members who want one sturdy decision instead of ongoing management; it is a respectable, professional-grade choice, not a beginner's compromise. The build-your-own route — individual stock, bond, and specialty funds — exists for members who genuinely want to steer, and it demands what steering always demands: attention, rebalancing, and the behavior discipline of Chapter 4's third responsibility. What the menu punishes is the third way most people actually take: a scattering of funds chosen in one distracted afternoon years ago and never revisited. Each option publishes an expense level. Costs compound against you the same way returns compound for you, and cost differences that look tiny annually become large across decades. The education-level rule from every book in this series: know what you own and what it costs, in writing — the plan's materials disclose both.

**Behavior.** An account you can see is an account you can panic about. Market drops arrive on a schedule of their own, and the plan's long-run logic only works for members who don't sell the bottoms. (Book Three's behavior chapter applies to Investment Plan members a full career earlier than everyone else.)

**The exit plan.** A pension retires *for* you: the checks just start. An account retires only as well as the withdrawal plan attached to it — how much, from what, in what order, for how long. That's Book Two and Three material, flagged now so it's never a surprise.

One more honest note: the Investment Plan carries a modest death-benefit and disability structure different from the pension's, and money left before vesting (that one year) follows its own rules. Details live in the plan's official materials — worth ten minutes with your actual statement.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Two Regular Class colleagues, both hired at 30, both earning $55,000. Aaron expects a full FRS career: for him, the pension's formula deepens with every year, DROP waits at the end, and the guarantee fits his temperament — he'd rather never think about markets. Tanya (our Tanya) realistically expects 6–10 more public years before another move: under the pension she might *never vest* on the newer 8-year rule, or vest into a small, frozen, no-COLA benefit; under the Investment Plan every one of those years produces owned, portable money — hypothetically, contributions in the low-$6,000s per year between her 3% and the employer share, invested through her working decades.
>
> Neither colleague is "right." They have different careers, and the plans are built for different careers. The failure mode isn't picking either plan — it's picking by default, rumor, or coin flip, which is how most members actually end up enrolled. The comparison deserves a real framework. That's the next chapter — along with the one-time do-over almost nobody knows they're holding.

> ❝ *One plan rewards staying. The other forgives leaving. Know which career you're actually having.*

#### What can go wrong

- Choosing by inertia during the enrollment window and calling it a decision.
- Never looking at fund choices or fees again after day one.
- Discovering at 55 that DROP was never available to you — and that you didn't know.
- Panic-selling the account's worst month, converting a temporary decline into a permanent one.
- Leaving small vested accounts scattered and forgotten across old employers. (Tanya nearly did.)

#### What to check before deciding

If you're in the Investment Plan now: pull your current statement, list your funds and their expense levels, confirm your beneficiary, and locate any orphaned accounts from prior FRS employment. If you're comparing plans: hold that thought one chapter — the framework's coming.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. What does my realistic career path look like — one employer, or several?
2. What am I invested in today, and what does each holding cost per year?
3. How did I handle the last real market drop — honestly?
4. What is the DROP door worth to someone with my class and timeline?
5. Who is the beneficiary on my account — verified, not assumed?

> **Ask your team:** MyFRS resources include free guidance lines and plan-choice tools; use them before any election, and bring the fee sheet to any adviser conversation.

#### Chapter checklist

- [ ] I can state the defined-contribution trade in one sentence each direction
- [ ] Current statement pulled; funds and costs listed
- [ ] Beneficiary confirmed in writing
- [ ] Old FRS accounts located and inventoried
- [ ] My honest market temperament written down — it's a planning input

#### Reflect

1. Which responsibility — choices, behavior, or the exit plan — worries you most, and what would shrink it?
2. What has your actual job history been over the last decade — and what does it predict?
3. If your account dropped 20% next year, what does the best version of you do?

## CHAPTER 5 — Pension vs. Investment Plan — and the Second Election

*Plan choice is individual and consequential. This chapter is a framework for questions, not a recommendation. Free MyFRS guidance resources exist for exactly this decision — use them.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Jordan had been a 911 dispatcher for nine years when she learned — from a podcast, of all places — that her plan choice wasn't necessarily permanent. Every FRS member carries a one-time "second election": a single switch between the Pension Plan and the Investment Plan, usable once in a career, while actively employed.
>
> She'd defaulted into one plan at 24 without understanding the question. Now, at 33, with a clearer picture of her career, her family, and her stomach for markets, she could actually *answer* it — once, and only once. The podcast host said the sentence that stuck: "It's the most valuable do-over in Florida government, and most people holding it don't know it exists."
>
> Jordan didn't switch that month. She did something better: she finally treated the choice like it was hers.
>
> She built a one-page file — her service total, her honest career outlook, the MyFRS comparison output — and set a phone reminder for the same week every year: *re-decide on purpose.* Three years running, the answer has been "not yet." Her sister asked what the point was, if she never switches. Jordan's answer belongs in this chapter: "The point is that if I ever do, it'll be because my life changed — not because a market scared me or a coworker convinced me at lunch." 

### The comparison, laid flat

Everything from Chapters 3 and 4, one table:

| | Pension Plan | Investment Plan |
|---|---|---|
| What's promised | Lifetime monthly benefit by formula | Contributions only; balance varies with markets |
| Vesting | 6 yrs (pre-7/2011) / 8 yrs (after) | 1 year |
| Best-fit career | Long service, ideally to normal retirement | Mobile, shorter, or uncertain tenure |
| Who carries market risk | The state | You |
| Who carries longevity risk | The state | You |
| Inflation | COLA fraction (Ch. 3) — zero for post-2011 service, new Special Risk floor pending conditions | Whatever your investments earn |
| DROP access | **Yes** (Ch. 6–7) | **Never** |
| Ownership & heirs | No account; survivor protection via options (Book Two) | Real balance; beneficiary-designated |
| Your job | Show up for years | Choose funds, watch fees, behave, plan the exit |

### Five questions that actually decide it

The honest framework isn't "which plan is better" — it's "which plan fits the career and household I actually have." Five inputs do most of the work:

**1. Time.** The pension's value lives deep in a career: vesting, the multiplying formula, normal-retirement dates, DROP at the end. Realistic long-haul members lean pension; realistically mobile members lean toward the plan that vests in one year. Be honest about the career you're *having*, not the one HR assumed.

**2. Class.** Special Risk changes the math twice — the 3% multiplier makes pension years unusually valuable, and the 55/25 restoration plus DROP access stacks further value on staying. A framework that ignores class is broken.

**3. Household.** A spouse with a strong separate retirement changes how much guarantee your plan must supply. A household leaning entirely on you leans harder toward promised income — or toward disciplined planning around an owned account. (Survivor design is Book Two; it starts influencing choices here.)

**4. Temperament.** Chapter 4's responsibilities are real. Some people will manage an account calmly for forty years; some will lose sleep or sell bottoms. Self-knowledge is a legitimate, respectable planning input.

**5. The DROP door.** Post-2023 DROP — up to eight years, 4% interest — is a substantial end-of-career asset available only through the pension. Members within realistic reach of normal retirement should price the door before choosing to close it.

### The second election: the one-time do-over

The mechanics matter, so plainly: **every FRS member gets one second election** — a single, irrevocable switch between plans, exercisable while actively employed (not after termination). Money moves with rules: switch pension→investment and your accrued benefit converts to an opening account balance; switch investment→pension and you must effectively buy into the pension, using your account (and possibly more) — a calculation with real winners and real losers depending on service, age, and salary history.

Three disciplines govern this power. **It's single-use** — spent once, gone forever, so it's held until the picture is clear, not burned on a mood. **It's math-heavy** — the buy-in/conversion numbers are exactly what the free MyFRS choice services and a fee-only adviser or actuary-informed estimate exist for; nobody should exercise it on break-room arithmetic. **It's a standing insurance policy** — even unused, it means today's plan isn't a life sentence, which should lower the temperature on the whole question. Jordan's real win wasn't switching; it was knowing.

### When elections get spent well

Since the do-over is single-use, it helps to know what a *good* use historically looks like. The patterns worth knowing: a career plan that genuinely changed (the ten-year person who became a lifer, or the reverse — Jordan's watch-list); a class change that reweighted the math (moving into or out of Special Risk changes the multiplier's pull); a vesting realization (a post-2011 member six years in, now sure they'll leave at year seven, facing the difference between zero pension and an owned account); and a late-career, professionally-modeled switch where the buy-in numbers actually pencil. The patterns that spend it badly are just as consistent: market fear, market greed, a persuasive lunch, and paperwork momentum. The election rewards biography and punishes mood — which is why the annual re-decide ritual, boring as it is, is the whole strategy.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Three second-election postures. **Aaron** (Ch. 4's lifer, 22 pension years, Special Risk-adjacent timeline): his election's best use is probably *never* — his pension is deep, DROP is near, and the do-over's value to him is pure insurance. **Tanya** (mobile career, currently Investment Plan): switching *into* the pension would require a buy-in her one-employer-at-a-time history doesn't obviously justify — her election likely stays holstered too, for opposite reasons. **Jordan** (nine years in, genuinely uncertain path): hers is the live case — and her right move is an annual ritual, not a decision: each year, she updates the five inputs, runs the MyFRS comparison, and re-decides *not yet* on purpose. The second election rewards exactly one behavior: staying informed enough to use it well — or to knowingly never use it at all.

> ❝ *The most valuable do-over in Florida government is held by people who don't know they have it.*

#### What can go wrong

- Burning the second election on a market mood — panic out of the Investment Plan at a bottom, or greed out of the pension at a top.
- Exercising it near retirement without pricing the DROP door it may close.
- Doing the buy-in math on a napkin. This is professional-grade arithmetic with an irrevocable signature attached.
- Never learning you had it — the most common failure, and now impossible for you.

#### What to check before deciding

Confirm on MyFRS whether your second election is intact (a small number of members have already used theirs, sometimes without registering the significance). Run the plan-comparison tools with your real numbers. If a switch tempts you, get the conversion/buy-in figure in writing and put professional eyes on it before anything is signed.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. Which of the five inputs — time, class, household, temperament, DROP — dominates my situation?
2. Is my second election still available, confirmed in writing?
3. What would a switch cost or convert to, in actual dollars, today?
4. What would have to change in my life for the answer to change?
5. Who has reviewed this with me who does *not* earn anything from my choice?

> **Ask your team:** MyFRS choice-service counselors (free) for the mechanics and numbers; a fee-only adviser for how the choice fits the whole household picture. Both, ideally, before any irrevocable election.

## CHAPTER 6 — DROP, Part One: What It Is

*DROP rules reflect law as of July 2026, including the 2023 changes. Confirm current rules, your eligibility, and all deadlines with MyFRS and the Division of Retirement before acting.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Lieutenant Brooks had heard the word DROP for fifteen years the way you hear a highway you never take — background noise, other people's exit. Firefighters two shifts ahead of him talked about "getting in," "riding it out," "the check at the end." He nodded along and understood roughly none of it.
>
> Then his engineer, Soto, hit 25 years and sat the whole shift down at the station kitchen table with a legal pad. "It's simpler than they make it sound," Soto said, drawing two boxes. "Box one: I keep coming to work, same job, same paycheck. Box two: as far as the retirement system is concerned, I already retired — my pension started, but instead of mailing me the checks, they're stacking them in an account with my name on it, earning four percent. When I actually walk out, I get the stack *and* the monthly check starts coming to my mailbox."
>
> Brooks stared at the two boxes for a long moment. "Wait. You get paid to work *and* your retirement gets paid at the same time?" Soto tapped the pad. "For up to eight years. Now you know why the old guys never explain it — they're too busy being in it."

### Retired on paper, working in person

The **Deferred Retirement Option Program** is exactly Soto's two boxes. It's available only to **Pension Plan** members (Investment Plan members never have access — Chapter 4's closed door), and only once you've reached **normal retirement eligibility** for your class and tier (Chapter 2's table: 62/30 or 65/33 for Regular, 55/25 for Special Risk).

When you enter DROP, three things happen at once. **You retire, formally, inside the system:** your pension benefit is calculated and locked using your service and AFC *as of that entry date* — the formula stops accruing; more years and raises after entry no longer grow the monthly benefit. **You keep working:** same job, same salary, same day-to-day, for a defined participation window. **Your pension checks start — into a holding account:** every month, the benefit you would have received is deposited into your DROP accumulation, where it earns **4% annual interest**, compounding while you work.

At the end of your DROP window, you separate from FRS employment. Then two streams begin: your monthly pension arrives at your actual mailbox for life, and your DROP accumulation — often the largest single sum a public employee ever receives — gets distributed per your instructions: lump sum, direct rollover (an IRA, or even into the FRS Investment Plan), or a combination. Those exit decisions carry serious tax consequences and deadlines, and they are the heart of Book Two. This chapter's job is the machine; the next chapter's job is the clock.

### The 2023 rebuild — why old DROP knowledge is wrong

If your DROP understanding predates 2023, most of it needs replacing. The Legislature's overhaul changed the program's three biggest levers:

**The entry window is gone.** For years, most members had to enter DROP within a narrow window around normal retirement or lose access forever — the source of a generation of regret stories. Eliminated. Eligible members can now enter **any time after reaching normal retirement eligibility**.

**The clock is longer.** Maximum participation grew from 60 months to **96 months (8 years) for every class** — and **K-12 instructional personnel can extend to 120 months (10 years)**, a provision currently set to sunset June 30, 2029 (teachers within reach of that window should watch that date).

**The interest tripled.** DROP accumulations now earn **4%** instead of 1.3% — the difference between an afterthought and a genuine accumulation engine over an eight-year window.

Together, these turned DROP from a rigid, modest program into one of the most substantial end-of-career features in American public retirement — which is exactly why timing it (Chapter 7) and exiting it (Book Two) deserve their own chapters.

### Life inside DROP — the small print of the day-to-day

A few things change quietly the day you enter, worth knowing before rather than after. **Your 3% contribution stops** — you're retired in the system's eyes, so the deduction ends and your take-home nudges up. **Your service and HIS are fixed** — because the formula locks at entry, the service count that drives your Health Insurance Subsidy generally locks with it; DROP years add accumulation, not service. **You've formally resigned, effective your DROP end date** — the entry paperwork includes it, which changes nothing about Tuesday but everything about the endpoint; extensions and adjustments have rules, and "I changed my mind in year six" is a conversation with real constraints. **Your survivor and option decisions get made at entry** — because entry *is* retirement, the pension-option election (Book Two's biggest chapter) happens then, not at your last day. That last point surprises more members than any other sentence in this chapter: if DROP is in your future, Book Two's option decision is closer than your exit date. It arrives with your entry forms.

### What DROP is not

Three clarities prevent most DROP confusion. **It is not extra pension accrual** — entry freezes the formula; DROP trades future benefit growth for accumulation-plus-interest now. **It is not free money** — it's *your* pension, redirected and compounded; the real question is whether frozen-formula-plus-accumulation beats kept-on-growing-formula, which is a timing question (next chapter). **It is not reversible** — entering DROP sets your retirement in motion; the participation window ends in separation. It's a commitment with a calendar, entered best by people who understand both.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical, simplified.* Lieutenant Brooks reaches 25 Special Risk years with an AFC around $80,000. His locked monthly benefit at entry: 25 × 3% × $80,000 = $60,000/year, or $5,000/month. In DROP, that $5,000 deposits monthly into his accumulation, earning 4% — across a full 8-year window, deposits alone total $480,000, and with compounding the accumulation lands meaningfully north of $560,000, all while his regular salary never missed a beat. At exit: the monthly $5,000 (plus any COLA his fraction and the new Special Risk floor eventually provide) starts arriving for life, and the accumulation rolls or distributes per Book Two's decisions.
>
> Now the honest other side of the ledger: had Brooks *not* entered, those 8 years would have kept growing the formula instead — 33 years × 3% ≈ 99% of a likely-higher AFC. Which path wins depends on salary trajectory, class, longevity, and household needs — in other words, on Chapter 7. What this chapter establishes is only, finally, what the old guys never explained: both boxes are real, both are yours, and the choice between them deserves real numbers.

> ❝ *DROP doesn't grow your pension. It starts it early and banks it — at 4%.*

#### What can go wrong

- Learning DROP mechanics for the first time at the eligibility meeting.
- Carrying pre-2023 rules — the vanished entry window, the 5-year clock, the 1.3% rate — into 2026 decisions.
- Hearing "get in the DROP" as universal advice. It's a fit question, not a commandment.
- Forgetting entry locks the formula — then feeling robbed by raises that no longer count.
- Investment Plan members budgeting around a program they can never join.

#### What to check before deciding

Confirm your normal-retirement eligibility date (your DROP eligibility date), your class's maximum window (96 months; 120 if instructional, noting the 2029 sunset), and — before any entry paperwork — a written benefit estimate showing exactly what monthly amount would lock. Nothing here needs deciding today; everything here needs understanding before Chapter 7.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. When exactly do I reach normal retirement eligibility — the date DROP becomes available to me?
2. What monthly benefit would lock if I entered today? In three years?
3. What is my realistic maximum window, and does the instructional sunset touch me?
4. What does my household need this program to be — accumulation, bridge, or nothing?
5. What did I "know" about DROP before this chapter that was actually pre-2023?

> **Ask your team:** MyFRS/Division of Retirement for official DROP estimates and current forms; a tax professional *before* exit-year decisions (Book Two will insist).

- [ ] Soto's two boxes explainable to a colleague in ninety seconds
- [ ] My eligibility date and maximum window written on the rulebook page
- [ ] Formula-freeze mechanic understood: entry locks the calculation
- [ ] Pre-2023 DROP folklore consciously discarded
- [ ] Chapter 7 flagged as the actual decision chapter

#### Reflect

1. What was your mental image of DROP before the two boxes — and where did it come from?
2. Which appeals to you more instinctively: banking the pension early, or growing the formula longer?
3. Who do you know inside DROP right now — and what could their experience teach you *if you asked specific questions*?

## CHAPTER 7 — DROP, Part Two: Why Timing Matters

*Timing scenarios below are hypothetical frameworks, not recommendations. Entry decisions are irrevocable — obtain official estimates and professional review first.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Principal Washington and Coach Green hit normal retirement eligibility the same spring, in the same building, and made opposite DROP decisions — both correctly.
>
> Washington was finishing her climb: a district-level promotion was realistically two or three years out, and it would raise her AFC substantially. Entering DROP now would lock her formula at pre-promotion pay — banking eight years of a permanently smaller number. She waited, took the promotion, let three high-salary years rebuild her average, *then* entered. Her locked benefit was hundreds of dollars a month larger, for life.
>
> Green's salary had plateaued years ago — coaches' schedules cap out — and his AFC was as high as it was ever getting. Every month he waited was a month of pension deposits and 4% interest he'd never collect. He entered the week he was eligible, rode the full window (plus the instructional extension), and walked out with the accumulation Soto had drawn on the legal pad.
>
> Same hallway. Same program. Opposite answers. The variable was never DROP. It was the shape of two different careers.

### The one question under all DROP timing

Strip away the noise and DROP entry timing is a single trade, run against your own facts:

**Enter earlier** → your benefit locks at today's formula, but the accumulation machine starts sooner — more months of deposits, more years of 4% compounding, and (since the total window is capped) entering early is the only way to use the *whole* window before any personal retirement deadline.

**Enter later** → every additional working year can grow the formula itself: more service years × the multiplier, and possibly a higher AFC if your best salary years are still ahead. The locked monthly number — the one that arrives for *life* — gets bigger.

So the deciding input is almost embarrassingly concrete: **what will your salary and service do between now and each possible entry date?** Rising sharply (Washington)? Later entry buys a permanently larger lifetime check. Flat (Green)? Waiting mostly burns accumulation months for nothing. Somewhere between — the common case — the answer comes from running the actual numbers at two or three candidate dates, which MyFRS estimates make free.

Three refinements sharpen the framework:

**The window is a budget.** Ninety-six months is the maximum *participation*, not a guarantee you'll want all of it. Someone who intends to fully retire at 62 and reaches eligibility at 58 can only ever use four years — which weakens the "wait for raises" argument, since unused window is unpriced loss.

**Class changes the weights.** Special Risk's 3% multiplier makes each *additional formula year* unusually valuable — but the 55/25 date also opens DROP unusually early, offering the longest possible runways. High multiplier, early door: Special Risk timing decisions carry the biggest dollars in both directions and most deserve professional math.

**Life outranks arithmetic.** DROP entry is also a commitment: the window ends in separation. Health, family, the honest question of how many more years the body and heart have for this work — those inputs belong in the decision with full votes. The best DROP timing on a spreadsheet is wrong if it signs you up for years you don't actually want.

### The quiet mechanics people miss

Four details that surface later as surprises, moved earlier on purpose: **Entry paperwork has its own process and effective dates** — entry "when I got around to it" can differ from entry "when it was optimal" by months of accumulation. **Your 3% employee contribution stops in DROP** (you're retired, remember) — a small take-home bump people forget to expect. **Working conditions don't change, but employment terms can feel different** — you've formally resigned effective your DROP end date; understand what that means with your employer. **The exit is a project of its own** — distributions, withholding, rollover choices, and a deadline clock, all waiting in Book Two. Entering DROP without an exit plan is starting an eight-year trip with no thought about the landing.

And for instructional personnel, one date deserves its own alarm: the 120-month extension currently **sunsets June 30, 2029**. A teacher whose eligibility and intentions would use those extra months should confirm the provision's current status *before* building plans on it — legislative sunsets get extended sometimes, and sometimes they simply arrive. Date-stamped knowledge, again: the rule is real today; verify it's real the day you rely on it.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical, simplified — the Washington/Green math.* Green, instructional, AFC flat at $62,000, 30 years at eligibility (pre-2011 tier): locked benefit ≈ 30 × 1.68% × $62,000 ≈ $31,200/year, $2,600/month. Entering immediately, his ten-year instructional window banks roughly $312,000 in deposits plus compounding at 4% — call it ~$380,000 — versus waiting three years "to be sure," which would shrink the window he could use, grow the formula barely at all, and cost him tens of thousands in accumulation.
>
> Washington: 30 years, AFC $70,000 today — but a promotion path to $85,000+. Entering now locks ≈ $35,300/year. Entering after three promoted years (33 years × ~1.68% × higher AFC, per her tier's rules) locks north of $44,000/year — a permanently larger check whose lifetime value can outweigh the accumulation months she gave up, especially across a long retirement. The lesson is not "wait" or "go." It's that a fifteen-minute MyFRS estimate run at each candidate date — with a professional pressure-testing the close calls — is worth more than every hallway opinion combined.

> ❝ *The variable was never DROP. It was the shape of the career.*

#### What can go wrong

- Entering the week of eligibility because "everyone does" — with a promotion two years out.
- Waiting for raises that were never coming, while the accumulation clock ran unpaid.
- Sizing the decision to the maximum window you can't actually use.
- Making a Special Risk timing call — the biggest-dollar version — on break-room math.
- Entering with zero exit plan, meeting Book Two's decisions as strangers.

#### What to check before deciding

Run official estimates at three candidate dates (now, +2 years, +4 years, adjusted to your facts). Map your realistic salary trajectory honestly — promotions you'll *pursue*, not just imagine. Count the window you could genuinely use. Then put the close calls in front of a professional who profits from none of the answers.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. What does my benefit lock at today versus each realistic later entry date — in writing?
2. What will my salary genuinely do in the next 2–5 years?
3. How much of the maximum window can I actually use, given when I truly want to stop?
4. What do health, family, and honest appetite for the work say — independent of the math?
5. What is my exit sketch — even rough — before my entry signature?

> **BENOWITZ WEALTH PRINCIPLE** — *DROP can be powerful, but timing matters.*

- [ ] Three-date estimate comparison completed and filed
- [ ] Salary trajectory written down — the honest version
- [ ] Usable window counted against my real retirement intentions
- [ ] Life inputs (health, family, appetite) given full votes
- [ ] Exit-plan sketch stapled to any entry thinking; Book Two queued

## CHAPTER 8 — The Accounts You Built on the Side: 403(b), 457(b), and IRAs

*Contribution limits shown are 2026 figures and change annually. Tax concepts are education only — confirm your situation with a qualified tax professional. Investing involves risk, including possible loss of principal.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> The salesman found Coach D the way they always find teachers: in the lounge, fourth period, with donuts. Friendly guy, district-approved vendor list, a glossy brochure for a 403(b) "designed for educators." Coach D signed — it felt responsible — and for eleven years money left his paycheck into something he never looked at again.
>
> The reckoning came from an unlikely direction: his sister, a county budget analyst, glanced at his statement over Thanksgiving and started asking questions he couldn't answer. What are the fees? What's a surrender charge? Why is this an annuity inside a retirement account? Is there a 457 available too — and did anyone mention you could have both?
>
> Coach D didn't have a bad decade of saving. He had an unexamined one. And in side accounts, unexamined is where the money leaks.
>
> The part that stung wasn't the fees — it was the discovery that the salesman had been *optional*. His district's list included a plain, low-cost provider the whole time, no donuts attached, that nobody had ever stood in the lounge to promote. Of course not, his sister pointed out: low-cost providers can't afford lounges. "The good option doesn't market to you," she said. "That's part of what makes it the good option." Coach D wrote that down.

### The supplement layer — and why it's not optional homework

Your FRS benefit is the foundation. But Chapters 3 and 9 keep pointing at the same gap: for most members — especially post-2011 hires with little or no COLA — the pension alone won't carry a multi-decade retirement through inflation. The supplement layer is where the gap gets closed, and public employees have unusually good tools for it, frequently available *simultaneously*:

**403(b)** — offered by school districts, universities, and some other public employers. Payroll-deducted, tax-advantaged, invested through employer-approved vendors. The vendor detail matters enormously: 403(b) menus range from excellent low-cost funds to high-fee insurance products with surrender charges, sometimes on the same district list, sold with the same donuts. Same paycheck deduction; very different decades.

**457(b) deferred compensation** — offered by the State of Florida and many counties, cities, and districts. Structurally similar to a 403(b) with one famous superpower: **money withdrawn after separation from service is not subject to the 10% early-withdrawal penalty, regardless of age.** For a Special Risk member retiring at 50, or anyone leaving public service before 59½, the 457(b) can function as the bridge account other workers don't have. Public safety careers should read that sentence twice.

**IRAs** — individual accounts outside your employer entirely: 2026 limit $7,500 plus a $1,100 catch-up at 50+. Useful as a supplement, a rollover destination later (Book Two), and a Roth venue.

**The stacking rule most people miss:** 403(b) and 457(b) limits are **separate**. A member with access to both can defer up to the full limit into *each* — a door almost no one is told exists, and the single biggest late-career catch-up lever in public employment.

### The 2026 numbers, and the new Roth rule

Current limits, date-stamped and destined for the annual refresh:

| 2026 limit | Amount |
|---|---|
| 403(b) / 457(b) elective deferral (each) | $24,500 |
| Age-50+ catch-up | +$8,000 (→ $32,500) |
| Age 60–63 "super catch-up" | +$11,250 (→ $35,750) |
| 457(b) special 3-year pre-retirement catch-up | up to 2× limit (→ $49,000) |
| 403(b) 15-year service catch-up | up to +$3,000/yr (lifetime cap applies) |
| IRA (+ age-50 catch-up) | $7,500 (+$1,100) |

Two mechanics deserve plain English. **Roth vs. pre-tax:** pre-tax contributions reduce today's taxable income and are taxed on withdrawal; Roth contributions are taxed now and — rules met — withdraw tax-free later, with no lifetime RMDs on Roth workplace accounts under current law. Neither is universally better; the comparison is your tax rate now versus your best guess later, a genuinely personal question your pension income (which will be taxable) complicates in interesting ways a professional can walk through.

Here's the interesting complication, at education level, because it's specific to readers of this book: a career FRS member retires with a *floor of taxable income already installed* — the pension arrives as ordinary income every year, forever, before a single withdrawal decision gets made. That floor means many public retirees never fall to the very low tax brackets that make "I'll be in a lower bracket later" automatically true — and it's precisely why Roth dollars (taxed now, tax-free later, no lifetime RMDs on workplace Roth accounts under current law) can play a distinctive role *around* a pension: flexible income in retirement that doesn't stack on top of the taxable floor. Whether, how much, and when is professional-conversation territory — Book Two's tax chapter and Book Three's long-haul chapter build on it — but the framing belongs here, a decade early, where it can shape which box your next contribution checks.

**New for 2026 — the Roth catch-up mandate:** if your **prior-year FICA wages exceeded $150,000**, your age-based catch-up contributions must now be **Roth**. Two exemptions matter to this book's readers: the **457(b) special 3-year catch-up** and the **403(b) 15-year catch-up** are *not* subject to the Roth-only rule. And one trap: if your employer's plan offers no Roth option, affected high earners may be unable to make age-based catch-ups at all — a question for HR now, not in December.

### The vendor audit Coach D never did

The supplement layer has one recurring failure mode: **product quality varies wildly inside the same employer's offerings**, and nobody audits by default. The audit is one page: for each account — provider, current balance, every fee in writing (expense ratios, admin charges, surrender schedules), Roth availability, and beneficiary. High-cost products with surrender charges aren't illegal; they're just *comparable* — and district lists usually include lower-cost alternatives that a transfer within the plan can often reach. Whether and how to move is a facts-and-fine-print question for professional review. Knowing what you own and what it costs is not. It's this chapter's homework.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Coach D's audit, one Thanksgiving later: his 403(b) held a variable annuity charging ~2.1% all-in with a declining surrender schedule; the same district list included diversified fund options near 0.3%. On his $85,000 balance, the difference is roughly $1,500 *per year* in costs — before compounding does its quiet damage across his remaining fifteen working years. His sister's county 457(b), by contrast: 0.4% costs, Roth option live, and — because she plans to retire at 56 — penalty-free access from separation, making it her designed bridge to Social Security and Medicare ages. Same intention to save. One examined system, one leak. The audit took Coach D forty minutes, and the paperwork to reposition took an afternoon with a professional — the highest hourly wage of his year.

> ❝ *The 403(b) and 457(b) limits stack — the best-kept secret in public-employee saving.*

#### What can go wrong

- Signing in the lounge and never looking again. Unexamined is where money leaks.
- Never learning the 457(b)'s no-penalty-after-separation superpower — especially tragic in Special Risk households.
- Missing the stacking rule during peak-earning years.
- High earners discovering the Roth catch-up mandate in December, or in a plan with no Roth option.
- Forgetting beneficiaries on decade-old accounts. (The mistakes catalog will return to this.)

#### What to check before deciding

Run the one-page audit on every account. Confirm which plans your employer actually offers (403(b), 457(b), both?), whether Roth options exist in each, and — if you're within sight of 50, 60, or your last three working years — which catch-up doors open when. Then bring the page to a professional conversation about Roth/pre-tax mix and any repositioning.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. What do I own, and what does each account cost per year — in writing?
2. Do I have access to both a 403(b) and a 457(b) — and am I using the stack if it fits my budget?
3. Roth or pre-tax for my next dollar — given my pension will be taxable income?
4. Does the $150,000 Roth catch-up rule touch me, and does my plan offer Roth?
5. Who is the beneficiary on each account — verified this year?

> **Ask your team:** HR/benefits for the actual vendor list and plan documents; a tax professional for the Roth/pre-tax and catch-up questions; a fee-only adviser for the audit review.

> **BENOWITZ WEALTH PRINCIPLE** — *Advice is only as good as the incentives behind it.*

#### Chapter checklist

- [ ] One-page audit complete: provider, balance, all fees, Roth availability, beneficiary — per account
- [ ] 403(b)/457(b) availability and stacking confirmed with HR
- [ ] 457(b) separation superpower understood (and starred, if I'll retire before 59½)
- [ ] My catch-up calendar mapped (50 / 60–63 / final-3-years / 15-year)
- [ ] Roth-vs-pre-tax question scheduled with a professional

#### Reflect

1. When did you last actually read a statement from your side accounts?
2. What was sold to you in a lounge, and what would an audit of it find?
3. If your pension is the foundation, what job — bridge, inflation-fighter, flexibility — do you most need this layer to do?

> **For You and Your Spouse** — Audit *both* of your supplement layers side by side, including the account one of you forgot existed. Households retire on combined systems; tonight the household finally sees its whole board.

## CHAPTER 9 — Estimating Your Retirement Income

*All figures are hypothetical illustrations. Estimates are planning tools, not promises — benefits depend on your actual record and current rules at retirement.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> For years, Denise and Robert's retirement plan was a feeling. Denise, a state agency analyst with 24 years in, felt like retirement was "probably fine." Robert, self-employed most of his life, felt like it "probably wasn't." Same household, same numbers, two feelings — and every conversation about it ended in the same low-grade argument, because feelings can't be reconciled. Only numbers can.
>
> The truce came from a Saturday morning and a legal pad: one hour, three layers, both spouses. Denise's MyFRS estimate. Both Social Security statements, pulled from ssa.gov. The side accounts, totaled honestly. A monthly spending number they finally computed instead of guessed. When the pad was full, something surprising happened — not celebration, not panic. *Quiet.* The gap was real, but it was a number now, roughly $900 a month, with nine working years available to close it.
>
> "I can fix a number," Robert said. "I couldn't fix a feeling."
>
> The legal pad went on the refrigerator — Denise's idea, over Robert's mild objection that guests would see their finances. "Good," she said. "Maybe their houses have a number too and nobody's ever said it out loud either." By spring, two of their friends' houses did.

### The three-layer estimate

Every retirement income picture in this system stacks the same three layers. The estimate is just building the stack honestly, in today's dollars, on one page.

**Layer one: FRS.** Your official benefit estimate (MyFRS runs it free) at your realistic retirement date — as a *monthly* figure, because retirement is lived monthly. Note beside it the two asterisks this book has installed: your **COLA fraction** (does this layer grow?) and, if applicable, your **DROP intentions** (accumulation is an asset, not monthly income — it joins the plan through Book Two's decisions). Investment Plan members: your layer one is the account, translated conservatively into sustainable monthly draw — a translation Book Three teaches; for now, a deliberately modest placeholder beats an optimistic one.

**Layer two: Social Security.** Most FRS members earn it — pull both spouses' statements at ssa.gov. Three honest notes for the estimate: the figures shown assume you keep working to the claiming age listed; claiming spans 62 (reduced) to 70 (increased), so write down the *range*, not one number; and if your household ever had non-covered government work or expected the old offsets — the **WEP and GPO were repealed in 2025**, so any planning done around those penalties deserves a fresh look. Timing strategy is Book Two; the estimate just needs real figures on the page.

**Layer three: your savings.** The Chapter 8 audit, totaled — 403(b), 457(b), IRAs, old accounts — expressed two ways: the balance, and a conservative monthly translation for planning (professionals can refine; the page just needs a defensible placeholder). Plus the **HIS** footnote: retirees receive the Health Insurance Subsidy — **$7.50 per year of service, up to $225/month** — real money that belongs on the page, and a reminder (Book Two) that health coverage itself is a cost the page must face.

One line item deserves its own preview flag before you total anything: **health coverage between retirement and 65.** If your realistic retirement date lands before Medicare age, the bridge years' premiums are often the largest new expense on the whole page — routinely several hundred to over a thousand dollars a month per person depending on the route (retiree coverage, COBRA, marketplace). Book Two's eighth chapter maps the options; the estimate page just needs the honesty: a pre-65 retirement plan without a health-premium line isn't conservative, it's incomplete.

**Then the other side: spending.** The layer people skip. Not a budget fantasy — the *actual* number: last twelve months of real outflows, adjusted for what retirement changes (commute down, health premiums up, the 3% contribution and payroll deductions gone, the mortgage's remaining years). Retirement math is one comparison: **layers one through three versus this number.** Everything else is commentary.

### Reading the gap

Three findings come off the page, each with a different assignment. **Surplus:** verify it (conservative translations? honest spending?), then Book Two's job is protecting it through the transition. **Small gap** (Denise and Robert's $900): the pre-retirement years are exactly the tool — Chapter 8's stack and catch-ups, DROP window choices, spending adjustments, or a slightly later date; small gaps found early are the most fixable problem in this book. **Large gap:** not a verdict — a *timeline change*. Found at 50 rather than 62, a large gap has a dozen levers; the mistake was never the gap, only the lateness of its discovery. This chapter exists to make lateness impossible.

One discipline governs all three: **date the page and redo it annually.** Estimates drift — raises, markets, rule changes, life. A yearly one-hour refresh (it's on Chapter 12's calendars) keeps the plan tethered to reality instead of to the year you happened to do the math.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Denise and Robert's pad, cleaned up. Layer one: Denise's estimate at 62 — $2,340/month (COLA fraction ~0.55, so partial growth). Layer two: her SS at 67, $1,910; his, $1,480 — ranges noted from 62 to 70. Layer three: combined accounts $310,000 → conservative planning translation ~$1,050/month; HIS ~$180. Income stack ≈ $6,960. Spending page, honest version: $7,850 today, adjusted to ≈ $7,860 in retirement (premiums replace commute). **Gap: ~$900/month.** Their nine-year response, sketched that same morning: activate the 457(b) alongside her existing 403(b) (the stack), point Robert's strongest self-employed years at his SS record, revisit her date (63 vs 62 moves two layers), and re-run the page every January. No products, no panic — arithmetic with a deadline. The feeling never came back to the argument. It didn't need to; the number had taken its seat.

> ❝ *You can't reconcile feelings. You can reconcile numbers.*

#### What can go wrong

- Estimating annually and living monthly.
- One number for Social Security instead of the claiming range.
- Counting DROP accumulation as monthly income before it's been given a job.
- Optimistic savings translations — retirement plans built on 10% assumptions.
- Skipping the spending side entirely, which makes the whole page decoration.
- Doing it once at 58 instead of annually from 45.

#### What to check before deciding

Book the Saturday hour. Pull the four documents: MyFRS estimate, both SSA statements, the Chapter 8 audit, twelve months of real spending. Build the page in today's dollars. Date it. Calendar next year's.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. What does each layer actually pay monthly, from official sources — not memory?
2. Which layers grow (COLA fraction, SS COLA, investments) and which stand still?
3. What is our honest spending number, adjusted for what retirement changes?
4. What is the gap — and which levers fit its size and our timeline?
5. When is next year's refresh scheduled?

> **Ask your team:** MyFRS for the official estimate; ssa.gov for statements; a tax professional or fee-only adviser to pressure-test the translations and the gap plan.

> **BENOWITZ WEALTH PRINCIPLE** — *A retirement paycheck should be planned before the last work paycheck stops.*

#### Chapter checklist

- [ ] Three layers pulled from official sources, in monthly terms
- [ ] Honest spending number computed from real outflows
- [ ] Gap identified, sized, and matched to levers
- [ ] Page dated; annual refresh calendared
- [ ] Both spouses can explain the page unassisted

#### Reflect

1. Which had more authority in your household before tonight — the feeling or the number?
2. Which layer surprised you most on paper?
3. If your gap is fixable, what's the first lever — and if it's zero, what needs verifying before you trust it?

> **For You and Your Spouse** — Build the page *together*, trading the pen every layer. The rule for the hour: no blame for old numbers, no credit for old feelings. Just the stack, the spending line, and the calm that comes from finally seeing them on the same page.

## CHAPTER 10 — Getting Your House Ready

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Officer Womack could tell you his retirement eligibility date from memory — March of his 25th year — and had a countdown app on his phone to prove it. What he could not tell you was what his household actually spent in a month, how much of his truck was still owed to the bank, or where the emergency money was, exactly, if the AC died in August.
>
> His wife Carla knew. Carla had been quietly managing all of it for nineteen years — the accounts, the autopays, the folder of passwords Womack had never opened. Which worked fine, in the way things work fine right up until the stakes change. One evening she slid a legal pad across the table (their marriage counselor's suggestion, adopted with eye-rolls that didn't last): "You know the pension. I know the house. If we're retiring in six years, we're going to need to know each other's half."
>
> It took them three Sundays. It was less romantic than the countdown app and worth roughly a hundred times more.

### The benefit is only half the plan

Nine chapters of this book have been about the *system* — formulas, elections, DROP clocks, account stacks. This chapter is about the *household* the system's money will land in, because the honest truth about retirement readiness is that it has two halves, and the second one is where most of the actual stress lives. A well-timed DROP entry pouring into a household with a $740 truck payment, no cash reserve, and a spending number nobody's ever computed is a strong benefit funding a shaky building.

Four load-bearing walls, in order of leverage:

**Debt, on a deadline.** The question isn't "is debt bad" — it's *which debts cross the retirement line with you*, and on purpose or by accident. List every balance with its payoff date next to your target retirement date. Consumer debt (cards, vehicles, personal loans) riding into fixed-income years is the classic self-inflicted gap from Chapter 9; the pre-retirement years are precisely when aggressive payoff math works best, because raises and catch-up-era income can be aimed at it. The mortgage is a genuinely two-sided question (payoff certainty versus liquidity and rate math) that deserves professional conversation rather than a slogan — but it deserves the conversation *years* out, not months.

**A real cash reserve.** Working households borrow resilience from the paycheck: surprise expenses get absorbed by the next deposit. Retired households can't — and Book Three will show why forced withdrawals in bad markets are the quiet killer of good plans. The habit starts now: a named reserve, in boring insured savings, sized in months of the Chapter 9 spending number (working years: commonly discussed at three-ish months; the retirement-sized reserve gets built during the countdown). If your household's current reserve is "the credit card," this wall is first.

**The spending number, owned by two people.** Chapter 9 computed it; this chapter institutionalizes it. Not a restrictive budget — an *instrument panel*: what does this household actually run on monthly, which lines are fixed versus flexible, and what changes at retirement. Households that know their number make every other decision in this series faster and calmer. Households that don't are planning a trip without knowing the fuel burn.

**One Florida-specific line worth planning early: the leave payout.** Many public employers pay out unused annual leave (and sometimes a portion of sick leave, per local policy) at separation — for long careers, a genuinely large final check. Two planning notes, both time-sensitive: the payout's *tax treatment* makes it one of the most withholding-surprised dollars in public retirement, and some employers permit directing part of it into deferred compensation, subject to plan limits and election deadlines that typically must be handled *before* the final pay period. Translation: the leave payout is a decision with a calendar, not a bonus with a bow. Ask HR what your policy pays and what elections exist, a year out — and put the answer on the Chapter 9 page and in front of a tax professional. (Book Two returns to it in the paperwork chapter.)

**The knowledge audit — Carla's legal pad.** In most long marriages, financial knowledge has quietly specialized: one spouse knows the pension, one knows the bills; one holds the passwords, one holds the paperwork. Efficient — and fragile, in exactly the years when illness, widowhood (Book Three faces it squarely), or plain overload will test it. The audit is simple and slightly awkward: each spouse writes what they *actually* manage and know; then you teach each other the halves, and build the one-page household map — accounts, institutions, autopays, passwords' location, documents' location — that either of you could run the house from. Three Sundays. A hundred times the countdown app.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* The Womacks' three Sundays, itemized. **Sunday one — the list:** truck ($21,400 left, payoff two years *past* his date at current payments), cards ($6,300 revolving), mortgage (nine years left — landing three years past retirement, a conscious keep-or-kill decision they tabled for a professional). Reserve: $2,100 and a credit card. Spending number: computed for the first time — $6,400/month, with $610 they couldn't explain (they found it; it was subscriptions and lunch). **Sunday two — the plan:** truck payments raised to clear it eighteen months *before* the line; cards attacked with this year's raise; reserve automated to $150/paycheck toward a three-month target, to be tripled during the final countdown years. **Sunday three — the map:** Carla taught the household; Womack taught the pension; one laminated page went in the fire box. Total cost: zero products, three Sundays, some pride. Total effect: every remaining chapter of this series now lands in a household that can actually catch it.

> ❝ *A strong benefit landing in a shaky household is still a shaky retirement.*

#### What can go wrong

- Counting down to a date while the truck loan counts past it.
- "The credit card is our emergency fund."
- One-spouse knowledge systems meeting two-spouse emergencies.
- Treating the mortgage question as a bumper sticker instead of a math problem.
- Doing all nine previous chapters perfectly and skipping this one because it's unglamorous. It's the load-bearing one.

#### What to check before deciding

Build the three lists this month: every debt with payoff date versus retirement date; the reserve's real size and location; the spending number from Chapter 9, now assigned an owner (both of you). Then schedule the knowledge audit — and actually hold it.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. Which debts currently cross my retirement line, and which cross it on purpose?
2. What's our reserve target in months — and what's automated toward it?
3. Can each spouse state the household's monthly number within $200?
4. Could the non-manager spouse run this house from the map — today?
5. What's our mortgage question, precisely, and who's professionally reviewing it?

> **Ask your team:** a fee-only adviser or nonprofit credit counselor for payoff sequencing; a tax professional before any large payoff funded from retirement accounts (Book Two explains why that order matters).

#### Chapter checklist

- [ ] Debt list built, payoff dates versus retirement date
- [ ] Reserve named, automated, target set
- [ ] Spending number owned by both spouses
- [ ] Household map drafted; knowledge audit held
- [ ] Mortgage question scheduled for professional review, unhurried

#### Reflect

1. Which wall is weakest in your house — and how long have you known?
2. What does your household's "Carla" carry alone that shouldn't be carried alone?
3. What would it feel like to cross your retirement date with no consumer debt and a funded reserve — and what would it cost, monthly, starting now?

## CHAPTER 11 — Common Pre-Retirement Mistakes (A Field Catalog)

> **Fictional Example**

Small check, permanent question.

Coach D's decade: a high-fee product sold with donuts, never audited, surrender charges discovered only when moving the money finally came up. **Counter-move:** the Chapter 8 one-page audit, annually. What you own, what it costs, in writing.

### 6. The Stack Never Used

Peak-earning years — the exact window when the 403(b)/457(b) double limit and the catch-up ladder could close a Chapter 9 gap — spent contributing a token percentage to one account because nobody explained the second one existed. **Counter-move:** one HR email: *"Do I have access to both a 403(b) and a 457(b), and do they offer Roth?"*

### 7. The Beneficiary Time Capsule

An ex-spouse on a twenty-year-old 403(b). A deceased parent on the life insurance. Nobody at all on the IRA. Beneficiary designations generally outrank wills and memories — and they pay whoever the yellowed form says. **Counter-move:** the beneficiary sweep — every account, every policy, verified against current life — annually, and after every family change. Forty minutes; entire inheritances.

### 8. The Single-Spouse Plan

Every decision optimized for the member's lifetime; the survivor's income never modeled. The widow discovers the shape of her situation in the worst possible month. ### 9. The Feeling Instead of the Page

## BACK MATTER

### The whole book on two pages — chapter summaries

**1 · The Retirement You Earned.** Public retirement is a system, not an account — and it runs on two rulebooks split at July 1, 2011, plus rules that changed again in 2023 and 2026. The veteran at the next desk isn't your manual. Confirm your enrollment date, tier, class, and plan on MyFRS, and date-stamp everything you know.

**2 · How the FRS Actually Works.** Five parts: service credit, vesting (6 or 8 years), AFC (highest-5 or highest-8), the percentage value (~1.6% Regular, 3% Special Risk), and your 3% contribution. Normal retirement: 62/30 or 65/33 Regular by tier; 55/25 Special Risk for all, post-2023. Pull real numbers; retire the rules of thumb.

**3 · The Pension Plan.** A defined benefit trades ownership and growth for certainty and lifetime income. The COLA is 3% × your pre-2011 service fraction — zero for post-2011-only service, with a new conditioned 1.5% floor for eligible Special Risk retirees (five-years-retired wait). Know your fraction; assign something in your plan to fight inflation.

**4 · The Investment Plan.** Defined contribution: one-year vesting, real ownership, portability — and you carry market, behavior, and longevity risk. No DROP, ever. Built for the mobile careers the pension forgives least. Know your funds, your fees, and your beneficiary.

**5 · The Comparison and the Second Election.** Five inputs decide fit: time, class, household, temperament, and the DROP door. Every member holds one lifetime plan-switch — irrevocable, math-heavy, most valuable in informed hands, and valuable even unused. Confirm yours exists; re-decide annually on purpose.

**6 · DROP, Part One.** Pension members only, after normal-retirement eligibility: your benefit locks and deposits monthly into an accumulation earning 4%, for up to 96 months (120 instructional, sunsetting mid-2029), while you keep working at full salary. Entry windows are gone. It's not extra accrual, not free money, and not reversible.

**7 · DROP, Part Two.** Timing is one trade: enter earlier and bank more months at 4%, or later and lock a larger formula. Salary trajectory decides — Washingtons wait, Greens go — refined by usable window, class weights, and life's full vote. Run three dates on MyFRS; sketch the exit before the entry.

**8 · The Side Accounts.** 403(b) and 457(b) limits stack ($24,500 each in 2026, plus the catch-up ladder: $8,000 at 50, $11,250 at 60–63, special catch-ups beyond). The 457(b) waives early-withdrawal penalties after separation — public safety's bridge. New in 2026: $150k+ earners' catch-ups must be Roth (with 457/403(b) special-catch-up exemptions). Audit what you own and what it costs.

**9 · The Income Estimate.** Three layers — FRS, Social Security (WEP/GPO now repealed), savings plus HIS ($7.50/service year, $225 max) — against your honest monthly spending number. Feelings can't be reconciled; numbers can. Build the page in one Saturday hour, both spouses, dated, refreshed annually.

**10 · The Household.** Four walls: debts that don't cross the retirement line by accident, a real cash reserve, a spending number both spouses own, and the knowledge audit that lets either of you run the house. A strong benefit landing in a shaky household is still a shaky retirement.

**11 · The Field Catalog.** Ten patterns from the benefits office: the pension-costing refund, the coworker's rulebook, the forgotten second election, the hallway DROP entry, the lounge annuity, the unused stack, the beneficiary time capsule, the single-spouse plan, the untested feeling — and their ancestor, the exit-meeting education. The counter-moves are Chapters 1–10.

**12 · The Countdown.** Ten years: build knowledge. Five: aim the money. Three: decide the big shapes. One: verify everything. Ninety days: execute slowly. Enter wherever you stand, compress what you've missed, and refuse to be busy later.

### Glossary

**AFC (Average Final Compensation)** — The salary average your formula uses: highest 5 fiscal years (pre-7/2011 enrollees) or highest 8 (later enrollees).
**Beneficiary designation** — The account-level form that generally outranks wills and memories. Sweep annually.
**Catch-up contributions** — Extra deferrals at 50+ ($8,000 in 2026), with a larger 60–63 amount ($11,250) and special 457(b)/403(b) versions.
**COLA fraction** — Your pension's annual adjustment: 3% × (pre-July-2011 service ÷ total service). Zero for post-2011-only service; see also the 2026 Special Risk floor.
**Defined benefit / defined contribution** — Promise-based (Pension Plan) versus account-based (Investment Plan) retirement design.
**DROP** — Deferred Retirement Option Program: benefit locks and accumulates monthly at 4% interest for up to 96 months (120 instructional) while you keep working. Pension Plan members only.
**FRS** — Florida Retirement System: the multi-employer system covering state, county, district, university, and many municipal employees.
**HIS (Health Insurance Subsidy)** — Retiree subsidy of $7.50/month per year of service (min $45, max $225), with coverage certification required.
**Investment Plan** — The FRS defined-contribution option: one-year vesting, member-directed, portable, no DROP.
**MyFRS** — The system's official member portal: estimates, choice services, records, forms.
**Normal retirement** — The age/service point where the unreduced formula benefit is payable (62/30 or 65/33 Regular by tier; 55/25 Special Risk).
**Pension Plan** — The FRS defined-benefit option: formula income for life, funded by employers and your 3%.
**Roth / pre-tax** — Taxed-now-tax-free-later versus deduct-now-taxed-later treatment; 2026 adds mandatory Roth catch-ups for prior-year FICA wages over $150,000.
**Second election** — Each member's one-time, irrevocable switch between the two plans, exercisable while actively employed.
**Service credit** — Your years (and purchasable additions) inside the formula.
**Special Risk Class** — Law enforcement, fire, corrections, EMS and related roles: 3% multiplier, 55/25 normal retirement.
**Vesting** — Service required to own the benefit: 6 years (pre-7/2011) or 8 (after); 1 year in the Investment Plan.
**457(b) separation rule** — Withdrawals after separation from service avoid the 10% early-withdrawal penalty at any age — the public employee's bridge account.
