### How to use this book

This book covers the transition window — roughly the final year of work through the first ninety days of retirement. It assumes Book One's foundation (your rulebook page, your income estimate, your household map); where it matters, quick recaps appear with pointers rather than repetition.

Three reading paths:

- **You have time — a year or more.** Read front to back, in order. The chapters are sequenced the way the decisions actually arrive.
- **A form is sitting on your table right now.** Go directly to the chapter that owns it — Chapter 2 for the pension option election, Chapter 4 for DROP exit, Chapter 9 for Medicare — read it, then read Chapter 11's Before-You-Sign master checklist before your pen moves.
- **You're the spouse.** Chapters 2 and 3 were written with you in the room on purpose — the law itself requires your acknowledgment on certain elections, and this book's position is stronger than the law's: nothing permanent should be chosen without you at the table. Start there, then Chapter 7.

One rule governs the whole book, and it deserves its own paragraph: **nothing in the retirement transition is so urgent that it can't survive one more week of understanding.** Deadlines exist — this book maps every one — but almost every "sign it today" moment in this passage is manufactured by convenience, not by rules. When the two collide, the rules win, and the rules almost always give you more time than the line behind you at the benefits office suggests. Use it.

### Introduction: Thirty years, thirty days

Every spring, in benefits offices from Pensacola to the Keys, the same scene repeats. A public employee — thirty years of service, competent at everything, trusted by everyone — sits down across from a coordinator with a folder. Inside the folder: an option election that determines what a surviving spouse lives on. A distribution form governing the largest sum of money the family has ever held. Withholding elections, insurance continuations, beneficiary confirmations. The coordinator is kind, knowledgeable, and has eleven more appointments today.

Forty minutes later, the folder is signed.

Nothing about that scene is scandalous. Most of those retirements turn out fine. But "most" is doing heavy lifting in that sentence, and the ones that don't turn out fine share a signature feature: **the decision was executed before it was understood, because the calendar arrived before the knowledge did.** The widow receiving nothing because Option 1 paid more per month. The DROP check cashed instead of rolled, taxed in one catastrophic year. The Medicare window missed by a retiree who had "coverage through the county," with a penalty that never expires. The rollover captured at a steak-dinner seminar by the only person who ever explained anything — explained it wrong, but explained it.

This book replaces that scene. Twelve chapters, sequenced as the decisions arrive: the transition map and its points of no return (Chapter 1); the four pension options, flat on the table (Chapter 2); the family math underneath them (Chapter 3); leaving DROP without a tax disaster (Chapters 4–5); assembling the retirement paycheck (Chapter 6); Social Security timing (Chapter 7); the health-coverage bridge and Medicare's unforgiving windows (Chapters 8–9); the first years' taxes (Chapter 10); the paperwork itself, with a master checklist standing guard (Chapter 11); and the first ninety days on the other side (Chapter 12).

None of it is hard. All of it is permanent-adjacent. The difference between the families who cross this passage calmly and the ones who carry regrets is rarely intelligence or income. It's sequence and speed: they learned first and signed second, and they refused to let anyone — including their own excitement — rush the order.

Thirty years earned this. Thirty days shouldn't be allowed to spend it.

## CHAPTER 1 — Where the Mistakes Happen

> **Fictional Example**
>
> The retirement party was lovely. That was part of the problem.
>
> Sandra, twenty-nine years with the clerk of court's office, spent her final month the way most people do: wrapping up cases, training her replacement, being celebrated. The retirement paperwork got one evening — a Tuesday, after cake, tired. Her husband Gil offered to sit with her; she waved him off. "It's just forms, honey."
>
> It was, in fact, four permanent decisions, one closing window, and a tax election, wearing the costume of forms. Sandra is fine — mostly. But three years later she can list, from memory, the two she'd take back: an option election made "because it was the biggest number," and a distribution she didn't know could have been split. Nobody rushed her, exactly. The calendar did. The cake did. The costume did.
>
> "It's just forms" — she tells the people retiring behind her now — "is the most expensive sentence in this building."
>
> Gil, for the record, never says he offered to help. He doesn't have to. Sandra says it for him, to every retiring coworker, as the second bullet on her index card: "Take the spouse. Not for support. For the second set of questions. Gil would have asked what happens to me under this option, because that's the only question he had. I didn't ask it, because I had forty others." 

### The transition, mapped

The retirement transition is not one event; it's a corridor of decisions, and the first discipline is simply seeing the whole corridor before entering it. In rough sequence: the retirement date election → the **pension option election** (or, for DROP members, this happened at entry — Book One, Chapter 6) → **DROP exit and distribution** decisions, if applicable → rollover choices for DROP and side accounts → Social Security claiming → health coverage bridging (pre-65) or **Medicare enrollment** (at 65, with windows) → HIS application → tax withholding setup → beneficiary confirmations → final employer items (leave payouts, insurance continuations).

Now sort the corridor by a single question — *can this be undone?* — because the answer divides everything:

**Effectively permanent:** the pension option election (once benefits begin, treat it as carved — confirm the exact change rules for your situation, but plan as if there are none) · DROP entry and its locked calculation (already behind DROP members) · a cashed DROP distribution and its tax year · a missed Medicare enrollment window's penalty · certain Social Security claiming consequences (limited do-overs exist, with strict conditions — never assume yours applies).

**Correctable, at a price:** withholding elections · most rollover destinations · Medigap and drug-plan selections (within rules and windows) · budget and paycheck design.

**Freely adjustable:** almost everything else — which is precisely why the permanent list deserves 90% of your preparation time and usually receives 10%.

### The slow-down doctrine

Every chapter ahead enforces the same protocol on the permanent list, so let's state it once, fully:

1. **Get the form early.** Every retirement form can be obtained and read weeks before it's due. Request the packet the moment your date is set; read at kitchen-table speed, not appointment speed.
2. **Both spouses, every permanent decision.** Not as courtesy — as structure. Chapter 2's elections literally determine the survivor's income; several require spousal acknowledgment by law. This book requires presence.
3. **Questions answered in writing.** "The coordinator said" is not documentation. For anything permanent, get the answer on paper — from the Division of Retirement, MyFRS, SSA, or Medicare — before relying on it.
4. **One professional review for the big three.** The option election, the DROP distribution, and the Social Security timing decision each justify a session with a tax professional and/or fee-only adviser. Three meetings against three decades of consequences.
5. **One night's sleep, minimum.** Between the completed review and any irrevocable signature. Nothing legitimate in this corridor expires overnight.

That's the whole doctrine. It costs a few weeks of lead time and a few hundred dollars of professional review. What it protects took thirty years to earn.

### Real deadlines versus manufactured ones

Since the doctrine's whole engine is time, learn to tell the two kinds of deadline apart. **Real deadlines** come from rules and print in documents: the DROP distribution window, Medicare enrollment periods, leave-payout election dates, benefit-effective-date cutoffs. They are firm, findable, and — importantly — usually *further out* than the process implies. **Manufactured deadlines** come from convenience and momentum: the appointment slot, the coordinator's queue, the "we like to have these back by Friday," the seminar's "this pricing ends tonight," your own itch to be done. The test is one question, asked politely and in writing when stakes are high: *"What rule sets this date, and what happens under the rules if I take another week?"* Real deadlines answer crisply. Manufactured ones evaporate. In thirty years of transition stories, the signatures people regret were almost never racing a real deadline — they were racing a manufactured one that would not have survived the question.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Replay Sandra's Tuesday with the doctrine installed. Six weeks before her date, she requests the full packet — and discovers the option election inside, which sends her to Chapter 2 territory with time to actually think. Gil is at the table (the acknowledgment form was coming for him anyway). Two questions get emailed to the Division of Retirement and answered in writing; one — "can my distribution be split between cash and rollover?" — turns out to be yes, the exact option she didn't know existed. One ninety-minute session with a tax professional re-shapes the withholding election and flags her leave payout's tax treatment (Book One, Chapter 10's warning, arriving on schedule). Total added time: perhaps six hours across six weeks. On her three-year list of regrets: nothing. The corridor didn't change. Her speed through it did.

> ❝ *"It's just forms" is the most expensive sentence in the building.*

#### What can go wrong

- Meeting the corridor for the first time inside it.
- Spending preparation time proportional to form length instead of decision permanence.
- Treating the coordinator's line as the deadline. The rules set deadlines; the line sets convenience.
- Solo-signing decisions the law itself considers a two-person matter.
- Letting the party month absorb the paperwork month. Schedule them apart.

#### What to check before deciding

Build your personal corridor map this week: list every decision between you and ninety days post-retirement, mark each permanent / correctable / adjustable, and date its true deadline (from the rules, in writing — not from the appointment calendar). The rest of this book fills in the chapters; the map tells you which ones are load-bearing for *you*.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. Which decisions on my corridor are actually permanent — and have I verified the list in writing?
2. What is each true deadline, per the rules rather than the office calendar?
3. Which forms can I obtain today, weeks early?
4. Which of the big three (option, DROP distribution, Social Security timing) apply to me, and who's professionally reviewing each?
5. Where might I be rushed — and what's my sentence for slowing it down?

## CHAPTER 2 — Your Benefit Payment Options: 1, 2, 3, and 4

*Option rules reflect FRS provisions as of July 2026. This chapter explains the choices; it does not recommend one. Confirm current rules and your specific figures with the Division of Retirement before electing.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Deputy Reyes — Book One's Deputy Reyes, now at the threshold — laid four index cards on the kitchen table, one per option, the way his attorney sister-in-law had suggested. "Don't read the form," she'd said. "The form is a menu written by lawyers. Make it four cards and read them to Elena."
>
> Card by card, he read. Elena stopped him on the first one. "So Option 1 pays the most every month... and if you die Tuesday, I get nothing Wednesday?" That's what the card said. "And the office just... lets people pick that? Married people?"
>
> "They make you sign that you know I picked it," Reyes said. "There's a form for you."
>
> Elena was quiet for a moment. "Good," she said finally. "Because I have opinions."
>
> That — the opinions, at the table, before the election — is this chapter working as intended.
>
> One more beat from that kitchen. Reyes asked, honestly, why the office doesn't just do this — four cards, plain words — for everyone. Elena, who has managed a medical front desk for twenty years, answered before the question finished: "Because the office processes elections. It doesn't live with them. The living-with-it part is ours." She picked up the third card again. "So we do this part like it's ours." 

### The election, plainly

At retirement — or, for DROP members, **at DROP entry** (Book One, Chapter 6's most surprising sentence, repeated here on purpose) — every Pension Plan member elects how the benefit pays. The election shapes two things at once: the size of your monthly check, and what, if anything, continues to anyone else after your death. Once benefits begin, treat the election as **permanent** — narrow change provisions exist in limited circumstances, but no plan should ever be built on them.

The four options, as four cards:

**Option 1 — Your life only, maximum check.** The largest monthly amount the formula produces. Payments continue for exactly as long as you live, and stop at your death — a surviving spouse receives no continuing monthly benefit from this election (only any unrecovered member contributions, typically modest). The card's honest summary: *most money, no survivor protection.*

**Option 2 — Ten years guaranteed, then your life.** A reduced monthly amount. If you die within the first ten years of retirement, your named beneficiary receives the same monthly amount for the remainder of that ten-year window — then payments end. Live past year ten, and it behaves like Option 1 thereafter. Notably, the Option 2 beneficiary can be anyone, not only a spouse. Summary: *a decade of insurance, not a lifetime of it.*

**Option 3 — Two lifetimes, full amount.** A further-reduced monthly amount that continues **unchanged for as long as either of you lives** — you, or your joint annuitant (a spouse, or certain qualifying dependents). The classic survivor-protection election. Summary: *the same check until the second funeral.*

**Option 4 — Two lifetimes, with a step-down.** An adjusted amount that pays while **both** of you are living, then reduces to **two-thirds upon the death of either one of you** — read that carefully: the reduction happens whichever of you dies first, including the joint annuitant. Summary: *more than Option 3 while you're both here, less for whoever remains.*

Two structural notes complete the tour. **The spousal acknowledgment:** if you're married and elect Option 1 or 2, your spouse must sign an acknowledgment that they're aware of the election — the system's way of ensuring Elena's Wednesday scenario never arrives as a surprise. The acknowledgment is not consent, and it is not protection; it is only proof of awareness. This book's standard is higher: awareness plus arithmetic plus agreement (Chapter 3). **The pricing:** the reductions from Option 1 to the others aren't penalties — they're actuarial pricing of the survivor coverage, influenced by both ages. Younger joint annuitants mean longer expected coverage and deeper reductions. Your exact four numbers come from the Division of Retirement's estimate, and no decision should proceed without all four, in writing, side by side.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical, illustrative figures.* Reyes's four cards, priced by his official estimate at 52, with Elena, 50, as joint annuitant: Option 1 — $5,000/month, his life only. Option 2 — roughly $4,890, ten-year guarantee. Option 3 — roughly $4,320, unchanged across both lifetimes. Option 4 — roughly $4,610 while both live, stepping to about $3,075 at the first death, *whichever of them it is*.
>
> Now the reading that matters. The gap between Options 1 and 3 — about $680/month — is effectively the *premium* for insuring Elena's income for life. Whether that premium is worth paying is not a form question; it's a question about Elena's own pension (she has a small one), their savings, their ages, their health, and what $4,320 versus $0 means to a 78-year-old widow — the entire subject of Chapter 3. What the cards accomplish tonight is narrower and essential: for the first time, both spouses can see all four futures priced side by side, and the words "biggest number" have quietly lost their authority.

> ❝ *The acknowledgment form proves awareness. It does not prove arithmetic.*

#### What can go wrong

- Electing by check size — Sandra's regret, the most common error in the corridor.
- DROP members discovering the election happened at entry, years after skimming it.
- Misreading Option 4's step-down as survivor-only. It triggers at *either* death.
- Treating the spousal acknowledgment as a formality instead of a family alarm bell.
- Electing without all four figures in writing, from your actual estimate.

#### What to check before deciding

Obtain the official option-by-option estimate — all four numbers, your ages, in writing. Confirm who can serve as your joint annuitant. If any window exists to adjust an election in your circumstances, get its exact boundaries from the Division of Retirement in writing. Then do not decide yet: Chapter 3 first.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. What are my actual four numbers, side by side, from the official estimate?
2. What does each option pay my survivor — in dollars, not descriptions?
3. Exactly how permanent is this election in my situation, per the Division in writing?
4. If I'm in (or entering) DROP — when does this election actually occur for me?
5. What does the Option 1-to-Option 3 gap buy, and what else could buy it? (Chapter 3's question — carried in, not answered here.)

> **Ask your team:** Division of Retirement for the four-figure estimate and permanence rules; a fee-only adviser and tax professional for the Chapter 3 analysis before any election.

## CHAPTER 3 — Choosing as a Family

*This chapter offers frameworks for a household decision, not a recommendation. Insurance concepts are education only; product decisions involve licensed professionals and individual underwriting.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> The Harmons — both teachers, both retiring within eighteen months of each other — did the option analysis twice, because the first time they did it wrong in an instructive way.
>
> The first pass optimized *his* election in a vacuum: spreadsheets of his four numbers, break-even ages, internet forum wisdom. The second pass — after their adviser asked one reorienting question — optimized something else entirely: *"Don't show me his pension. Show me the household's income in four futures: both alive, him surviving, her surviving, and the long widowhood nobody budgets for."*
>
> On that page, everything changed. Her pension was smaller and had no COLA. His Social Security was the household's largest single stream — and would partially vanish at his death under survivor rules. The "obvious" election from pass one left the her-surviving future nearly $2,300 a month short at age 80. "We weren't choosing his option," Marla Harmon said afterward. "We were choosing my eighties. I just hadn't been invited to the math."

### The four-futures page

The option election makes sense only inside the household's complete picture, so build that picture first — one page, four columns:

**Column 1: both alive.** Every income stream, monthly: pension(s) under each candidate option, both Social Security benefits, sustainable draw from savings, HIS. This is the retirement you've been planning.

**Columns 2 and 3: each survivor future.** Here's where honest math gets uncomfortable, because widowhood changes income on *both* sides of the ledger at once. The pension continues per the option elected — $0 under Option 1, full under Option 3, two-thirds under Option 4. **Social Security consolidates:** a surviving spouse generally keeps the larger of the two benefits, not both — for many couples, an instant loss of a third or more of household Social Security. Savings remain but must now last one lifetime under one person's management. Meanwhile expenses fall far less than people assume — the mortgage, insurance, property taxes, and utilities don't halve at a funeral — and taxes actually tighten (the survivor soon files single, with narrower brackets: Book Three's "widow's penalty" chapter, previewed here on purpose).

**Column 4: the long survivorship.** Run column 2 or 3 out fifteen and twenty years with inflation working on whatever doesn't grow. This is the future the election most exists to protect, and the one the biggest-number reflex most reliably starves. Statistically and actuarially, this column most often belongs to a wife — which is why this book insists she's at the table when it's written.

With the page built, the election question finally becomes answerable in plain terms: **which option keeps every column livable?** Not optimal — *livable*, by your household's own definition, written in the margin.

### The life-insurance alternative — handled honestly

Somewhere in this process, someone will present the alternative: elect Option 1 (maximize the check), and buy life insurance to manufacture the survivor protection yourself — sometimes called pension maximization in sales literature. This book's job is not to bless or ban it; it's to hand you the honest examination, because the idea is legitimate *in concept* and frequently oversold *in practice*.

The concept: the Option 1–3 monthly gap is a premium; if commercial insurance can deliver equivalent survivor income for less, the arithmetic could favor it. The examination the concept must survive: **Is the coverage actually equivalent?** (The pension's survivor benefit is lifetime, inflation-touched by your COLA fraction, and cannot lapse; a term policy ends — and the survivor risk doesn't.) **Will it stay in force for life, at what premiums?** (Permanent coverage priced honestly at your age, underwritten against your actual health, is the real comparison — not a teaser term quote.) **What happens if premiums slip during a hard decade?** (An elected Option 3 cannot be forgotten in a drawer. A policy can.) **And who profits from the recommendation?** (The election pays no commission. The alternative does. That fact settles nothing by itself — and belongs on the table anyway.)

Run it with real quotes, real underwriting, a tax professional's eyes, and a fiduciary's incentives — or don't run it at all. What it must never be is a steak-dinner slide.

One timing note completes the family framework, because DROP members need it early: since the option election occurs **at DROP entry** (Book One, Chapter 6), the four-futures work in this chapter belongs *years* before the exit party — at entry-decision time. A household entering DROP is electing survivor protection now, for a retirement that begins the better part of a decade later; ages, health, and the other spouse's benefits should be read as they'll be *then*, not just as they are today. Couples who discover this chapter at exit have discovered it after their election. Couples who find it at entry get to use it. Hand this book to the friend behind you accordingly.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* The Harmons' four-futures page, condensed. Both alive: $9,140/month across two pensions (his four options pending), two Social Security checks, modest draw, two HIS subsidies. His-surviving future: livable under every option — her pension continues, his was never the fragile column. Her-surviving future — the column that ran short: under his Option 1, $5,290 at today's prices and *flat*; under Option 3, $7,610; under Option 4, about $6,830. Their livability line, written in the margin after real budget work: $6,800. The page didn't make the election — the Harmons did, choosing between 3 and 4 with numbers instead of vibes, and pricing an insurance quote against the gap for completeness (it didn't survive the underwriting math at his age; sometimes it does). What the page ended permanently: any version of this decision that happened to Marla instead of with her.

> ❝ *You're not choosing an option. You're choosing someone's eighties.*

#### What can go wrong

- Optimizing one pension in a vacuum instead of the household's four futures.
- Forgetting Social Security consolidates at the first death — the quiet second cut.
- Assuming expenses halve when the household does. They don't.
- Buying pension maximization from the person selling the policy, unexamined.
- Building the page without the person whose eighties it describes.

#### What to check before deciding

Build the four-futures page with real figures: the official four option numbers, both SSA statements, honest expenses, the survivor's tax status shift. Define "livable" in writing. Only then return to Chapter 2's cards — and if the insurance alternative is on your table, subject it to the full examination with independent professionals before it earns a second look.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. What does each column pay, in dollars, under each candidate option?
2. Where is our livability line — and which options keep every column above it?
3. What happens to household Social Security at each first death?
4. If considering insurance instead: equivalent, lifetime, lapse-proof, honestly underwritten — and says who?
5. Whose eighties does this election actually protect — and were they at the table?

> **Ask your team:** the Division of Retirement for option figures; SSA for survivor-benefit estimates; a tax professional for the filing-status shift; a fee-only adviser to build and stress the page.

## CHAPTER 4 — Leaving DROP: The Exit

*DROP distribution rules and deadlines are strict and current as of July 2026 — confirm yours in writing with the Division of Retirement. Tax treatment is education only; involve a qualified tax professional before electing anything.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Two firefighters exited DROP the same summer, eight figures of combined career service between them, and their distributions told opposite stories by April.
>
> Lieutenant Brooks — Book One's Brooks, whose accumulation had grown past $560,000 across his full window — treated the exit as a project with a deadline. Ninety days out, he had the distribution forms, a tax professional, and a plan: direct rollover of nearly everything, a modest cash portion sized *with* the tax math, withholding understood in advance. His April was an errand.
>
> His academy classmate Ortiz treated the exit as a payday. The full accumulation, paid directly — because it was his money, because a truck and a dock and a daughter's wedding were waiting, because nobody sat him down. Twenty percent vanished to mandatory withholding before the check printed. April revealed the twenty percent hadn't covered it: the lump had stacked on top of his pension and salary in a single tax year, driving his bracket to heights his career had never seen. The truck was nice. The tax bill outlived it.
>
> Same program. Same table of options. The difference was sequence: Brooks decided, then signed. Ortiz signed, then learned.
>
> The epilogue is worth its paragraph. Ortiz is fine — firefighters are durable, and the rolled-up sleeves that rebuilt his savings were the same ones that earned them. But ask him about it at the station and he'll give you the line that belongs on the wall of every exit meeting: "The check had a comma in it, and the comma did my thinking. Don't let the comma do your thinking." 

### The exit, mechanically

DROP ends with separation — your resignation effective date from the entry paperwork arrives, and employment with your FRS employer concludes. Two things then happen on schedule: your monthly pension benefit begins arriving at your actual address (the option you elected at entry now live), and your **DROP accumulation** — deposits plus 4% compounding, often the largest sum of your life — awaits your distribution election, due within the required window after termination. That election window is real and short; missing or mishandling it forfeits control you can't recover. Get the current deadline, in writing, before your last day — not after.

Your accumulation has three destinations, combinable:

**Cash (direct payment to you).** Available, immediate — and the tax event of a lifetime, literally. The entire amount paid to you is taxable ordinary income *in that single year*, stacked on top of your salary-plus-pension year. **Mandatory 20% federal withholding** comes off the top before you see a dollar — and, as Ortiz learned, 20% is a down payment, not the bill: large distributions routinely land in brackets well above it, with the balance due at filing.

**Direct rollover.** The accumulation moves trustee-to-trustee — you never touch it — into an IRA, an eligible employer plan, or (a destination many members never hear about) the **FRS Investment Plan** itself, keeping the money inside the system's low-cost institutional structure. No current tax, no withholding, full continued deferral; taxes arrive later, on your schedule, as withdrawals (Chapter 6's paycheck design, Book Three's long game). This is the default posture for money you don't need immediately — which, for most exiting members, is most of it.

**The combination.** The election most families need and fewest know exists (Sandra's regret, resolved): a portion paid as cash — sized deliberately, with tax math, for the real near-term purposes — and the remainder rolled. The planning question is never cash-versus-rollover; it's *how much cash does this year's tax picture accommodate, for which actual purposes,* with a professional running the bracket arithmetic before the form fills in.

### The exit-year tax frame

One framing converts most DROP-exit mistakes into non-events: **your exit year is probably the highest-income year of your life** — final salary, possibly a leave payout (Book One, Chapter 10), your first pension months, and any DROP cash, all stacking in one tax return. Every cash dollar you take joins that stack at your *top* rate. Every rolled dollar waits for a quieter year. That single sentence, understood in advance, is why the combination election exists and why the professional session before this signature is the highest-value meeting in the entire corridor. (Two footnotes for that session's agenda: distributions have their own age-related rules worth confirming for your situation, and rolled dollars eventually meet required minimum distributions — Chapter 10 and Book Three carry those forward.)

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical, simplified.* Brooks's exit, itemized. Accumulation: $562,000. Real near-term needs, listed honestly with his wife: $38,000 (a roof, a bridge fund for the gap before his first pension deposit, one modest celebration). The professional session prices $38,000 of cash *in his stacked exit year* — acceptable — and flags that taking it in January of the *following* year from the rollover IRA instead would land in a lower-bracket year; they split the difference. Election filed: ~$20,000 cash now, $542,000 direct rollover (most to an IRA, a portion to the FRS Investment Plan after comparing costs and access — Chapter 5's homework). Withholding on the cash: understood before the check, not discovered on it. Ortiz's counterfactual, same numbers: $562,000 cash → $112,400 withheld instantly, a six-figure *additional* balance due in April, and — the quieter loss — a half-million dollars that left tax deferral forever on one summer's momentum. The table of options was identical. The sequence was the whole story.

> ❝ *Twenty percent is the down payment, not the bill.*

#### What can go wrong

- Treating the accumulation as a payday instead of a portfolio.
- Learning the 20% withholding exists from the check stub.
- Missing that the combination election exists.
- Blowing the election window itself — the unforced error with no appeal.
- Sizing the cash portion by wishes instead of by the exit-year bracket math.

Book the tax session with all of it in hand. List your genuine near-term cash needs with your spouse — dollar amounts, not moods.

## CHAPTER 5 — Rollovers Without Regret

*Rollover rules are education only and change; confirm specifics with the IRS guidance in force, your plan providers, and a qualified tax professional. Investing involves risk, including possible loss of principal.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> The invitation was heavy cardstock: *An Exclusive Retirement Dinner for Florida's Public Servants.* Free steak, a hotel ballroom, and a presenter who — give him this — was the first person in thirty-one years to explain rollovers to Ms. Delacroix in complete sentences.
>
> He was warm. He was fluent in FRS vocabulary. He praised her service, walked her through "the problems with leaving money in government plans," and had paperwork ready that very week for her DROP rollover and her 457(b) — into an annuity product whose surrender schedule, she'd learn much later, ran nine years, and whose costs ran triple her old plan's. Nothing he did was illegal. Most of what he said was even technically true, arranged in a misleading order.
>
> Her niece, reviewing the statements two years in, said the sentence this chapter exists to move earlier in the story: "Auntie — the dinner wasn't free. It was the most expensive meal of your life. You just hadn't been given anything to compare him to."
>
> Here is the comparison.

### The mechanics: direct, or dangerous

First, the plumbing, because one distinction prevents the most expensive category of rollover accident. A **direct rollover** moves money trustee-to-trustee — you sign a form; the institutions talk; you never hold the funds; nothing is withheld; nothing is taxable now. An **indirect (60-day) rollover** pays *you*, and starts a clock: redeposit the full amount into a qualified account within 60 days or the whole thing becomes a taxable distribution — and because employer-plan payments to you arrive minus 20% mandatory withholding, completing the rollover requires replacing that withheld slice *from your own pocket* until tax time reconciles it. Miss the window — illness, mail, a bank's processing lag, a misunderstanding — and there is very little mercy. The rule this book states without hedging: **for transition-sized money, use direct rollovers, always.** The 60-day route is how disciplined people have accidents.

### The destination decision — an honest comparison

Where the money goes matters as much as how it travels, and here the marketplace's noise is loudest, because destinations pay differently for your arrival. Lay the candidates flat:

**Stay put (or roll within the system).** Leaving money in your 457(b)/403(b), or rolling DROP into the **FRS Investment Plan**, keeps institutional pricing that individual products rarely beat — and preserves plan-specific advantages, the crown jewel being the **457(b)'s no-early-penalty-after-separation rule** (Book One, Chapter 8), which an IRA rollover *extinguishes*. A 52-year-old Special Risk retiree who rolls the 457(b) to an IRA has voluntarily reinstalled a penalty the law had removed for them. Read that twice; the dinner presenters never mention it.

**An IRA.** Maximum flexibility: any custodian, the full investment universe, consolidated statements, easier beneficiary and withdrawal management — real advantages, especially for simplifying a multi-account life (Chapter 6). Costs span from nearly free to ruinous depending entirely on what's *inside* the IRA, which is the comparison's whole point.

**A new employer's plan** (for the encore-career crowd): sometimes available, occasionally advantageous, plan-by-plan.

The comparison sheet — the back-matter worksheet, previewed: for each candidate, in writing: **all-in annual cost** (fund expenses + account/product fees + any advisory layer) · **surrender charges or lockups** (the honest answer for many pitched products; near-zero for staying put) · **access rules at your age** (the 457 rule's fate; IRA age rules) · **services actually received** for any added cost · **who benefits from my arrival, and how are they paid.** Five rows. One page. It would have saved Ms. Delacroix nine surrendered years.

And name the pattern plainly, because regulators have: the retirement-dinner circuit, the "government plans are limited" script, urgency around your exit date, and commission-bearing products presented as the sophisticated choice are a *documented sales architecture* aimed at exactly your demographic in exactly this season. The tell is never the steak; it's the sequence — product first, comparison never. Anyone unwilling to fill out your comparison sheet, in writing, next to your current plan's numbers, has answered your question at the cost of one dinner.

### The tells, collected

For the wallet card: **urgency tied to your exit date** ("we need to move before your separation processes") · **"government plans trap your money"** as a thesis (they are usually the cheapest thing you will ever own) · **guarantees emphasized before costs are mentioned** · **a surrender schedule anywhere in the paperwork** — ask them to read it aloud, watch what happens · **hostility toward your getting a second opinion** ("most advisers don't understand FRS") · **the credential blizzard** — titles are not registrations; the registries are (adviserinfo.sec.gov, BrokerCheck) · and the master tell that contains the others: **any resistance to your five-row sheet.** A legitimate professional fills it out gladly, baseline column included, because comparison is where legitimate offers win. Resistance to comparison *is* the answer.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Ms. Delacroix's decision, re-run with the sheet. Her 457(b): 0.35% all-in, no surrender, penalty-free access at her age 56 — the bridge account working exactly as designed. The dinner annuity: ~2.4% all-in plus a nine-year surrender schedule starting at 8% — and rolling her 457 into it would have added back the under-59½ penalty on any early exit. Her DROP rollover candidates: the FRS Investment Plan at institutional cost, or a plain IRA at a low-cost custodian at ~0.2% — either defensible; the sheet made it a choice instead of a capture. Total time to run the comparison: one evening plus one fee-only review. The niece's arithmetic on the road not taken: roughly $9,000 a year in cost drag on the pitched product, before the surrender handcuffs — per year, compounding, for a product whose principal virtue was that its salesman explained things in complete sentences. Explanation is not advice. The sheet knows the difference.

> ❝ *The dinner wasn't free. It was the most expensive meal of her life.*

#### What can go wrong

- Taking a 60-day rollover when a direct one existed. For this money, it always exists.
- Rolling a 457(b) to an IRA before 59½ without pricing the penalty rule you just extinguished.
- Consolidating *into* high costs for the feeling of simplicity.
- Confusing fluency with fiduciary duty. The dinner circuit is fluent.
- Signing anything with a surrender schedule you can't recite.

#### What to check before deciding

Run the five-row sheet on every candidate destination, current plans included as the baseline. Verify the mover's registration (adviserinfo.sec.gov, BrokerCheck — Book One's reflex, highest stakes yet). Confirm the transfer will be direct, in the paperwork itself. If under 59½ with a 457(b): the penalty-rule question goes first.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. Is this transfer direct, trustee-to-trustee — shown where in the paperwork?
2. What is each destination's all-in annual cost, in writing, next to my current plan's?
3. What access do I lose or gain at my age — the 457 rule especially?
4. What surrender charges or lockups exist — recited, not skimmed?
5. Who profits from my arrival, how much, and who told me?

> **Ask your team:** your current plan providers for the baseline numbers; a tax professional before any distribution paperwork; a fee-only adviser — paid by you, not by the destination — for the sheet review.

## CHAPTER 6 — Building the Retirement Paycheck

*Withdrawal and income concepts are education only, not individualized advice. Investing involves risk, including possible loss of principal.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Denise and Robert — Book One's legal-pad couple — hit an odd wall four months before her date. The plan was funded. The gap they'd found at 53 had been closed by nine years of the stack. And Robert, of all people, couldn't sleep.
>
> "For forty years, money has arrived," he finally said. "Every two weeks, somebody deposits it, and we spend out of what arrives. In January, nothing arrives. We just have... piles. Nobody's ever taught me how to get paid by a pile."
>
> That's this chapter: the mechanics of turning a pension, two Social Security dates, a rollover IRA, a 457(b), and a cash reserve into the thing forty years of adulthood ran on — a paycheck, arriving on schedule, sized to the budget, with taxes already handled. It is the least glamorous chapter in this book and, for households like Robert's, the one that ends the 3 a.m. math.
>
> Denise's contribution to the 3 a.m. conversation deserves recording too. "You realize," she said, "that we already know how to live on a paycheck. We've done it for forty years. We just have to keep *getting* one." Robert considered this. "So we're not learning something new. We're refusing to stop doing something old." Exactly that.

### The paycheck stack, assembled

Book One's Chapter 9 built the estimate; this chapter turns it into plumbing. A retirement paycheck is layers, switched on in a deliberate order:

**Layer one — the guaranteed floor.** Your pension (option elected, Chapter 2) and, when claimed, Social Security. These arrive monthly without management, and the design question is only *withholding* (set it on purpose — Chapter 10) and *timing* (the pension starts at retirement; Social Security starts when Chapter 7 says your analysis concluded, which for many households is deliberately later).

**Layer two — the scheduled draw.** A fixed monthly transfer from your invested accounts (rollover IRA, 457(b), 403(b)) into checking, automated on the first of the month like the paycheck it's replacing. Sized initially from your Chapter 9 page and your professional's sustainability math (Book Three tunes it for the decades); *sourced* deliberately — which account funds the draw first is a genuine tax question, since the 457(b)'s access rules, pre-tax IRA dollars, and any Roth dollars each stack differently on your return (Chapter 10 and a tax professional own that ordering).

**Layer three — the cash buffer.** Book One's reserve, graduated to its retirement job: one to two years of the *scheduled-draw* amount (not of total spending — the floor covers most of that) held in boring insured savings. Its purpose is Book Three's sequence-risk defense in embryo: when markets fall, the draw can sip from the buffer instead of selling investments at the bottom. Retirees with a funded buffer make calmer decisions in every bad year; that calm is the buffer's actual yield.

**The gap-month plan.** One transition-specific wrinkle deserves its own paragraph: there is often a **gap between the last work paycheck and the first benefit deposit** — retirement processing takes time, and Social Security (if claimed) has its own start lag. The buffer's first assignment is bridging that gap without drama; sizing it into your final-year savings (Book One's countdown flagged it) turns a notorious new-retiree panic into a non-event.

### One switchboard, run monthly

The design goal is a system either spouse can run from one page (the household map, now with an income tab): where each layer originates, what date it lands, what it's sized at, and the one monthly ritual — fifteen minutes, statement in hand — confirming everything fired. Resist the instinct to make it clever. Cleverness is for the annual review with professionals (Book Three, Chapter 12); the monthly machine should be boring enough to survive distraction, grief, and Gulf-Coast hurricane season. Robert's pile anxiety, it turns out, wasn't about money at all. It was about *rhythm* — and rhythm is exactly the thing you're allowed to manufacture.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* The Robertses' switchboard, January edition. Floor: Denise's pension $2,340 (withholding set with their tax pro), Social Security deliberately deferred for now per their Chapter 7 analysis. Scheduled draw: $1,450 on the 1st — sourced from her 457(b) first (penalty-free at her 62 regardless, but chosen for tax-ordering reasons their professional mapped), automated. Buffer: $34,000 — fourteen months of draw — which bridged the six-week gap before her first pension deposit without a single tense conversation. Total landing in checking each month: $3,790 against their $3,700 budget line. Robert's 3 a.m. math retired in February. "Turns out I didn't need the piles explained," he said. "I needed payday to exist again."

> ❝ *Nobody teaches you how to get paid by a pile. So we build a payday.*

#### What can go wrong

- No system at all: ad-hoc withdrawals whenever checking runs low — the design that maximizes both taxes and anxiety.
- Sizing the draw from hope instead of the Chapter 9 page and professional math.
- Ignoring source-ordering and paying avoidable taxes for years.
- No gap-month plan; the first six weeks fund themselves on a credit card.
- A system only one spouse can operate. (Book Three's survivor chapter will not forgive this.)

#### What to check before deciding

Draft the switchboard on one page: each layer, source, date, amount, withholding status. Confirm the first-deposit timing for your pension (and any claimed benefits) so the buffer's bridge is sized to reality. Book the source-ordering conversation with your tax professional before the first draw fires.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. What lands, from where, on what date — on one page?
2. What does the gap month actually look like on my processing timeline?
3. Which account funds the draw first, and what does that ordering cost or save in taxes?
4. Is the buffer sized to its two jobs — the bridge now, the bad-year sip later?
5. Could my spouse run this switchboard alone by February?

> **Ask your team:** Division of Retirement for first-payment timing; a tax professional for source ordering and withholding; a fee-only adviser for draw sizing and the buffer design.

## CHAPTER 7 — Social Security: Timing and Coordination

*Social Security rules are federal, individual, and change; figures here are education only. Confirm your numbers at ssa.gov and your decisions with SSA directly — Benowitz Wealth Management does not file claims or represent anyone before SSA.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> The Okafors — Book One's calendar couple — approached Social Security the way they approached everything: as a project with inputs. What surprised them was discovering it wasn't *one* project. It was two claims, three ages each, survivor rules binding them together, and — because Grace had spent six early years teaching in a district that hadn't participated in Social Security — a family memory of something called the "windfall penalty" that had scared her mother's generation of teachers for decades.
>
> "Mom always said the government takes back a teacher's Social Security," Grace said, statements spread across the table.
>
> Emmanuel had done the reading. "They repealed that. Last year. The whole thing — WEP, GPO, gone." Grace stared at him. "So the number on my statement is... the actual number?" It was. Some of her mother's friends were receiving adjustment checks. An entire generation's flinch, suddenly obsolete — and a reminder that in this system, even the fears need annual verification.

### Your claim: one decision, three doors

Most FRS careers pay into Social Security (verify yours: your earnings record at ssa.gov is the ground truth), so most readers hold a claim with three broad doors:

**62 — the early door.** Benefits can start, permanently reduced — for those with a full retirement age of 67 (everyone born 1960 or later), claiming at 62 cuts the check by roughly **30%, for life**. Sometimes right anyway — health, necessity, household math — but "permanently" belongs in the sentence every time it's considered.

**67 — full retirement age.** The statement's headline number, unreduced.

**70 — the patient door.** Every year waited past FRA adds **delayed credits of about 8%**, topping out at 70 — roughly 24% above the FRA amount, and roughly 75–80% above the age-62 amount, *for life, with COLAs compounding on the larger base*.

The break-even arithmetic between doors is real and your professionals can run it — but for pension households, two framings usually matter more than break-even. First, **your pension changes the question**: with a guaranteed floor already arriving (Chapter 6), many FRS retirees don't *need* Social Security at 62 the way a floorless retiree might — which converts delay from a sacrifice into a purchasable upgrade: the household's only inflation-adjusted, government-backed annuity, bought at actuarially fair prices by waiting. Second — and this is the sentence this book most wants remembered — **the higher earner's claiming age is also a survivor decision**: at the first death, the household keeps the *larger* of the two checks (Chapter 3's consolidation). The bigger earner delaying to 70 doesn't just raise today's income; it permanently raises the check the survivor lives on for, potentially, decades. For many couples, that reframes everything: her claim funds the seventies; his claim funds her nineties.

### Coordination notes for the FRS household

**The earnings test, for early claimers still working:** claim before FRA while earning wages, and benefits above an annual earnings limit (set yearly — in the low-to-mid $20,000s in recent years, with a higher limit and gentler rule in the year you reach FRA) get temporarily withheld — roughly a dollar for every two above the limit. Two corrections to the folklore: it applies to *wages*, not to pension or withdrawal income, and withheld amounts are recalculated back into your benefit at FRA — deferred, not confiscated. Still, it converts "retire in June, claim in January, consult a little" into arithmetic worth doing in advance. Retiring mid-year, encore jobs, and DROP-exit consulting gigs all touch this; know the current limit before claiming early with income still flowing.

**WEP and GPO — the repeal, plainly:** the Windfall Elimination Provision and Government Pension Offset — which reduced Social Security for workers (and spouses/survivors) with pensions from *non-covered* government employment — were **repealed by the Social Security Fairness Act, signed January 2025, retroactive to January 2024**. If your career, or your spouse's, includes non-covered public service — some out-of-state teaching, certain local positions — the old planning (and the old fear) no longer applies: statements reflect real numbers, affected retirees received adjustments, and any household strategy built around those offsets deserves a fresh professional look. Grace's mother's warning served its generation. Retire it.

**Spousal and survivor basics, one paragraph:** a lower-earning (or non-earning) spouse may claim up to half the higher earner's FRA benefit under spousal rules; survivors step into the larger check per Chapter 3. The interactions between spousal rules, claiming ages, and pension income are exactly the tangle the SSA appointment and your fee-only review exist to untangle — bring both statements and the four-futures page.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* The Okafors' analysis, condensed. Emmanuel's FRA benefit: $2,150; Grace's (post-repeal, finally trusted): $1,480. Their pensions plus buffer make the floor livable without either claim at retirement (Chapter 6's switchboard confirmed it in rehearsal). The plan their review produced: Grace claims at her FRA — her check funds the travel decade — while Emmanuel, the higher earner, delays to 70, growing his benefit toward roughly $2,670 *plus intervening COLAs*. The quiet headline is Chapter 3's math: whichever Okafor survives the other will live on that $2,670-and-growing for life. "We're not delaying a check," Emmanuel said, closing the folder. "We're funding whoever's left." Their SSA appointment confirmed the numbers; the household calendar got two claiming dates; and Grace mailed her mother a printout about the repeal, circled in red pen.

> ❝ *The higher earner's claiming age is a survivor decision wearing a personal one's clothes.*

#### What can go wrong

- Claiming at 62 by reflex — because the paperwork was there — with a pension floor that made waiting affordable.
- Optimizing two claims separately and missing the survivor consolidation entirely.
- Working while claiming early, unaware of the earnings test.
- Planning around WEP/GPO fears (or strategies) that no longer exist.
- Trusting break-even math alone to answer what is partly a longevity-insurance question.

#### What to check before deciding

Pull both earnings records and statements at ssa.gov (verify the history line by line — corrections are possible and time matters). Bring statements, pension figures, and the four-futures page to a professional review, then to an SSA appointment. If early claiming with any work income is on the table, get the current earnings-test limit first.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. Are both earnings records accurate — checked line by line?
2. What does each claiming age pay each of us — and the survivor, permanently?
3. What does our pension floor make affordable that a floorless household couldn't consider?
4. Does the earnings test touch any version of our plan?
5. Did any of our assumptions predate the WEP/GPO repeal?

> **Ask your team:** ssa.gov and an SSA appointment for records and filing; a fee-only adviser for the household timing analysis; a tax professional for how each claiming age interacts with Chapter 10.

#### Chapter checklist

- [ ] Both records verified; both statements current
- [ ] Three doors priced for each spouse, survivor line included
- [ ] Pension-floor framing applied before any break-even math
- [ ] WEP/GPO-era assumptions audited out
- [ ] Claiming dates chosen, calendared, and filed on purpose

#### Reflect

1. What did your family's generation believe about public employees and Social Security — and does it survive verification?
2. Which decade of whose life does each claiming age actually fund?
3. What would you want the surviving one of you to know about why you chose these dates?

>
> She kept the sticky note. It's on her monitor still, next to a newer one in her own handwriting that she shows to every colleague who mentions retiring before 65: *The subsidy was never yours. It was theirs, and it retires when you do.*

### The gap, named

Medicare begins at 65. Retire earlier — as Special Risk members almost always do, and as 62-ish Regular Class retirements routinely do — and the years between your last work day and your 65th birthday are the **coverage gap**: the stretch where health insurance stops being an invisible payroll line and becomes, for many households, the largest new expense in the entire retirement budget. Book One's estimate page flagged it; this chapter maps the routes across.

**Route one: retiree coverage through your employer.** Many FRS employers let retirees continue group coverage — the familiar network, the familiar card. The catch is arithmetic, not eligibility: the *employer subsidy* that made working coverage affordable typically shrinks or vanishes, leaving you the full (or nearly full) premium. Sometimes it's still the best route; it's never safely assumed. Yolanda's sticky note is the whole assignment: get **your** retiree premium, in writing, for every household member, before the date is set.

**Route two: COBRA-style continuation.** Federal and state continuation rules generally allow keeping the group plan for a limited period (commonly up to 18 months) at full premium plus an administrative margin. Useful as a *bridge to a bridge* — covering a short stretch to 65, or buying time to enroll elsewhere — and rarely economical for a full three-to-five-year gap.

**Route three: the marketplace.** Individual coverage through healthcare.gov, priced by age, county, and — critically — **income**: premium tax credits phase with household MAGI, which for a new retiree is a *plannable* number. The composition of your gap-year income (pension, draws from pre-tax accounts, Roth dollars, cash buffer) directly shapes what coverage costs — one of the most concrete places where Chapter 6's source-ordering and Chapter 10's tax work pay visible monthly dividends. This is professional-conversation territory with real money attached.

**Route four: a working spouse's plan.** Frequently the cleanest bridge of all, and a legitimate input into *whose* retirement date comes first — plenty of Florida households sequence retirements around exactly this.

### The HIS: real money, correctly sized

Into this budget arrives the **Health Insurance Subsidy** — the FRS retiree benefit of **$7.50 per month per year of creditable service** (minimum $45, maximum $225 at 30 years). Two practical mechanics: it requires an application and **certification that you have health coverage** (any qualifying coverage — it is not tied to a specific plan), and for DROP members the service count behind it locked at entry (Book One, Chapter 6). Two sizing truths: $225 a month is real money worth claiming promptly and correctly — and it is a *subsidy*, not a solution; against a $900 gap-year premium it's a fifth of the bill. Budget it as what it is: a helpful, permanent offset that starts with paperwork you should have ready in the first 90 days (Chapter 12).

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Yolanda's gap plan, rebuilt. Retiring at 62 with Medicare at 65: a 36-month canyon. Retiree continuation quote: $940/month, her alone. Marketplace quote at her *planned* gap-year income — pension plus a deliberately modest 457(b) draw, buffer covering the difference — priced meaningfully lower after credits; her tax professional and adviser modeled the income line both ways before she chose. HIS: 28 years × $7.50 = $210/month, certified and flowing by month two. Final budget line: coverage secured for roughly the price of her old car payment — visible, planned, and boring. The alternative timeline — discovering the comma sixty days out — would have cost the same dollars plus a season of panic, or worse, a "we'll go uncovered for a while" gamble that this book declines to dignify as a route. The canyon didn't change. The map did.

> ❝ *The gap years turn an invisible payroll line into the biggest new number in the budget.*

#### What can go wrong

- Assuming "I'll keep my coverage" without pricing the retiree premium in writing.
- Discovering the spouse's coverage question after the date is set.
- Burning the whole gap on continuation coverage priced for a bridge.
- Ignoring how gap-year income composition drives marketplace pricing.
- Leaving HIS unclaimed — or assuming it covers more than a fifth of the problem.
- The uncovered gamble. One diagnosis erases a decade of careful saving.

#### What to check before deciding

Get the written retiree-premium quote for every household member. Price the marketplace at your *planned* retirement income with professional help. Confirm the HIS application, certification requirement, and your locked service count. If a working spouse's plan is the bridge, verify dependent rules and its own timeline. Do all of it **before** the retirement date is chosen — this is a date-setting input, not a post-decision errand.

3. Which route covers each person, each year, from last-day to 65 — mapped month by month?
4. What's my HIS amount, and what does the application require?
5. Should this analysis move my retirement date — or my spouse's?

> **Ask your team:** HR for retiree and continuation quotes; a licensed insurance professional or the marketplace for individual pricing; a tax professional for the income-composition math; the Division of Retirement for HIS mechanics.

## CHAPTER 9 — Medicare at 65

*Medicare rules, premiums, and thresholds shown are 2026 figures and change annually. Education only — confirm your situation with Medicare.gov, SSA, and SHINE (Florida's free counseling program). Benowitz Wealth Management does not sell insurance or enroll anyone in Medicare.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Two retired coworkers, both 65 the same spring, both certain they "didn't need to do anything about Medicare yet."
>
> Al was right — by accident. His wife's active employer coverage counted as qualifying coverage, giving him a protected enrollment window for later. Walt was wrong — almost expensively. His retiree continuation coverage *felt* identical to Al's situation, but retiree coverage generally does **not** count the way active-employment coverage does. Sixty days before his window closed forever into penalty territory, his golf partner — a retired benefits coordinator named Ms. Pat, who cannot stop working even on a golf course — asked one diagnostic question: "Is anyone in your house still *actively employed* at the place providing that coverage?"
>
> Nobody was. Walt enrolled that month, penalty-free, by eleven weeks. The two men had identical instincts and opposite facts — which is Medicare enrollment in one story: the rules don't punish ignorance evenly. They punish it precisely.
>
> Ms. Pat's postscript, delivered on the next tee box, belongs in the chapter: "You know what the saddest part of the penalty cases is? They're all people who *had* coverage. Nobody skips Medicare to be reckless — they skip it because they feel covered. The rule doesn't ask how covered you felt. It asks one question, and it's the question I asked you." 

### The four parts, one map

**Part A — hospital insurance.** Inpatient care. Premium-free for almost everyone with a sufficient work record; most people enroll at 65 regardless of other coverage. (One niche flag: enrolling in any Medicare part ends eligibility to contribute to an HSA — a detail for the still-working-at-65 crowd to time deliberately.)

**Part B — medical insurance.** Doctors, outpatient care, equipment. Premium-bearing: the 2026 standard is **$202.90/month** with a **$283 annual deductible** — and it's the part whose enrollment timing carries the famous penalty (below).

**Part D — drug coverage.** Private plans with their own premiums; carries its *own* separate late-enrollment penalty that accrues monthly and lasts for life. Even readers taking nothing today typically need a plan (a low-cost one) to avoid the meter starting.

**Part C — Medicare Advantage.** The alternative packaging: private plans bundling A, B, usually D, often extras, in exchange for networks and plan rules. The genuine fork in the road is **Original Medicare + Medigap + Part D** versus **Medicare Advantage** — a personal decision about networks, travel, health, and budget that this book maps but does not make. One asymmetry belongs in every version of that conversation: your **Medigap open-enrollment window** — the six months after Part B starts, when supplemental coverage is guaranteed-issue regardless of health — is largely a one-time offer. Choosing Advantage first and switching to Medigap later can mean medical underwriting. Whichever way you choose, choose *knowing* that door swings mostly one way. Florida's free **SHINE** counselors exist for exactly this fork; use them.

### Windows and penalties — the unforgiving part

**Your Initial Enrollment Period (IEP):** seven months bracketing your 65th birthday (three before, the month of, three after). Retired by 65 with no active-employment coverage? This is your window, full stop.

**The Special Enrollment Period (SEP):** still covered by **active employment** (yours or your spouse's) past 65? You can delay Part B penalty-free and enroll during employment or within the months following its end. The trap — Walt's trap — is what *doesn't* count as active-employment coverage for this purpose: **retiree coverage and COBRA-style continuation generally do not protect you.** The diagnostic is Ms. Pat's question, verbatim.

**The penalties, plainly:** late Part B enrollment costs roughly **10% of the premium for each full 12-month period** you were eligible but unenrolled — added to your premium **permanently**. Part D's penalty accrues at about 1% per month and is likewise lifelong. These are the corridor's least forgiving deadlines: no appeal to good intentions, no expiration, and they compound with every future premium increase. The entire defense is calendar awareness eighteen months early — which is why this chapter is in Book Two and not Book Three.

### IRMAA — the income-based surcharge, previewed

Part B and D premiums are income-tested: retirees with **MAGI above $109,000 (single) / $218,000 (joint)** pay **IRMAA** surcharges — 2026 Part B totals run from $284.10 up to $689.90/month at the top tiers — based on your tax return from **two years prior** (2024 income sets 2026 premiums). Two planning facts make IRMAA more than trivia for FRS households: the **two-year lookback means your final working years and DROP-exit year can set your first Medicare premiums** — a stacked exit year (Chapter 4) can echo as a surcharge at 65 — and a **life-changing event appeal (Form SSA-44)** exists precisely for "my income dropped because I retired," frequently reducing the surcharge to match reality. Chapter 10 and Book Three carry the management strategies; this chapter's job is the alarm: know the thresholds, know the lookback, and know the appeal exists.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* Walt's near-miss, priced. Had the golf-course question come a year later, his Part B penalty would have been 10% — roughly $20 a month at 2026 rates — added to every premium for the rest of his life, growing with every increase, for a coverage delay that bought him nothing. His neighbor's actual case ran the other direction: she retired at 64 with a high final-earnings year, turned 65, and received an IRMAA determination based on that peak income — then filed SSA-44 with her retirement documentation and watched the surcharge recalculate to her real retirement income within the season. Same system, two lessons: the penalties are permanent and the surcharges are appealable, and the difference between the two is entirely paperwork filed on time. Ms. Pat, holing out: "Medicare has no mercy and excellent forms. Learn the forms."

> ❝ *The rules don't punish ignorance evenly. They punish it precisely.*

#### What can go wrong

- Believing retiree or continuation coverage protects a Part B delay. It generally doesn't.
- Skipping Part D "because I take nothing" and starting a lifelong meter.
- Sleepwalking past the one-time Medigap guaranteed-issue window.
- Meeting IRMAA as a surprise generated by a two-year-old paycheck — and never filing the appeal.
- Getting the Advantage-vs-Medigap fork explained only by someone paid on one answer.

#### What to check before deciding

Eighteen months before 65 (or before retiring past 65): identify your window — IEP or SEP — using the active-employment test. Map every household member's own timeline. Book a SHINE session for the fork. Pull your two-years-ago MAGI and preview IRMAA; if retirement changed the picture, have SSA-44 ready.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. Which window is mine, per the active-employment test — verified, not felt?
2. What are my A, B, and D enrollment dates — on the calendar?
3. Medigap or Advantage — decided with the one-way-door asymmetry on the table?
4. Does my two-year-old income trigger IRMAA, and does my retirement justify the appeal?
5. Who is walking me through this who sells nothing? (SHINE. The answer is SHINE.)

> **Ask your team:** Medicare.gov and SSA for enrollment; SHINE (Florida's free counseling) for the fork; a tax professional for the IRMAA lookback; HR for the active-employment verification letter you'll want on file.

> **BENOWITZ WEALTH PRINCIPLE** — *You don't need to know everything — you need to know what to ask before it becomes irrevocable.*

- [ ] My window identified via the active-employment test
- [ ] Enrollment dates calendared eighteen months out
- [ ] SHINE appointment booked for the Medigap/Advantage fork

## CHAPTER 10 — Taxes in the First Retirement Years

*Tax education only — not tax advice. Federal rules shown reflect July 2026 law, including temporary provisions, and change; state facts are Florida-specific. Work with a qualified tax professional on your actual return.*

> **Fictional Example**
>
> Robert — the payday-by-pile Robert, now three months into the switchboard's smooth rhythm — opened the mail in late January and found a form he'd never met: a 1099-R. Then another. Then a third. Pension, 457(b) draw, a small IRA distribution — each pile, it turned out, files paperwork.
>

>
> Denise, who had read ahead in this chapter, slid over the one-page tax map they'd built with their professional in November. Every 1099-R on the table already had a line on the map, a withholding rate chosen on purpose, and a projected April outcome within a few hundred dollars of boring. "We're not a filing cabinet," she said. "We're a system with receipts."
>
> Robert studied the map, then the forms, then the map again. "When did you get smart about taxes?" he asked, genuinely. "November," Denise said. "It cost one afternoon and one professional's invoice. Turns out that's when April happens — in November." 

### Your income changed clothes — the map

Working taxes were simple because one employer did the withholding math. Retirement taxes are the same math wearing five outfits, and the first-year job is a one-page map of what's taxable, what withholds, and what you must handle yourself:

**The pension:** taxable as ordinary income, federally. You control withholding via your election (the W-4P-style form in your retirement packet) — set it with professional input, not defaults, because it's the anchor stream the rest of the plan tunes around.

**Account withdrawals:** traditional 457(b)/403(b)/IRA dollars are ordinary income as withdrawn (your Chapter 6 draw is a taxable event on schedule); **Roth** dollars, rules met, come out federally tax-free — which is exactly why source-ordering was a design decision and not a coin flip. DROP cash, if any, already made its statement in the exit year (Chapter 4).

**Social Security:** taxed by a formula of its own — depending on your "combined income," between 0% and **85%** of benefits become taxable (never 100%). The planning insight hiding in that formula: because other income drives how much of Social Security gets taxed, *withdrawal decisions can change your Social Security taxation* — one more place the streams interact and one more line for the professional map.

**Florida's gift, sized honestly:** no state income tax on any of it — pension, withdrawals, Social Security. A genuine structural advantage worth naming exactly once and never inflating: federal tax is the whole conversation here, and it is plenty.

**A temporary federal note:** current law (2025–2028) provides an **additional deduction for taxpayers 65 and older** — income phase-outs apply, and its scheduled expiration is precisely the kind of moving part your annual professional session tracks. Plan with it; don't build on it.

### The first-year moves that matter

**Withholding, deliberately.** The classic first-year error is under-withholding across several streams (each payer withholding as if it were your only income) and meeting a penalty-flavored April. The fix is one afternoon: project the year with your professional, set the pension election to carry the right load, and adjust once mid-year against reality. Boring Aprils are manufactured in November.

**The Roth-conversion conversation — education, not homework.** Here's the strategic shape of many FRS retirements: a **valley** may open between your last paycheck and the arrival of Social Security and (eventually) required minimum distributions — years where taxable income dips into lower brackets. Converting some traditional-IRA dollars to Roth *inside the valley* — paying today's lower rate to buy tax-free, RMD-free dollars for later — is a legitimate, widely used planning idea. It is also a transaction with tentacles: conversions raise that year's MAGI, which can touch marketplace credits (Chapter 8) and, through the two-year lookback, **IRMAA at 65+** (Chapter 9) — a conversion at 64 sets a premium at 66. Which is the point of raising it here: the valley is real, the idea is worth a professional conversation *early* (the valley has a closing date), and the interactions are exactly why this book keeps buying you the meeting instead of the conclusion.

**The widow's-penalty preview, one sentence:** the survivor of a couple soon files **single** — same-ish income, narrower brackets, higher IRMAA exposure — which is one more quiet argument, filed for Book Three, that tax planning is a two-lifetime project.

#### Here's what this means in real life

*Hypothetical.* The Robertses' first full year, mapped in November: pension $28,100, scheduled draw $17,400, Social Security deliberately not yet claimed (Chapter 7's plan), no DROP echo (her exit math finished last year). Projection: comfortably inside a modest bracket — and sitting in exactly the valley their professional had circled. The session's output: pension withholding set to carry the projected load; a **measured partial Roth conversion** sized to fill, not breach, their current bracket — with the IRMAA lookback consciously cleared (she's 62; the conversion year won't be in the window that prices her first premiums, a sentence that required the professional to say). April's result: a refund small enough to be a rounding error, three 1099-Rs filed without drama, and a growing Roth bucket whose entire job is Book Three flexibility. "A system with receipts," Robert repeated, approvingly. The filing cabinet had become furniture.

> ❝ *Boring Aprils are manufactured in November.*

#### What can go wrong

- Default withholding on every stream, and an April with penalties attached.
- Discovering the streams interact — withdrawals driving Social Security taxation, conversions driving IRMAA — after the transactions.
- Doing conversions by internet enthusiasm instead of bracket math with dates.
- Building permanent plans on a temporary deduction.
- Treating tax planning as a solo-lifetime project. The single-filer future is on the map or the map is incomplete.

#### What to check before deciding

Book the November session (or the month you retire): full-year projection, withholding elections set on purpose, the valley identified if you have one, and any conversion idea priced with its marketplace and IRMAA tentacles visible. Put every expected 1099-R on one page with its withholding status before the first one mails.

#### Questions to ask before deciding

1. What's my projected first-year picture, stream by stream — and who ran it?
2. Is my withholding load set deliberately, and where does it get adjusted mid-year?
3. Do I have a valley — and what are its opening and closing dates?
4. What would a conversion touch besides my bracket — credits, IRMAA, the survivor's future filing status?
5. What on my map is temporary law — and marked as such?

> **Ask your team:** a qualified tax professional for the projection, elections, and any conversion math; your adviser for how the map feeds the switchboard; the Division of Retirement/providers for withholding forms.

Switchboard live: the scheduled draw fired on the 1st, the buffer stands at target, and the monthly fifteen-minute ritual has its first two entries.

**Pass two — the paperwork completes (days 1–60).** The **HIS application** filed with its coverage certification, and the first subsidy payment confirmed when it begins. Health coverage transitions verified in hand — cards received, premiums drafting from the right account, any marketplace or continuation start dates matched to the map (Chapter 8). Any Medicare enrollments (for the 65-plus crowd) confirmed active, Medigap/drug plans in force (Chapter 9). Beneficiary confirmations from the sweep filed in the folder — including the brand-new rollover accounts.

**Pass three — the withholding checkup (days 45–90).** Two deposits in, do the Chapter 10 look: is the pension withholding what the November projection chose? Did the draw's tax treatment match the map? One small correction now — a revised election form, a fifteen-minute call — is the difference between a boring April and an annotated one.

**Pass four — the loose ends (by day 90).** Final employer items reconciled: last paycheck, leave payout (and its tax treatment against Chapter 10's plan), insurance continuations, return of equipment and badges — the small stuff that generates month-six phone calls when skipped. MyFRS and SSA online accounts confirmed working *as a retiree*. The household map updated to retirement configuration and re-shared with your spouse. And the folder — the whole corridor's paper trail — archived where the map says, because Book Three's annual reviews will visit it.

### The other ninety days

Now the part no form covers. The first three months of retirement are also the first three months of a new *life* — and this book would be lying by omission if it pretended the money was the whole story. Some honest expectations: the first Monday feels like vacation; somewhere around the third or fourth, many retirees — especially those whose work was identity-heavy, which describes most public service — hit an unexpected quiet. This is normal, it is nearly universal, and it is not a financial problem, which is why it can't be fixed with the switchboard. Book Three's opening chapters take it seriously (purpose, routine, and the design of days deserve the same planning energy the money got). For now, the ninety-day assignment is smaller: notice it, name it to your spouse, and resist the two reflexive escapes — unretiring in a panic, or spending your way past the quiet. You verified the money. The life gets the same patience.

One practical note in the same spirit: **reemployment** with FRS employers after retirement is governed by real rules with real penalties for missteps (waiting periods and limitations that vary by circumstance) — if an encore offer appears in your first year, verify the current rules with the Division of Retirement *before* accepting anything. Book Three covers the working-in-retirement question properly.

*Hypothetical.* Sandra's own ninety days, reconstructed. Day 12: gap month bridged by the buffer, zero drama. Day 31: first pension deposit — $47 different from her expectation; one call revealed a corrected insurance deduction, documented, resolved by day 45 (found in month one; the year-two version of that discrepancy costs an afternoon of archaeology). Day 38: HIS certified; first $210 arrived the following cycle. Day 55: withholding checkup caught the pension defaulting to a rate her November projection hadn't chosen — one form, fixed. Day 74: the quiet arrived on schedule; she named it to Gil over coffee instead of over a car purchase, and they started the Book Three conversation early. Day 90: folder archived, map updated, lunch scheduled with the next retiree. Her cursive card, proven: verify, then celebrate — and the celebrating is better when the verifying is done.

#### What can go wrong

- Assuming the first deposit is right because it arrived.
- The rollover that never landed — discovered at statement season instead of week three.
- HIS unfiled for months; subsidy simply unclaimed.
- Withholding on autopilot until April grades it.
- Treating the quiet as a crisis — or medicating it with the buffer.
- Accepting encore work before checking reemployment rules.

## BACK MATTER

### The whole book on two pages — chapter summaries

**1 · Where the Mistakes Happen.** The transition is a corridor of decisions sorted by one question: can it be undone? The permanent list — option election, DROP distribution, Medicare windows — gets the slow-down doctrine: forms early, both spouses, answers in writing, professional review, one night's sleep. "It's just forms" is the most expensive sentence in the building.

**2 · The Four Options.** Option 1: maximum check, your life only. Option 2: reduced, ten years guaranteed to any beneficiary. Option 3: reduced further, unchanged across both lifetimes. Option 4: adjusted, stepping to two-thirds at *either* death. DROP members elected at entry. Spousal acknowledgment proves awareness — this book requires arithmetic and agreement too. All four figures in writing before anything else.

**3 · Choosing as a Family.** Build the four-futures page: both alive, each survivor, the long widowhood. Social Security consolidates at the first death; expenses don't halve; the survivor files single. Define "livable," then elect the option that keeps every column above it. Pension-maximization insurance pitches get the full examination or no hearing.

**4 · Leaving DROP.** Within the strict post-termination window, the accumulation goes to cash (taxable in one stacked year, 20% withheld as a down payment), direct rollover (IRA, employer plan, or the FRS Investment Plan), or the combination most families need. The exit year is likely your lifetime tax peak — size any cash with professional bracket math first.

**5 · Rollovers Without Regret.** Direct rollovers always; the 60-day route is how accidents happen. Compare every destination on one sheet: all-in cost, surrender terms, age-based access (rolling a 457(b) to an IRA before 59½ reinstalls a penalty the law removed), services, and who profits. Nothing signed at, near, or because of a free dinner.

**6 · The Retirement Paycheck.** Three layers: the guaranteed floor (pension, eventually Social Security), the scheduled draw (automated, professionally sized, source-ordered for taxes), and the cash buffer (bridging the gap month now, defending against bad markets later). Boring, one-page, runnable by either spouse. Rehearse a practice month before the salary stops.

**7 · Social Security.** Three doors — 62 (about 30% smaller, forever), 67, 70 (about 24% larger, plus COLAs on the bigger base). A pension floor makes patience affordable; the higher earner's claiming age is really the survivor's income. Earnings test applies to early claimers still working. WEP/GPO are repealed — audit out the old fears and strategies.

**8 · Health Coverage Before 65.** The gap years are the budget's biggest new line. Four routes: retiree coverage (get *your* premium in writing), continuation (a bridge, not a residence), the marketplace (priced by your plannable income), a working spouse's plan. HIS pays $7.50 per service year to $225 — claim it promptly; size it honestly. Map the gap before setting the date.

**9 · Medicare at 65.** Seven-month initial window, or a special period protected only by *active* employment coverage — retiree and COBRA coverage generally don't count. Part B late penalty: ~10% per year late, permanent; Part D has its own. The Medigap guaranteed-issue window mostly swings one way. IRMAA surcharges start at $109k/$218k MAGI on a two-year lookback — and SSA-44 exists because retirement is a qualifying event. SHINE counselors are free; use them.

**10 · First-Year Taxes.** Every stream files paperwork: pension (set withholding on purpose), traditional withdrawals (ordinary income), Social Security (0–85% taxable by formula), Roth (tax-free, rules met). Florida adds nothing — a real, finite advantage. The valley between paychecks and RMDs may invite measured Roth conversions — with marketplace and IRMAA tentacles professionally checked. Boring Aprils are manufactured in November.

**11 · The Paperwork.** The folder: every form obtained early, read at kitchen-table speed, matched to its chapter. The transition beneficiary sweep — old time capsules and brand-new rollover accounts alike. The Before-You-Sign master checklist between every pen and every line. Leave-payout elections can deadline *before* the final paycheck.

**12 · The First 90 Days.** Four verification passes: money arrives, paperwork completes, withholding checks out at day 55, loose ends close by day 90. HIS certified, rollovers landed, discrepancies caught while they're phone calls instead of archaeology. And the life part — the quiet of month one — named, not medicated. Verify, then celebrate.

### Glossary

**Before-You-Sign checklist** — This book's standing protocol between any pen and any permanent line: early forms, both spouses, written answers, professional review, one night's sleep.
**Combination election** — Splitting a DROP distribution between a deliberately sized cash portion and a direct rollover.
**Corridor** — The transition's decision sequence, sorted by permanence.
**Direct rollover** — Trustee-to-trustee transfer; no withholding, no 60-day clock, the only rollover this book endorses for transition-sized money.
**Earnings test** — Temporary withholding of benefits for pre-FRA claimers with wages above the annual limit; recalculated at FRA.
**Four-futures page** — Household income modeled across both-alive, each-survivor, and long-survivorship columns.
**HIS** — Health Insurance Subsidy: $7.50/month per service year ($45–$225), application and coverage certification required.
**IEP / SEP** — Medicare's Initial Enrollment Period (seven months around 65) and Special Enrollment Period (protected only by active-employment coverage).
**IRMAA** — Income-based Medicare premium surcharges, starting at $109,000/$218,000 MAGI (2026), assessed on a two-year lookback; SSA-44 appeals cover life-changing events including retirement.
**Joint annuitant** — The person (spouse or qualifying dependent) whose lifetime Options 3 and 4 also cover.
**Medigap open enrollment** — The largely one-time, six-month guaranteed-issue window following Part B enrollment.
**Option election** — The permanent choice among Options 1–4 at retirement (or DROP entry) governing survivor income.
**Provisional/combined income** — The formula deciding what share (0–85%) of Social Security is taxable.
**SHINE** — Florida's free, unbiased Medicare counseling program.
**Spousal acknowledgment** — Required signature confirming a spouse knows Options 1 or 2 were elected; awareness, not agreement.
**SSA-44** — The life-changing-event form that recalculates IRMAA when income drops (retirement qualifies).
**Valley** — Lower-bracket years between the last paycheck and Social Security/RMDs; the classic window for measured Roth-conversion conversations.
**W-4P** — The withholding election governing pension payments.
